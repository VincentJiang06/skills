### Economic Development of Hong Kong: Retrospective and Outlook (2020-2029)
### Abstract

This paper analyzes the economic trajectory of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region over the past five years (2020-2024) and considers prospects for the next five years (2025-2029). Drawing on macroeconomic indicators, industrial restructuring, foreign trade and investment, labor markets, and financial markets, it finds that Hong Kong’s economy recovered during 2023-2025 after the combined shocks of the 2019 social unrest and the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020-2022. Although geopolitical tensions and rising trade protectionism continue to generate external uncertainty, Hong Kong has retained substantial economic resilience through the institutional framework of “One Country, Two Systems,” its intermediary role between Mainland China and global markets, and domestic growth drivers including the Northern Metropolis and innovation and technology industries. For 2025-2029, the economy is expected to record moderate annual growth of 2.5%-3.5%, with artificial intelligence, green finance, high-end professional services, and deeper integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area likely to remain central areas of development.

**Keywords:** Hong Kong economy; Greater Bay Area; Northern Metropolis; IPO market; talent policy

---

### Introduction

Hong Kong has long been studied as an international financial center, trade hub, and shipping center. The decade from 2020 to 2029 is a particularly important period because it combines an adverse opening phase with a broader effort to reposition the economy under China’s 14th Five-Year Plan.

The years 2020-2024 brought multiple shocks. These included the aftermath of the 2019 social unrest, the COVID-19 pandemic from 2020-2022, supply chain disruptions, and prolonged cross-border travel restrictions. According to World Bank data, Hong Kong’s GDP growth was -6.54% in 2020, rebounded to 6.45% in 2021, and fell again to -3.68% in 2022 following the fifth wave of the pandemic. These fluctuations tested both economic resilience and policy capacity.

The HKSAR government responded with measures intended to stabilize activity and support livelihoods. In February 2024, it announced the full removal of property market cooling measures, including Buyer’s Stamp Duty for non-permanent residents, New Residential Stamp Duty for second-time buyers, and Special Stamp Duty on short-term resale. The policy change released deferred housing demand. In 2025, Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing introduced IPO reforms that included adjustments to price discovery and lower public float requirements for large listings, altering Hong Kong’s competitive position within Asian equity markets.

The outlook for 2025-2029 is shaped by a difficult external environment and substantial domestic policy initiatives. Geopolitical tensions remain persistent, trade protectionism has increased, and changes in US-China relations continue to matter for a highly open economy such as Hong Kong. The ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) projects growth of 2.1% in 2025 and 1.9% in 2026. At the same time, the HKSAR government is advancing the Northern Metropolis, the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop, and a range of talent admission schemes, all of which are intended to support new sources of growth.

This paper reviews Hong Kong’s economic development over the past five years through systematic analysis of available data and assesses the next five years in light of current policy frameworks and macroeconomic trends.

### Retrospective of Economic Development (2020-2024)

#### 2.1 Macroeconomic Performance: From Recession to Recovery

Hong Kong’s economy passed through a pronounced reversal over the past five years. World Bank data show GDP growth of -1.67% in 2019, -6.54% in 2020, 6.45% in 2021, -3.68% in 2022, 3.22% in 2023, and 2.50% in 2024. The pattern reflects both exposure to external shocks and the capacity for recovery once restrictions eased.

In 2020, the COVID-19 outbreak caused a severe contraction in an economy closely tied to cross-border movement and trade. GDP fell by 6.54%, the second-worst performance since records began in 1962, exceeded only by the 1998 Asian Financial Crisis. Unemployment rose to 5.81%, the highest level in 15 years. Private consumption weakened sharply under social distancing rules and border controls, while visitor arrivals collapsed from 55.9 million in 2019 to about 3.57 million in 2020, a decline of more than 93%.

In 2021, GDP rebounded by 6.45%, supported by the low base in the previous year and the recovery of external demand. Goods exports increased by 26.3%, aided by strong demand for medical supplies, electronics, and home office equipment. Service exports also improved as local pandemic conditions became more manageable.

The recovery was interrupted in 2022 by the fifth wave of COVID-19. Strict social distancing and dynamic zero-COVID measures contributed to a 4.0% contraction in the first quarter, and GDP declined by 3.68% for the year as a whole. Private consumption expenditure fell by 1.8% and fixed capital investment by 7.8%. Unemployment increased to 4.32%.

Conditions changed materially in 2023. Cross-border travel with the Mainland resumed fully on February 6, and overseas entry restrictions were removed. GDP grew by 3.22%, supported by a 21.2% increase in service exports and a 7.4% rise in private consumption expenditure. Visitor arrivals recovered to 34 million, still below the pre-pandemic level but far above the 600,000 recorded in 2022.

In 2024, recovery continued at a slower but more stable pace. Full-year GDP growth reached 2.6%. External demand softened because of geopolitical tensions and tighter monetary conditions in major economies, but domestic demand improved. Private consumption expenditure rose by 1.2%, and fixed capital investment returned to growth at 1.8%. The unemployment rate edged down from 2.95% in 2023 to 2.94% in 2024, close to full employment.

In nominal terms, GDP rose from USD 363.1 billion in 2019 to USD 406.9 billion in 2024, while per capita GDP increased from USD 48,359 to USD 54,075. On both measures, the economy moved above pre-pandemic levels.

#### 2.2 Industrial Structure Evolution: Service Sector Dominance and I&T Breakthrough

Services continue to dominate Hong Kong’s economy, accounting for more than 93% of GDP. Over the past five years, the industrial structure has changed mainly through adjustments within the service sector and the expansion of innovation and technology activities.

Financial services remain a core sector, contributing about 22% of GDP. Hong Kong has retained its position as the world’s largest offshore renminbi business center. By the end of 2024, renminbi deposits in Hong Kong exceeded RMB 1 trillion, and average daily turnover in the renminbi Real Time Gross Settlement system was above RMB 1.8 trillion. Asset and wealth management also expanded. According to Securities and Futures Commission data, the sector reached HKD 35.5 trillion by the end of 2023, roughly 42% above the 2019 level.

The IPO market also shifted during this period. In 2024, Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing revised the Chapter 18C regime for specialist technology companies and launched the TECH service platform. In 2025, the IPO market strengthened markedly. Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. raised USD 5.3 billion in the largest global IPO since 2023, and total IPO fundraising reached USD 22 billion, returning Hong Kong to the leading position globally.

Tourism and retail were among the sectors most affected by the pandemic. In 2019, tourism contributed about 4.5% of GDP and directly employed more than 250,000 people. Retail sales fell from HKD 431.1 billion in 2019 to HKD 326.5 billion in 2020, a decline of 24.3%. After full cross-border reopening in 2023, tourism began to recover. Visitor arrivals rose to 45 million in 2024, about 75% of the 2019 level. In the first quarter of 2025, arrivals reached 85% of the pre-pandemic level, and retail sales value increased by 12.8% year on year.

Innovation and technology industries advanced more quickly in the second half of the period. The government identified I&T as a source of economic diversification and committed greater resources to it. On December 22, 2025, the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Hong Kong section of the Loop formally opened. More than 60 firms had already signed agreements to move in, with activities centered on artificial intelligence, biomedicine, and new materials. Development of the Northern Metropolis also accelerated in 2025, particularly the San Tin Technopole and the Hung Shui Kiu/Ha Tsuen New Development Area. The San Tin Technopole is expected to contribute at least HKD 250 billion annually to GDP once fully operational and to provide more than 300,000 jobs. In addition, the InnoHK research clusters expanded research capacity in health technology, artificial intelligence, and robotics, and the 2025-26 Budget allocated HKD 1 billion for the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute.

#### 2.3 Foreign Trade and Investment: Resilience and Transformation

As a free port and separate customs territory, Hong Kong depends heavily on external trade. Between 2020 and 2024, its trade structure showed increasing movement away from conventional re-export functions toward higher-value trade-related services.

Goods exports rose from USD 543.1 billion in 2019 to USD 631.1 billion in 2024, while imports increased from USD 558.4 billion to USD 633.1 billion. Over the same period, the trade deficit narrowed from USD 15.3 billion to USD 2 billion. At the same time, the traditional re-export model came under pressure as Mainland supply chains upgraded and direct export capacity improved. Hong Kong responded by relying more heavily on services linked to trade, including supply chain management, trade finance, and quality inspection.

Service trade remained Hong Kong’s most competitive area. Between 2019 and 2024, service exports continued to expand, especially in financial services, professional services, and logistics. Connectivity mechanisms such as Stock Connect, Bond Connect, and Cross-boundary Wealth Management Connect deepened cross-border financial activity and supported further growth in service exports.

Foreign direct investment also remained strong. According to World Bank data, net FDI inflows rose from USD 58.3 billion in 2019 to USD 125.8 billion in 2024, an increase of more than 116%. This suggests continuing investor confidence in Hong Kong’s role as an entry point to the Mainland market and as a jurisdiction with internationally familiar commercial institutions.

#### 2.4 Labor Market: From High Unemployment to Talent Competition

Labor market conditions deteriorated sharply in 2020 and then improved quickly after reopening. Unemployment rose from 2.92% in 2019 to 5.81% in 2020, with catering, retail, and tourism particularly affected. As economic activity resumed, unemployment fell to 2.95% in 2023 and 2.99% in 2024, close to the estimated natural rate. Underemployment declined from 3.4% in 2020 to 1.1% in 2024.

Wage growth, however, remained moderate. From 2020 to 2024, the nominal wage index increased at an average annual rate of about 2.5%, only slightly above average inflation of 2.1%. Median monthly household income rose from HKD 29,500 in 2019 to HKD 30,500 in 2024, implying limited gains in real purchasing power.

At the same time, population ageing, labor outflows, and industrial restructuring pushed talent policy higher on the government agenda. Between 2022 and 2023, the HKSAR government introduced or expanded several talent admission schemes. The Top Talent Pass Scheme, launched at the end of 2022, targeted high-income individuals earning more than HKD 2.5 million annually and graduates from the world’s top 100 universities. By the end of 2025, it had received more than 70,000 applications and approved about 55,000, for an approval rate of 88.2%.

The Quality Migrant Admission Scheme removed quota restrictions and simplified approval procedures. In 2025, it received 18,707 applications and approved 7,101, while the renewal approval rate reached 97.3%. The Admission Scheme for Mainland Talents and Professionals relaxed sectoral restrictions and accelerated approvals; 26,714 applications were approved in 2025, with a renewal rate above 93%. The Talent List was updated in March 2025 to cover 60 professions, with nine new additions including accountants, patent professionals, and aircraft maintenance engineers.

According to government figures, these schemes attracted more than 230,000 people to Hong Kong between the end of 2022 and the end of 2025. The main effect was to reduce shortages in selected professional occupations rather than to eliminate overall labor constraints.

#### 2.5 Property Market: From Bubble Adjustment to Steady Recovery

The property market underwent a substantial change during this period, moving from stringent cooling measures to full removal of those measures while also passing through a prolonged price correction.

Data from the Rating and Valuation Department show that the private domestic price index peaked in August 2019 and then declined for four years. By the end of 2024, prices had fallen by about 25% from the peak, the longest and deepest correction since 1998. In 2024 alone, prices fell by 7.1% year on year, or 8.4% in real terms.

Several factors contributed to the adjustment. Federal Reserve rate increases were transmitted into Hong Kong’s interest rate environment and raised mortgage costs. The pandemic weakened local demand. Population outflows reduced rental demand. Supply also increased: 24,261 private residential units were completed in 2024, up 75.1% year on year.

A major policy change came on February 28, 2024, when the Financial Secretary, Paul Chan, announced the full removal of Buyer’s Stamp Duty, New Residential Stamp Duty, and Special Stamp Duty. Market activity picked up quickly after the policy change. In the first ten months of 2025, residential transactions reached 51,361, up 20.3% year on year and the highest level in four years. Prices reached a low point by mid-2025 and increased by about 3% over the year. Mainland buyers’ share of purchases rose from about 18% in 2024 to 22-24% in 2025 and exceeded 60% in the luxury segment.

Further adjustments followed in 2025. The government lowered the residential property threshold under the New Capital Investment Entrant Scheme from HKD 50 million to HKD 30 million and increased the maximum qualifying investment from HKD 10 million to HKD 15 million.

Even after the correction, housing remained expensive by international standards. According to the Demographia International Housing Affordability Survey, Hong Kong’s median house price in 2024 was 14.4 times median household income, making it the least affordable city in the world for the fourteenth consecutive year. This was nevertheless an improvement from 18.8 times in 2022.

#### 2.6 Financial Markets and IPO Ecosystem Reshaping

Financial markets moved through a difficult cycle between 2020 and 2024. The Hang Seng Index fell below 15,000 in 2022, its lowest level in 13 years. Even so, regulatory and institutional reforms continued, particularly in the listing regime.

The 2018 reforms that allowed companies with weighted voting rights and pre-revenue biotechnology firms to list had already widened market access. Chapter 18C, introduced in 2023, further lowered barriers for specialist technology companies. The 2025 reforms focused on price discovery and market structure. At least 40% of IPO shares were required to be allocated to the institutional bookbuilding tranche so that price formation would be led by professional investors. Public subscription tranches could adopt either Clawback Mechanism A, with an initial 5% allocation and a maximum clawback of 35%, or Clawback Mechanism B, with a fixed allocation between 10% and 60%. Cornerstone investor participation was capped at 50%-55% to reduce concentration, and new free-float requirements were introduced to improve post-listing liquidity.

The results became visible in 2025. IPO fundraising reached USD 22 billion, more than 200% above the previous year and enough to place Hong Kong first globally. Major deals included CATL at USD 5.3 billion, Hengrui Pharmaceuticals at about USD 2 billion, Haitian Flavouring and Food at about USD 1.5 billion, and Sanhua Intelligent Controls at about USD 1.2 billion. These four offerings accounted for four of the ten largest IPOs worldwide in 2025.

Market access schemes also expanded. In 2024, the Shanghai-Hong Kong and Shenzhen-Hong Kong Stock Connect arrangements marked their tenth anniversary. In 2025, southbound trading was widened so that eligible international companies listed in Hong Kong could be included. This change strengthened Hong Kong’s position in attracting international issuers.

### Analysis of Current Economic Situation (2025)

#### 3.1 Macroeconomic Situation: Steady Expansion

Hong Kong’s economy continued to expand in 2025. Census and Statistics Department data show real GDP growth of 3.1% year on year in both the first and second quarters, extending the sequence of positive growth to ten consecutive quarters. The full-year growth forecast remained at 2%-3%.

External demand was an important source of support. Goods exports grew by 8.4% in real terms in the first quarter of 2025, helped by demand for AI-related electronic products and by front-loading of trade activity. The outlook for the second half of the year was weaker because of US protectionist measures.

Domestic demand was less uniform. Private consumption expenditure declined slightly, by 0.5% in the first quarter. This reflected changes in consumption behavior, including the growing tendency of Hong Kong residents to travel north for consumption. At the same time, nominal employment income rose by 4.2% year on year in the first quarter, and government stimulus measures such as the “Happy Hong Kong” events were intended to support spending. Consumer confidence improved gradually rather than abruptly.

Investment expenditure also turned positive. Overall investment rose by 4.1% in real terms in the first quarter, driven mainly by machinery, equipment, and intellectual property products. Infrastructure projects linked to the Northern Metropolis and San Tin Technopole continued to support capital formation.

#### 3.2 Inflation and Monetary Policy Environment

Inflation remained moderate in 2025. The underlying Composite Consumer Price Index, which excludes one-off government relief measures, increased by 1.2% year on year in the first quarter and 1.1% in the second quarter. The official forecast for the full year remained 1.5% for underlying inflation and 1.8% for headline inflation.

Hong Kong’s monetary conditions continued to be determined by the linked exchange rate system, under which local interest rates broadly follow US monetary policy. As the Federal Reserve began to reduce rates in 2025, Hong Kong’s prime rate also declined. Lower financing costs provided some support to households, firms, and asset markets, especially property and equities.

#### 3.3 Labor Market: Full Employment and Structural Shortages

Labor market conditions remained tight in 2025, with unemployment close to 3%. Aggregate labor demand was therefore strong, but shortages became more visible in particular sectors, especially innovation and technology, healthcare, aviation, construction, and activities related to new industries and infrastructure projects.

The government continued to revise talent policy. In March 2025, the Talent List was expanded to 60 professions. In June 2025, a Technical Professional Stream was introduced to allow non-degree technical workers to enter Hong Kong in eight sectors, including new industrialization, nursing, and aircraft maintenance. In December 2025, the Technology Talent Admission Scheme was further relaxed by simplifying procedures and removing the requirement that applicants engage in research and development within 14 designated technology areas.

#### 3.4 Financial Market Prosperity and Challenges

Financial markets performed strongly in 2025. The Hang Seng Index rose with support from the IPO recovery and more favorable policy sentiment from the Mainland. The IPO market raised USD 22 billion over the year, returning Hong Kong to first place globally.

This performance did not remove underlying constraints. Geopolitical tensions continued to affect valuations of Chinese firms listed in Hong Kong. Market turnover remained below earlier peaks even as new listings increased. Competition for financial talent also intensified, particularly from Singapore, which continued to introduce policies aimed at the same labor pool.

### Economic Outlook for the Next Five Years (2025-2029)

#### 4.1 Growth Forecasts and Scenario Analysis

For 2025-2029, Hong Kong’s economy is likely to be shaped by external uncertainty, domestic infrastructure investment, and structural changes in finance and technology. A reasonable central estimate places average annual real GDP growth between 2.5% and 3.5%.

Under a baseline scenario, assigned a probability of 60%, the global economy expands moderately, US-China relations remain competitive without full decoupling, and Mainland China grows by 4%-5% annually. In this setting, Hong Kong’s growth would likely average 2.8%-3.2% a year. The main supports would be peak-period investment in the Northern Metropolis, with infrastructure spending rising by 5%-8% annually; increasing industrialization of innovation and technology, lifting the sector’s GDP contribution from about 1.5% to 3%; annual growth of about 6% in asset and wealth management; and a full return of tourism to the 2019 level of 55.9 million arrivals, followed by further growth.

An optimistic scenario, with probability 25%, assumes improvement in US-China relations and faster integration between Hong Kong and the Mainland, especially within the Greater Bay Area. In that case, annual growth could reach 3.5%-4.5%. This would require wider liberalization of cross-border flows of capital, people, and data; stronger use of Hong Kong as a headquarters base for Mainland firms expanding abroad; and larger capital inflows linked to demand for relatively safe financial jurisdictions.

A pessimistic scenario, with probability 15%, assumes global recession and escalating geopolitical conflict. Growth would slow to 1%-2% annually, with negative growth possible in some years. The main risks would be substantial increases in US tariffs on China, tighter global financial conditions, and a broader decoupling process that reduces Hong Kong’s value as an intermediary economy.

#### 4.2 Structural Growth Drivers

**4.2.1 Northern Metropolis and I&T Industries**

The Northern Metropolis is likely to be the most important medium-term source of growth. Covering about 300 square kilometers, or roughly one-third of Hong Kong’s land area, it is intended to reshape both the urban structure and the geography of production.

Several milestones are scheduled. In 2026, the Hung Shui Kiu Industrial Estate is expected to commence operation and construction is due to begin on Hung Shui Kiu Station on the Tuen Ma Line. In 2027, all major development projects are expected to begin land resumption procedures. In 2028, the first batch of public housing residents in the Northern Metropolis is expected to move in. By 2032, 40% of new development land formation and 40% of the additional housing units are scheduled for completion.

The economic contribution could be substantial. The San Tin Technopole alone is expected to add at least HKD 250 billion annually to GDP when fully operational. Combined with the Lok Ma Chau Loop, the Hung Shui Kiu logistics hub, and related projects, the Northern Metropolis could account for 15%-20% of Hong Kong’s GDP by around 2030 and generate 500,000 jobs.

Within this framework, several innovation and technology sectors are expected to expand. Artificial intelligence is supported by the HKD 1 billion Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute and related industry-university collaboration. Life and health technology may benefit from the Chapter 18A listing regime and the research capacity of HKU, CUHK, and HKUST. New energy technology aligns with national carbon reduction objectives and may support both green finance and green technology development. Advanced manufacturing is expected to develop through programs such as “Manufacturing+,” which support the upgrading of local production lines.

**4.2.2 Financial Services Upgrading**

Financial services are likely to shift from a narrower fundraising role toward broader functions in asset allocation and risk management. Green finance is one area of expected growth. Hong Kong is already Asia’s largest green bond issuance center, and green and sustainable bond issuance is projected to grow by 20% annually, exceeding USD 500 billion by 2029.

Virtual assets constitute another emerging segment, provided growth occurs under a cautious regulatory framework that includes stablecoin issuance and virtual asset exchange-traded funds. Offshore renminbi business is also expected to deepen as renminbi internationalization progresses. Deposits could rise from about RMB 1 trillion to RMB 2 trillion by 2029, while the renminbi share of trade settlement could reach 40%. Insurance and specialty risk management, including catastrophe bonds and cyber insurance, may also expand under the institutional flexibility afforded by “One Country, Two Systems.”

**4.2.3 High-End Professional Services and Trade**

Hong Kong is also positioned to retain a regional role in legal, arbitration, shipping, aviation, and trade-related professional services. Support from the 14th Five-Year Plan may strengthen international commercial arbitration and mediation, particularly for firms involved in Belt and Road projects. Trade-related services are likely to move further toward supply chain management, quality inspection, branding, and higher-value logistics rather than conventional entrepôt activity. In aviation, the full operation of the Three-Runway System is expected to raise Hong Kong International Airport’s annual capacity to 120 million passengers and 10 million tonnes of cargo, reinforcing its position as a major cargo airport.

**4.2.4 Talent and Population Strategy**

Population ageing remains a long-term structural constraint. The share of residents aged 65 or above is expected to rise from about 20% in 2024 to about 25% by 2029. Over the next five years, talent policy is likely to focus on more targeted recruitment, local training, regional qualification recognition, and retention.

Recruitment efforts are expected to continue concentrating on innovation and technology, healthcare, education, and finance. Local training will require stronger STEM education and closer cooperation between educational institutions and firms located in the Northern Metropolis. Regional collaboration may expand mutual recognition of professional qualifications within the Greater Bay Area, allowing Hong Kong and Macao professionals to practice in Mainland GBA cities more easily. Retention policy is likely to focus on renewal rules, housing, education, and healthcare support, with the aim of keeping talent renewal rates above 85%.

#### 4.3 Risks and Challenges

**4.3.1 External Risks**

Geopolitics and trade protectionism remain the principal external risks. A significant increase in US tariffs on China would affect Hong Kong’s role as a trade intermediary. One response would be broader market diversification toward ASEAN, the Middle East, Central Asia, and other Global South markets.

Global financial conditions are another source of uncertainty. If US inflation were to rise again and trigger renewed monetary tightening, Hong Kong’s property and equity markets could face renewed adjustment pressure through higher interest rates. Slower growth in the Mainland would also affect service exports, capital inflows, and overall sentiment. This would increase the importance of strengthening Hong Kong’s own competitive advantages rather than relying too heavily on Mainland growth cycles.

**4.3.2 Internal Challenges**

Domestic adjustment costs are also likely to remain significant. Traditional retail and catering continue to face pressure from changing consumption patterns, including increased northbound consumption and online shopping. High living costs weaken the city’s attractiveness to younger workers and to some incoming professionals, even after the recent property price adjustment. Social integration is a further issue. The inflow of talent through admission schemes takes time to absorb, and policymakers will need to balance recruitment with concerns about local employment and public service capacity.

**4.3.3 Structural Risks**

Structural vulnerabilities persist. If interest rate differentials between the United States and China remain wide for a prolonged period, the linked exchange rate system may come under speculative pressure. Hong Kong’s foreign exchange reserves of more than USD 400 billion provide substantial protection, but extreme market conditions would still require careful management. Economic concentration is another concern. Financial services still account for more than 20% of GDP, which leaves the economy exposed to changes in global financial market conditions despite current diversification efforts.

#### 4.4 Policy Recommendations

Regional integration should remain a priority. Coordination between the Northern Metropolis and Shenzhen’s Qianhai under the “Twin Cities, Three Circles” approach could support infrastructure links, industrial coordination, and easier movement of production factors. The Lok Ma Chau Loop would benefit from further pilot policies, particularly for cross-border data transfer and streamlined mobility for research personnel. A more functional “one-hour living circle” in the Greater Bay Area could also improve the practical conditions for cross-border residence and work.

Industrial policy should focus on sectors with plausible scale and institutional support. Artificial intelligence is an obvious candidate and would require both increased research funding and wider access to real application settings. The low-altitude economy, including drone delivery and air-taxi services, will require clearer regulation before large-scale expansion is possible. Traditional professional services, such as law, accounting, and construction, would also benefit from digital transformation to raise productivity.

Talent policy should place greater weight on retention and adaptation. More flexible renewal rules under the Top Talent Pass Scheme could better accommodate entrepreneurship and self-employment. Housing support, including rent-to-buy arrangements, may improve retention. Expanded international school capacity would address a common concern among incoming professionals with families. Stronger vocational retraining would also help local workers respond to structural change.

Financial policy should continue to strengthen market infrastructure. Listing rules need to balance institutional efficiency with retail participation while limiting excessive volatility. A deeper renminbi derivatives market would improve exchange-rate risk management for firms. Cross-border regulatory cooperation should also be reinforced in order to reduce systemic risk.

Livelihood policy remains relevant to long-term economic performance. Faster public housing delivery would reduce waiting times and relieve social pressure. Reform of the Mandatory Provident Fund could strengthen retirement protection. Transfer measures such as the Working Family Allowance could be better targeted at lower-income households.

---

### Conclusion

Hong Kong’s economy experienced a severe contraction in the early part of the period under review but recovered steadily thereafter. GDP fell by 6.54% in 2020 during the pandemic and returned to growth of 2.6% by 2024. By that point, both total GDP and per capita GDP had moved above pre-pandemic levels. The recovery reflected policy intervention, including the removal of property cooling measures and the expansion of talent schemes, as well as the underlying strengths of Hong Kong’s financial and trade institutions.

For 2025-2029, the outlook is moderately positive rather than assured. External conditions remain uncertain, but domestic growth drivers are identifiable. The Northern Metropolis is likely to alter the spatial and industrial organization of the economy. Innovation and technology sectors are moving from policy priority to commercial activity. Financial services are broadening into areas such as green finance and virtual assets. Talent policies are increasing labor supply in selected professions.

A growth range of 2.5%-3.5% per year appears plausible for 2025-2029. At the same time, this trajectory depends on Hong Kong’s ability to manage geopolitical risk, the costs of industrial restructuring, and the pressures associated with population ageing. Further reform, openness, and attention to distributional issues will remain necessary.

Hong Kong’s economic role continues to rest on its position between Mainland China and global markets. Under “One Country, Two Systems,” it remains both a channel for China’s external economic engagement and a gateway for international capital entering the Mainland market. Whether the city can sustain this role over the next five years will depend on how effectively it combines integration with the national development strategy and the preservation of institutions that remain legible and credible to global markets.
