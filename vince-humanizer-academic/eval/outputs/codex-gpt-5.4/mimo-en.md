# Hong Kong's Economic Development: Retrospect and Prospect (2021-2030)

## Abstract

This paper reviews the economic development of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR) from 2021 to 2025 and examines its main growth drivers, structural constraints, and policy responses. After the dual shocks of the social incidents and the global pandemic, Hong Kong's economy began to recover in 2021 and moved onto a more stable growth path after 2023. With support from national policy and local adjustment, Hong Kong consolidated its position as an international financial, trade, and shipping center, while making initial progress in emerging sectors such as innovation and technology. The paper also considers the likely development prospects, growth paths, major risks, and strategic directions for 2026 to 2030 in light of existing plans and broader macroeconomic trends. It argues that, over the next five years, Hong Kong's economy will continue to adjust through deeper integration into national development, industrial upgrading, and the maintenance of its distinctive external role. Long-term prosperity and stability will depend on whether this transition can be managed effectively and whether internal and external conditions remain supportive.

**Keywords**: Hong Kong Economy; Economic Development; 14th Five-Year Plan; Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area; International Financial Center; Innovation and Technology

## Part I: Retrospective on Economic Development (2021-2025)

### 1.1 Overall Economic Performance and Cyclical Fluctuations

From 2021 to 2025, Hong Kong's economy followed a V-shaped path marked by deep recession, uneven recovery, and gradual stabilization. This pattern reflects the interaction of external shocks, domestic disruptions, and policy adjustment.

- **2021: Recovery Initiation and Uneven Growth**. After the sharp contraction of 2020 caused by the combined effects of the social incidents and the global pandemic, Hong Kong's economy rebounded in 2021, with real GDP growing by 6.4%. Growth was driven mainly by strong global demand for goods in the first half of the year, which lifted exports. The recovery, however, was uneven. Sectors linked to external trade recovered relatively quickly, while sectors dependent on the movement of people, including tourism, retail, and catering, remained under heavy pressure. Total inbound visitors for the year were only about 90,000, compared with nearly 56 million in 2019 before the pandemic, which continued to weigh on related industries.
    
- **2022: Growth Moderation Amid Multiple Challenges**. In 2022, Hong Kong faced a more difficult environment. The fifth wave of the pandemic at the start of the year disrupted local consumption and business activity. At the same time, major central banks entered a rapid interest rate tightening cycle to contain inflation, weakening external demand and increasing financial market volatility. Rising geopolitical tensions, especially uncertainty in China-U.S. relations, added pressure to Hong Kong's role as an international business center. Under these conditions, annual economic growth slowed to 3.2%. Activity recovered to some extent in the second half of the year as anti-pandemic measures were relaxed, but inbound tourism remained low for the year as a whole, limiting the pace of recovery.
    
- **2023-2025: Moving Towards Solid Recovery and Structural Fine-tuning**. The year 2023 marked Hong Kong's broad return to normal economic conditions. The full resumption of travel with the Mainland and with overseas markets at the start of the year became the main driver of growth. Inbound visitor arrivals rose sharply to about 34 million. Although this remained below the pre-pandemic level, it supported tourism, hotels, retail, and catering. Financial markets continued to face headwinds from high global interest rates and weak capital market fundraising, but the banking sector remained broadly stable, and areas such as Renminbi (RMB) business and wealth management showed resilience. In 2024 and 2025, the economy is expected to maintain moderate growth, with a broader set of growth drivers. Government measures to attract enterprises and talent have begun to take effect and have partly offset fluctuations in external demand. Growth during this period is projected to remain in the 2.5% to 3.5% range. At the same time, the economic structure shows clearer movement toward innovation and technology, green finance, and other newer activities while retaining traditional strengths.

### 1.2 Key Driving Factors and Evolution of Pillar Industries

Over the past five years, Hong Kong's economy has been shaped by pressure on traditional pillar industries to adjust and by policy support for emerging sectors.

- **Financial Services: Stability with Change, Connecting Inside and Outside**. Over the past five years, Hong Kong's financial sector has faced sharp changes in global interest rates, higher geopolitical risk, and capital market volatility. Its central role, however, has remained intact.
    
    - **Stock Market**: Initial Public Offering (IPO) activity has fluctuated with global market conditions. At the same time, Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing Limited's (HKEX) Stock Connect mechanisms have continued to expand and adjust, including the further development of 沪深港通, the launch of Swap Connect, and consideration of RMB counters. These measures have reinforced Hong Kong's role in linking the Mainland of China with international capital markets.
    - **Asset and Wealth Management**: Hong Kong continues to benefit from the growth of Mainland household wealth and from global demand for asset allocation. Family office business and Wealth Management Connect have become key areas of development. Although competition from places such as Singapore has intensified, Hong Kong retains advantages in market scale, its legal system, and its institutional connection with the Mainland.
    - **Fintech and Green Finance**: The Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) has continued to promote fintech development, and virtual banks and digital payments have become more common. Green and sustainable finance has expanded quickly. Hong Kong has become one of Asia's major centers for green bond issuance, and related products continue to broaden.
    - **Offshore RMB Business**: Hong Kong's position as the world's largest offshore RMB business hub has been further consolidated, with continued growth in RMB deposits, loans, foreign exchange transactions, and clearing services.
- **Trade and Logistics: Challenges and Opportunities amid Supply Chain Restructuring**. Hong Kong's position as a global trade center has come under pressure from the regionalization of supply chains, rising protectionism, and geopolitical risk.
    
    - **Goods Trade**: Total goods trade has moved with the global cycle. Hong Kong retains advantages in the re-export of high-end consumer goods and electronic products, but it also faces pressure from direct competition with Mainland ports and from broader supply chain adjustment.
    - **Trade-Related Professional Services**: Professional services linked to trade, including arbitration, testing and certification, and supply chain management, have remained competitive.
    - **Shipping and Air Logistics**: Hong Kong International Airport has long ranked among the world's leading cargo airports. The completion of the Three-Runway System in 2024 will raise capacity. The recovery of passenger aviation, however, still depends on the extent of tourism recovery and on regional competition.
- **Tourism and Retail: A Long Thaw from Deep Freeze**. Tourism and retail were the sectors most directly affected by the pandemic.
    
    - **Changes in Visitor Composition**: After reopening, Mainland visitors remained the clear majority, but spending patterns became more cautious, with greater interest in in-depth travel and cultural experiences. The older model built mainly around shopping has become less dominant. Short-haul markets in Southeast Asia have become a more important source of growth.
    - **Retail Format Transformation**: Retail sales recovered markedly in 2023, but the pattern was uneven. Traditional strong categories such as luxury goods and cosmetics rebounded faster, while mass consumer goods grew more moderately. The sector has accelerated online-merge-offline (OMO) integration and expanded cross-border e-commerce in response to changing consumption habits.
- **Innovation and Technology: An Emerging Growth Pole Driven by Policy**. The current government has placed innovation and technology (I&T) high on the policy agenda by establishing the Office for Attracting Strategic Enterprises (OASES), launching the Research, Academic and Industry Sectors One-plus Scheme (RAISe+), and promoting the Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Science and Technology Innovation Co-operation Zone.
    
    - **Life and Health Technology**: This has become a priority area, attracting several leading biopharmaceutical and medical device firms to establish or expand research and development operations in Hong Kong.
    - **Artificial Intelligence and Data Science**: Research and applications have progressed gradually, but industrialization remains limited in scale and speed.
    - **Start-up Ecosystem**: The number of start-ups in Hong Kong has continued to increase. Venture capital activity has been affected by tighter global financing conditions, but government-backed funding has directed more attention toward deep tech. The sector still faces constraints, including limited local market size, intense competition for top talent, and incomplete channels for converting research outcomes into commercial activity.

### 1.3 Major Challenges and Structural Contradictions

The experience of the past five years has again brought out several structural weaknesses in Hong Kong's economy.

- **High Uncertainty of the External Environment**: Weak global growth, divergent monetary policies among major economies, continuing geopolitical conflict such as the Russia-Ukraine war, and prolonged China-U.S. strategic competition affect Hong Kong, as a highly open small economy, through trade, finance, and confidence.
    
- **Risks of Economic Structural Simplification**: Despite efforts to diversify the economy, finance, trade logistics, professional services, and tourism and retail still account for most of GDP. The economy remains heavily dependent on a limited number of sectors and on external conditions. When these sectors face cyclical or structural stress, overall resilience is limited.
    
- **Tension between Social Livelihood and Economic Development**: Deep-rooted social issues, including housing pressure, the wealth gap, and limited upward mobility for young people, have not been resolved by economic recovery alone. High business and living costs affect Hong Kong's long-term attractiveness to firms and talent. A more even distribution of the gains from growth remains important for long-term social stability and sustained development.
    
- **Intensified Regional Competition**: Singapore competes directly with Hong Kong for international headquarters, family offices, and skilled professionals. Mainland cities such as Shanghai and Shenzhen have also advanced rapidly in finance and technology, narrowing parts of Hong Kong's traditional lead. Hong Kong therefore needs to define new areas of comparative advantage while preserving its established functions.
    

### 1.4 Major Policies and Strategic Responses

In response to these challenges, the HKSAR Government, with support from the Central Government, has introduced a series of major policies and strategic initiatives.

- **Fully Aligning with and Integrating into National Development**. This has been the central line of Hong Kong's economic policy over the past five years.
    
    - **Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA) Development**: Hong Kong has taken a more active role in the Greater Bay Area, making progress in financial connectivity, the construction of an I&T corridor, and the alignment of rules and mechanisms. Cooperation platforms such as Qianhai, Nanshan, and Hetao have provided Hong Kong businesses and professionals with broader access to the Mainland market.
    - **Support from the 14th Five-Year Plan**: The National 14th Five-Year Plan explicitly supports Hong Kong in consolidating and strengthening its position as an international financial, shipping, and trade center; enhancing its role as a global offshore RMB business hub, an international asset management center, and a risk management center; and developing into an international I&T center and a regional intellectual property trading center. This provides Hong Kong with a defined strategic role within national development.
- **Actively Attracting Enterprises and Talent**: To address the outflow of talent and the relocation of firms, the government has launched measures including the Top Talent Pass Scheme, the new Capital Investment Entrant Scheme, and a range of sector-specific funds and subsidies. The effects have begun to appear.
    
- **Long-term Planning and Infrastructure Investment**: The Northern Metropolis Development Strategy is being advanced as a new source of future growth, with the aim of deepening integration with Shenzhen and other Greater Bay Area Mainland cities in innovation and technology and other emerging sectors. At the same time, large infrastructure projects such as the Three-Runway System and Lantau Tomorrow Vision are intended to expand economic capacity and support long-term competitiveness.
    
- **Maintaining the Linked Exchange Rate System and Financial Stability**: In the face of volatile global financial markets and pressure from capital outflows, the Hong Kong Monetary Authority has maintained the Linked Exchange Rate System. Strong capital and liquidity positions in the banking system have demonstrated the resilience of Hong Kong's financial system and helped preserve investor confidence.

## Part II: Future Economic Development Outlook (2026-2030)

### 2.1 Growth Drivers and Potential Pathways

For 2026 to 2030, Hong Kong's economy is likely to operate in an environment that remains complex but still offers room for growth. The main drivers are expected to become more diversified and somewhat more internally generated.

- **Financial Deepening and Functional Upgrading**: Finance will remain the core sector, but growth is likely to depend less on expansion in scale and more on deeper market functions and product development.
    
    - **Core Hub for RMB Internationalization**: As the RMB's share in international trade and reserves rises gradually, Hong Kong's role in offshore RMB pricing, clearing, and product development is likely to become more important. More RMB-denominated financial products, including stocks, commodities, and derivatives, are likely to emerge.
    - **Green and Sustainable Finance Center**: Global energy transition and the continued growth of ESG investment are likely to expand Hong Kong's green finance market further, with the potential to strengthen its role in regional and global green capital allocation.
    - **High Ground for Asset and Wealth Management**: Hong Kong's asset management industry is expected to return to growth and reach a new level by 2030. Family offices, cross-border pension investment, and compliant virtual asset management are likely to be major growth areas.
    - **Risk Management Center**: Greater geopolitical and economic volatility is likely to increase demand for hedging and risk management, creating room for further development in insurance, reinsurance, and derivatives markets.
- **Industrialization Breakthroughs in I&T**: After five years of policy support and capacity building, the next five years may bring more visible industrialization in selected areas.
    
    - **Life and Health Technology Cluster**: Drawing on the research capacity of leading universities and the presence of major firms, Hong Kong has the potential to build an internationally relevant cluster in biotechnology research and development, clinical trials, and high-end medical devices.
    - **AI and Smart City Applications**: Artificial intelligence is likely to be applied more widely in fintech, smart city management, and professional services such as legal technology and construction technology, with possible gains in efficiency and service quality.
    - **New Industrialization**: Re-industrialization is likely to focus on high-value, technology-intensive smart manufacturing rather than traditional labor-intensive production, including areas such as food technology, advanced materials, and precision engineering.
- **Northern Metropolis as a New Economic Geographic Pivot**. The Northern Metropolis is likely to move from planning into large-scale construction and industrial introduction. It is intended not only as a new residential area but also as Hong Kong's main platform for participation in the national innovation system and for coordination with Shenzhen. The Hetao Co-operation Zone is expected to deepen the "One Zone, Two Parks" arrangement and test institutional changes in the cross-boundary flow of people, goods, capital, and data. If implementation proceeds as planned, these new development areas could generate a substantial number of higher-quality jobs and reshape Hong Kong's economic geography.
    
- **Export of High Value-Added Professional Services**. Hong Kong's common law system, internationally oriented professional base, and institutional link with the Mainland give it an advantage in legal services, arbitration, accounting, architecture, design, and brand management. As Chinese firms expand abroad and Belt and Road projects continue, these services may increasingly serve regional and global markets rather than the local market alone.
    
- **Quality Upgrading of Consumption and Tourism**. Tourism is likely to shift from a focus on visitor volume to a focus on quality and total consumption contribution. Cultural, sports, and mega-event tourism are expected to account for a larger share. Retail is likely to continue moving toward experiential, personalized, and higher-end formats. In this context, Hong Kong aims to strengthen its role as a center for cultural exchange between East and West and to attract higher-spending visitors seeking deeper forms of engagement.
    

### 2.2 Major Risks and Challenges

The outlook for the next five years remains uncertain. Several risks could limit economic performance.

- **Global Economic and Geopolitical Risks**: A global environment of high interest rates and low growth may persist and continue to weaken external demand. If technology and financial decoupling between China and the United States intensifies, Hong Kong's linking role could be directly affected. Rising tension in the Taiwan Strait or the South China Sea could also damage investor confidence and regional stability.
    
- **Financial Market Volatility and Competition**: More volatile global capital flows could affect Hong Kong's equity and property markets. Competition from other financial centers, including Singapore, Shanghai, and Dubai, is likely to remain strong. Hong Kong will need to maintain an advantage in market innovation and operating efficiency.
    
- **Pain of Industrial Transformation and Talent Bottlenecks**: Some traditional industries, including parts of retail and lower-end services, may contract during structural transition, which would require effective retraining and job transition measures. Despite new talent schemes, Hong Kong still faces intense global competition for leading scientists, engineers, and fintech professionals. The supply of locally trained STEM talent, and its willingness to enter local industries, will also remain a constraint.
    
- **Social Cohesion and Business Costs**: If property prices and living costs remain high over the long term, they will continue to weaken social cohesion and reduce urban dynamism. Improving livelihoods while maintaining growth will remain a central governance challenge for the HKSAR Government.
    
- **Data Cross-boundary Flows and Cybersecurity**: As Hong Kong develops the digital economy and the I&T sector, it will need workable institutional arrangements for the orderly cross-boundary flow of data within the Greater Bay Area and beyond, while also ensuring data security and compliance with national regulation.
    

### 2.3 Suggested Strategic Development Pathways

To support sustainable and higher-quality development, Hong Kong needs a strategic approach along the following lines:

- **Adhere to the Precise Positioning of "National Needs, Hong Kong's Strengths"**: Hong Kong should integrate more deeply into national innovation systems and supply chains while continuing to play a distinct role in areas such as financial opening and technology-related institutional experimentation. The Greater Bay Area should be used more fully as economic depth and market support.
    
- **Promote a Better Combination of a "Proactive Government" and an "Efficient Market"**: The government needs to act more decisively in areas such as land, housing, and talent policy. At the same time, policy guidance and public investment should be used to reduce risk for market participants entering sectors such as I&T and the green economy.
    
- **Focus on Building a Global Hub for High-End Talent**: Attraction alone is insufficient. Retention and effective use of talent require internationally competitive compensation, a strong research environment, convenient entry and residence arrangements, and reliable education, healthcare, and living conditions.
    
- **Strengthen Regional Synergy and Rule Alignment**: Within the Greater Bay Area framework, Hong Kong should continue to promote mutual recognition of qualifications, standards alignment, and more efficient customs and clearance arrangements so that factors of production can move more smoothly and institutional differences can be converted into complementary advantages.
    
- **Embrace Green and Digital Transformation**: Carbon neutrality goals should be tied more closely to economic restructuring through the development of green technology, green finance, and the environmental upgrading of traditional sectors. At the same time, digital transformation across government, business, and society should continue in order to improve overall efficiency and competitiveness.

---

## Conclusion

From 2021 to 2025, Hong Kong's economy moved from recession to recovery and then toward relative stabilization. This adjustment rested on institutional continuity, market flexibility, and support from national policy. It was also a period of structural reorientation, in which traditional sectors adapted under pressure and new sectors expanded from a relatively small base.

For 2026 to 2030, Hong Kong's economic path will depend on how it manages several linked tasks: integrating more deeply into national development while retaining its international role, preserving existing strengths while building new sources of growth, and raising economic performance while addressing social pressures. The direction is clear, but the outcome will depend on execution. If Hong Kong can use its connection to both the Mainland and global markets effectively, continue institutional and industrial adjustment, and respond to external risks with some success, it is likely to maintain its position as an international center and contribute more substantially to national high-level opening-up and high-quality development.
