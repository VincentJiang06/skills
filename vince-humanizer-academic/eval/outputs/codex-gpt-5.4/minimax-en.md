# Hong Kong's Economic Development: Analysis of the 2020-2024 Period and Outlook for 2025-2029

## Executive Summary

Hong Kong's economy from 2020 to 2024 was shaped by two major shocks: the COVID-19 pandemic and political restructuring following the National Security Law. After contracting by about 4.8 percent in 2020, the economy grew by 6.6 percent in 2021, then fell again by 3.68 percent in 2022. Growth resumed thereafter, with GDP expanding by 3.28 percent in 2023 and 3.2 percent in 2024. The unemployment rate, which reached 7.2 percent in the December 2020 to February 2021 period, declined to about 3.2 percent by 2024.  

The outlook for 2025-2029 will depend on several linked developments: deeper integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area, major infrastructure projects such as the Northern Metropolis, continued investment in innovation and technology, and Hong Kong's changing role as a global financial center. The government projects growth of 2 to 3 percent for 2025, and medium-term estimates point to average annual growth of about 3 percent through 2030.

## 1. Introduction

Hong Kong has long held a distinct position in the global economy as an intermediary between Mainland China and international markets, while operating under the institutional framework of "one country, two systems." The five years from 2020 to 2024 were among the most demanding in its recent economic history. The period tested the resilience of its institutions and required rapid adjustment across sectors.

Several forces converged during these years. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted travel, trade, and local economic activity on a global scale, hitting sectors dependent on cross-border mobility especially hard. At the same time, the implementation of the National Security Law in June 2020 altered the political environment and affected business expectations and investor sentiment. External pressure also increased as tensions between the United States and Mainland China continued to shape trade and financial conditions.

This paper examines Hong Kong's economic performance and structural change during 2020-2024 through major indicators and sectoral developments. It then considers the outlook for 2025-2029, with attention to policy priorities, growth opportunities, and principal risks. The aim is to provide an evidence-based account of both recent adjustment and the conditions likely to shape the next phase of development.

## 2. Analysis of the Past Five Years (2020-2024)

### 2.1 Macroeconomic Performance and GDP Growth

Hong Kong's macroeconomic performance during 2020-2024 reflected the scale of the pandemic shock and the uneven recovery that followed. In 2020, gross domestic product contracted by about 4.8 percent, the steepest annual decline in decades. The pandemic sharply reduced economic activity from early 2020 onward. Border controls and social distancing measures largely halted tourism, retail, and hospitality. Initial government forecasts projected a contraction of 4 to 7 percent, but these were later revised downward as conditions worsened; some estimates suggested a decline of as much as 8 percent before the year closed with a final contraction of about 4.8 percent.

Economic conditions improved in 2021 as vaccination programs expanded and activity resumed in parts of the global economy. Hong Kong recorded GDP growth of 6.6 percent, its fastest rate since 2010. The rebound was supported by recovering domestic consumption and stronger external demand. Even so, the recovery remained incomplete. Recurrent outbreaks of COVID-19 variants interrupted normalization, and the emergence of the Omicron variant in late 2021 led to renewed restrictions that affected year-end activity and darkened the outlook for 2022.

The economy weakened again in 2022. The fifth wave of COVID-19 infections in early 2022 placed heavy pressure on the healthcare system and led to restrictions that disrupted output, employment, and spending. GDP contracted by 3.68 percent in 2022. Retail sales volumes fell sharply, unemployment rose, and consumer confidence weakened. Much of the partial recovery recorded in 2021 was lost.

Conditions changed in late 2022 and more clearly in 2023, when pandemic restrictions were removed. The decisive step came in February 2023, when the border with Mainland China fully reopened after nearly three years. The reopening brought a rapid increase in visitor arrivals and supported a recovery in tourism and retail. GDP grew by 3.28 percent in 2023, helped by stronger inbound tourism, renewed domestic consumption, and somewhat better external conditions.

Growth continued in 2024, with the economy expanding by about 3.2 percent. Government assessments indicated that this growth was driven mainly by the external sector, while domestic demand also improved. Financial services and trade performed well enough to offset weaker conditions in parts of the local economy. Nominal GDP in 2024 reached about 407 billion USD, and GDP per capita was 54,009 USD, well above the global average.

### 2.2 Labor Market Dynamics and Employment

Hong Kong's labor market deteriorated sharply during the pandemic and then recovered gradually. Before the pandemic, unemployment had been low by historical standards, at about 2.8 percent in early 2019. The social unrest of 2019 and, more importantly, the onset of COVID-19 changed those conditions quickly.

The unemployment rate began to rise in early 2020. In the December 2019 to February 2020 period, it reached 3.7 percent, the highest level in more than nine years at that point. As successive waves of infection disrupted business activity, the labor market weakened further. By April-June 2020, unemployment had risen to 6.2 percent, a 15-year high. The trend continued through late 2020 and early 2021. The seasonally adjusted unemployment rate peaked at 7.2 percent in the December 2020 to February 2021 period, a 17-year high and above the level recorded during the 2003 SARS crisis. Long-term unemployment also reached record levels, with clear social consequences.

The burden was concentrated in sectors tied to consumption and tourism. Unemployment in retail, accommodation, and food services reached double digits. Arts, entertainment, and recreation also saw large losses as public gatherings were restricted and tourist arrivals collapsed. Many small and medium-sized enterprises faced sustained revenue losses while fixed costs remained, leading to layoffs and business closures.

Recovery in the labor market was gradual and uneven. As vaccination rates rose and restrictions were eased, employment began to improve. By July-September 2021, unemployment had declined to 4.5 percent, and the downward trend continued through the rest of the year. That improvement was interrupted in early 2022, when the fifth COVID-19 wave pushed unemployment back above 5 percent before the decline resumed.

The full reopening of the border with Mainland China in February 2023 supported a stronger labor market recovery. Tourism, retail, and hospitality began hiring again, and labor force participation improved. By late 2024 and into 2025, the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate had stabilized at about 3.2 percent. Youth unemployment, which had risen above 12 percent during the worst phases of the crisis, had fallen to about 9.5 percent by 2024. That level remained high relative to earlier years, but it was well below the pandemic peak.

### 2.3 Inflation Trends and Price Stability

Price movements in Hong Kong during 2020-2024 were subdued, and at some points consumer prices fell. Compared with most developed economies, inflation remained low throughout the period, reflecting weak demand during the pandemic and the structure of the local economy.

In 2020, consumer prices declined by about 0.25 percent as the pandemic reduced demand and global oil prices fell. Underlying inflation, which excludes one-off effects from government relief measures, was slightly positive at around 0.5 percent in September 2020 but stayed close to zero for much of the year. This reflected weak domestic consumption, the collapse in tourism spending, and broad uncertainty.

Inflation returned in 2021 as economic activity recovered, but price increases remained moderate at about 1.57 percent for the year. Global reopening increased demand for goods and created some upward pressure on prices, but the linked exchange rate system and competitive labor market conditions limited pass-through. Inflation rose to 1.88 percent in 2022 as supply chain disruption and higher commodity prices affected local consumers.

The pattern continued in 2023, when inflation was about 2.1 percent, before easing to 1.73 percent in 2024. These rates were still low by developed-economy standards. Hong Kong's openness and strong competition in retail and services helped contain price increases. Inflation-linked retail bonds, introduced as part of government efforts to offer residents alternative investment products, tied returns to the local CPI and reflected concern not only with inflation risk but also with the possibility of deflation during the acute phase of the crisis.

### 2.4 The Impact of COVID-19 and Recovery Trajectory

COVID-19 was the largest external shock to Hong Kong's economy since SARS in 2003, but it lasted longer and caused broader economic damage. Its effects worked through several channels at once.

Tourism, a central part of Hong Kong's service economy, contracted almost immediately. Provisional visitor arrivals in 2020 totaled only 3.57 million, down 93.6 percent from about 56 million in 2019. As the virus spread globally in early 2020, Hong Kong moved quickly to restrict entry for overseas visitors and imposed strict quarantine measures from mid-March onward. With varying degrees of severity, these controls remained in place for nearly three years and removed tourism as a source of growth.

Retail was hit just as hard. The sector had already weakened during the 2019 unrest, and 2020 brought a further decline. Retail sales volume in the first quarter of 2020 fell by 36.9 percent year on year, the largest decline on record for that period. For the full year, retail sales volumes fell by 25.5 percent, the largest annual decline since records began. In March 2020 alone, retail sales dropped by 42 percent from a year earlier as shops closed and households reduced spending outside the home.

The hotel and hospitality industries faced similar pressure. With tourist arrivals near zero and local demand weak, occupancy rates fell to historic lows. Many hotels scaled back operations, placed employees on unpaid leave, or closed temporarily. The convention and exhibition sector also declined as events were cancelled or moved online.

Recovery followed the path of the pandemic and the associated policy response. The easing of social distancing measures in late 2021 and early 2022 provided some support, but the fifth wave in early 2022 reversed part of that improvement. The clearer turning point came with the reopening of the border with Mainland China in February 2023, which released pent-up demand and restored a visitor flow that had been interrupted since early 2020.

Government promotion contributed to the rebound. The "Hello Hong Kong" campaign offered 500,000 free airplane tickets and more than one million visitor consumption vouchers. Together with the removal of travel restrictions, these measures supported the return of mainland Chinese visitors, with daily arrivals exceeding 100,000 on several occasions in the months after reopening. Retail recovered strongly in 2023: total sales exceeded 406 billion Hong Kong dollars, up 16.2 percent year on year, and Hong Kong ranked as the world's highest per capita spender in luxury retail for that year.

### 2.5 Political Developments and the National Security Law

The Law of the People's Republic of China on Safeguarding National Security in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region, implemented on June 30, 2020, marked a major change in Hong Kong's political and economic setting. Enacted by the National People's Congress Standing Committee and signed into effect by President Xi Jinping, the law established a legal framework to prevent, suppress, and punish acts and activities deemed to endanger national security. It followed the prolonged unrest of 2019.

Its economic effects were mixed. Some international businesses expressed concern about possible consequences for the business environment and for the civil liberties associated with Hong Kong's role as a global financial center. Some foreign governments issued travel advisories and reconsidered aspects of their relationship with Hong Kong, adding to uncertainty among overseas investors.

Subsequent surveys and business assessments suggested a more differentiated picture. The Hong Kong General Chamber of Commerce reported that 61 percent of surveyed businesses expected the law to have either a positive effect or no effect on their operations over the long term. Many observers attributed the restoration of social stability after implementation to an improvement in the business environment compared with 2019, when violent disruptions had affected transport, retail activity, and investor sentiment. Businesses operating lawfully generally reported no effect on day-to-day commercial activity.

Financial Secretary Paul Chan argued that the law contributed to a safer business environment and that firms from different economies continued to view Hong Kong as a financial hub. The restoration of social and political stability, together with later changes to the electoral system, was presented by the government as creating more predictable conditions for business and investment. Official data also showed that the number of overseas and mainland companies with regional operations in Hong Kong remained substantial, indicating continued demand for Hong Kong as a business base.

These political changes also aligned Hong Kong more closely with Mainland China's broader development agenda. The government increasingly emphasized the use of "one country, two systems" to support integration with national development plans, and that shift became an important feature of economic policy in the years that followed.

### 2.6 Trade Relationships and Geopolitical Dynamics

Hong Kong's trade performance during 2020-2024 was influenced by broader geopolitical tensions, especially between the United States and Mainland China. As a small and highly open economy embedded in Chinese and global supply chains, Hong Kong was exposed to changes in the bilateral relationship between the two largest economies in the world.

The United States had long treated Hong Kong as a separate entity under various laws, including the Hong Kong Policy Act of 1992. After the implementation of the National Security Law and concerns about autonomy, the U.S. Congress passed the Hong Kong Autonomy Act in July 2020. That law authorized sanctions against individuals and entities considered to be undermining Hong Kong's autonomy. These policy changes introduced additional uncertainty into Hong Kong's commercial and financial relations with international partners.

Trade tensions affected Hong Kong through stricter scrutiny of goods transiting through the territory, limits on some technology exports, and measures bearing on the use of Hong Kong's financial infrastructure. For Hong Kong's re-export role between Mainland China and global markets, the implications were significant. Firms reassessed supply chains and sought to reduce exposure to geopolitical risk.

Even so, export performance remained relatively resilient. The value of total merchandise exports recorded positive growth in several years, indicating that Hong Kong continued to play an important role in regional trade flows. Government data showed that merchandise exports rose by 6.5 percent year on year in the first two months of 2025, suggesting continued momentum. At the same time, the government responded to geopolitical uncertainty by promoting stronger trade links with emerging markets in the Global South.

### 2.7 Real Estate Market Dynamics

Hong Kong's real estate market was volatile during 2020-2024. Prices, transaction volumes, and investment sentiment were shaped by the pandemic, shifting economic conditions, and changing expectations. Residential and commercial segments did not move in the same way, reflecting different demand drivers and different exposure to pandemic restrictions.

The residential market had already begun to cool after the government's 2019 cooling measures. The pandemic added further pressure. Transactions fell as uncertainty discouraged both buyers and sellers, and prices declined modestly in some segments. Even so, the market remained firmer than many expected. Some categories of housing, especially those favored by affluent buyers seeking alternatives to other investments, continued to attract demand.

Surveys conducted in mid-2020 showed divided expectations among buyers and investors. Some anticipated price declines because of economic weakness and rising unemployment, while others expected opportunities to emerge during the downturn. By the end of 2020, about 39 percent of respondents expected property prices to rise over the next six months, indicating a more optimistic outlook than in the previous quarter.

The luxury residential segment was notably resilient. Demand came in part from mainland Chinese buyers seeking to hold wealth in stable assets and to buy at valuations that had become more attractive. The role of Hong Kong and Shenzhen as IPO centers also contributed to the formation of a new group of high-net-worth buyers interested in premium residential property. Property consultants reported that transaction volumes in the luxury segment recovered markedly in the second half of 2020 and into 2021, with values in some prime locations reaching levels not seen in earlier periods.

Commercial property, especially office and retail space, faced greater pressure. The long absence of tourists and the spread of remote work reduced demand for office space in central business districts, while retail premises remained vacant as shops operated under repeated restrictions. Vacancy rates increased in some districts, and landlords offered rent concessions to retain tenants. Recovery in commercial property was slower and conditions remained soft through 2024.

Looking ahead, the Northern Metropolis is likely to alter Hong Kong's property market over the longer term. Covering about 30,000 hectares in the northern part of the territory, the project is intended to create new residential communities and commercial districts. It could reduce pressure on the more densely developed southern areas while also expanding the land available for economic activity.

### 2.8 Financial Sector Performance

Hong Kong's financial sector remained comparatively resilient during 2020-2024 and continued to support the territory's standing as a major international financial center. The sector benefited from established strengths: a well-developed regulatory framework, deep liquidity, major international financial institutions, and access to Mainland China's capital markets.

The stock market was volatile, and the Hang Seng Index reflected broader uncertainty. However, the initial public offering market remained active. In 2020, Hong Kong raised about 51.28 billion USD through IPOs, ranking second globally after Nasdaq and recording a 26.5 percent increase over the previous year. A total of 154 companies listed during the year, bringing the total number of listed companies to 2,538.

A notable development during this period was the return of major Chinese technology companies to Hong Kong's market. Alibaba's secondary listing in 2019 had already established a path for mainland firms seeking capital outside the United States. That trend continued during 2020-2024, with companies such as JD.com, JD Health International, and NetEase completing significant offerings in Hong Kong. Changes in Mainland China's regulatory environment and in U.S.-China relations further increased Hong Kong's appeal as an alternative listing venue.

Fintech also became more important within Hong Kong's financial system. The government and regulators moved to develop a policy and regulatory framework for virtual assets and digital finance. In October 2022, the government set out its policy stance on building a virtual assets sector, and the Securities and Futures Commission developed a broader regulatory structure for related activities.

The licensing regime for virtual asset service providers, introduced in June 2023, was a key step. By moving from voluntary participation to mandatory licensing, Hong Kong established a regulated environment for digital assets earlier than many jurisdictions. The market also saw the launch of virtual asset exchange-traded funds, including futures and spot products. By 2024, total assets under management in these products were approaching 300 million USD, indicating growing interest from both institutional and retail investors.

Hong Kong's role in channeling capital between Mainland China and the rest of the world also continued to support the sector. Stock Connect, Bond Connect, and the Greater Bay Area Wealth Management Connect expanded during the period, increasing financial integration while preserving the institutional distinction implied by "one country, two systems."

### 2.9 Tourism Industry Recovery

No sector was affected more directly by the pandemic than tourism. Visitor arrivals fell by more than 93 percent in 2020 as border closures and quarantine requirements effectively ended leisure travel. The 3.57 million arrivals recorded that year, down from 56 million in 2019, amounted to a near shutdown of an industry that had long been an important source of output and employment.

The consequences spread well beyond hotels and airlines. Retailers, restaurants, transport operators, and entertainment venues built around visitor demand lost a large share of their customer base. Hotels that had expanded on the assumption of continued growth were left with substantial excess capacity. Employment in tourism-related activities fell sharply, and unemployment in some segments rose to levels not seen in decades.

Once restrictions were removed, however, the recovery was rapid. The reopening of the border with Mainland China in February 2023 produced an immediate rise in visitor numbers. Within weeks, daily mainland Chinese arrivals exceeded 100,000 on several occasions, the first time those levels had been reached since the imposition of border restrictions. Government support also mattered. The "Hello Hong Kong" campaign distributed free airline tickets, MTR tourist day passes, and consumption vouchers to attract visitors.

Recovery was uneven across source markets. Arrivals from Mainland China rebounded quickly, while the return of visitors from Western markets was slower, reflecting different travel patterns and a more gradual resumption of long-haul travel. Tourism demand also changed in composition. Policymakers placed greater emphasis on value rather than volume and promoted new attractions and experiences designed to draw higher-spending visitors. The government's repositioning of Hong Kong's tourism offer included the language of "healthy tourism" and a broader attempt to refresh the city's appeal after the pandemic.

### 2.10 Innovation and Technology Development

Despite wider economic disruption, Hong Kong's innovation and technology sector continued to develop during 2020-2024. Government support and closer links with Mainland China's technology ecosystem sustained activity, while the National 14th Five-Year Plan identified Hong Kong as an international innovation and technology center.

Public investment in research and development infrastructure continued. Hong Kong Science Park and Cyberport remained central to the support of startups and the attraction of established technology firms. The Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop became a major cross-border initiative. Covering 87 hectares, the park is intended to house innovation and technology firms, research institutions, and related facilities.

Fintech received particular policy attention. The government allocated 50 million Hong Kong dollars to accelerate the development of the Web3 ecosystem, and it set up a task force to advise on sustainable development in the sector. The Securities and Futures Commission's virtual asset framework provided a clearer operating environment for both financial institutions and startups.

Talent development also remained a policy priority. Universities maintained strong international standings and research output, and the government continued efforts to attract overseas professionals through easier visa arrangements and incentives for targeted sectors. New programs in artificial intelligence, biotechnology, and other emerging fields reflected the attempt to expand Hong Kong's skills base in areas with future growth potential.

## 3. Future Outlook (2025-2029)

### 3.1 Macroeconomic Prospects and Growth Trajectory

For 2025-2029, Hong Kong's outlook is one of moderate growth supported by structural strengths and large policy initiatives, but constrained by clear risks. The government's official forecast is for GDP growth of 2 to 3 percent in 2025, and medium-term projections suggest average annual growth of about 3 percent through 2030.

The reopening of the border with Mainland China in early 2023 should continue to support the early part of this period. Consumer spending, tourism, and business activity that were suppressed during the pandemic still have some room to normalize further. The external sector may also benefit from a recovery in global trade and from continued demand for Hong Kong's services as an intermediary between Mainland China and international markets.

Financial Secretary Paul Chan has framed this strategy in terms of using the advantages of "one country, two systems" to integrate more effectively with national development while preserving Hong Kong's links to global markets. The official description of Hong Kong as a "super connector" and "super value-adder" captures this view of its role in regional and international networks. The underlying assumption is that Hong Kong's institutional strengths can complement Mainland China's scale and growth potential.

Inflation is expected to remain contained during the forecast period. Underlying inflation is projected at about 1.7 to 1.8 percent a year. The linked exchange rate system, which pegs the Hong Kong dollar to the U.S. dollar, continues to anchor monetary conditions and price expectations.

The outlook, however, remains uncertain. Geopolitical tensions, especially between the United States and Mainland China, could affect trade and financial flows. Changes in global interest rates will shape asset prices and financing conditions. Domestic constraints, including population ageing and limited land supply, will continue to weigh on potential growth. Policy success will depend on how well Hong Kong manages these constraints while using the opportunities available to it.

### 3.2 Greater Bay Area Integration

The Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area is likely to be one of the most important drivers of Hong Kong's medium-term economic development. The region includes Hong Kong, Macao, and nine Guangdong cities, among them Guangzhou and Shenzhen, and has been designated by Beijing as a major platform for growth and innovation.

Its scale is considerable. Combined output is projected to exceed 15 trillion yuan, or about 2.15 trillion USD, in 2025. For Hong Kong, deeper integration with this regional economy offers an opportunity to combine its comparative strengths in finance, professional services, and international connectivity with the manufacturing base and innovation capacity of mainland cities.

Physical integration within the Greater Bay Area has advanced quickly. Intercity rail has expanded into a network with total mileage of more than 4,000 kilometers. Cross-border passenger routes by water have also increased, and large infrastructure projects have already reduced travel times between Hong Kong and other Greater Bay Area cities. The Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge and the Guangzhou-Shenzhen-Hong Kong Express Rail Link have been especially important in improving the movement of people and goods.

Financial services are likely to benefit directly. Programs such as Wealth Management Connect allow eligible investors in Hong Kong and mainland Greater Bay Area cities to access each other's markets, opening new channels for capital movement and financial intermediation. Hong Kong's position as the largest offshore Renminbi business center also gives it a role in supporting the international use of the Chinese currency and in serving mainland firms operating abroad.

The government has stated that deeper cooperation with Guangdong and Macao will require policy innovation and closer alignment of rules and administrative arrangements. Current priorities include wider mutual market access, easier movement of people, goods, data, and capital, and broader regulatory coordination. The Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Science and Technology Innovation Co-operation Zone is an important part of this agenda, and the operational phase of the Hetao Hong Kong Park is intended to support more intensive cross-border technology collaboration.

### 3.3 Technology and Innovation Hub Development

The development of Hong Kong as an international innovation and technology hub is a central element of the strategy for 2025-2029. The aim is to use Hong Kong's strengths in research, professional services, and international links to build a more active innovation ecosystem that supports both local growth and national policy goals.

This strategy has backing from the National 14th Five-Year Plan, which explicitly supports Hong Kong's development as an international innovation and technology center. Public funding has been directed toward areas such as artificial intelligence, quantum computing, biotechnology, and new energy, indicating the sectors judged to have strategic importance.

Artificial intelligence occupies a prominent place in recent policy statements. The "AI+" strategy envisages the use of AI to raise productivity and develop new capabilities across finance, logistics, healthcare, and other sectors. Hong Kong's research base and its access to the mainland technology ecosystem could allow it to contribute to both the development and application of these technologies.

Web3 and virtual assets form another area of policy interest. The regulatory system established during 2020-2024 provides a foundation for further expansion, and consultations have continued on issues such as the regulation of stablecoin issuers and over-the-counter virtual asset trading. The government's approach has been to support development in the sector while retaining investor protection and regulatory oversight.

The Northern Metropolis will be important to this agenda because it provides physical space for research and technology activity. The San Tin Technopole, which includes the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park, is designed to support clustering among technology firms, research institutions, and skilled workers. That concentration could strengthen Hong Kong's innovation ecosystem if implementation proceeds as planned.

### 3.4 Financial Center Competitiveness

Hong Kong's competitiveness as a global financial center in 2025-2029 will depend on whether it can maintain established strengths while adjusting to regional competition and regulatory change. Its advantages remain substantial: stable regulation, deep capital markets, close access to Mainland China, and a large pool of financial professionals.

Several trends could support the sector. Mainland China's continued capital market opening creates scope for Hong Kong to remain the main gateway for international investors seeking exposure to mainland assets. Stock Connect, Bond Connect, and the expansion of similar programs increase the channels through which capital can move between the two systems. Offshore Renminbi business remains a specific area of strength, with Hong Kong still serving as the main center for offshore Renminbi transactions and financing.

The IPO market is also likely to remain important. Hong Kong's listing regime, including rules permitting weighted voting rights and specialist biotech listings, continues to attract firms in technology and related sectors. If Chinese companies continue to seek alternatives to U.S. capital markets, Hong Kong may remain a preferred listing venue.

Green and sustainable finance offers another area for expansion. Hong Kong has developed a position in green bond issuance and sustainable finance in the Asia-Pacific region, supported by efforts to develop standards, products, and market infrastructure. As global demand for sustainable investment grows, this could bring new business and reinforce the city's role in regional capital markets.

Competition is nonetheless intensifying. Singapore and other regional financial centers are competing more aggressively for firms, capital, and talent. Regulatory shifts connected to U.S.-China relations could also affect Hong Kong's position in cross-border finance. To remain competitive, the city will need continued investment in market infrastructure, human capital, and regulatory adaptation.

### 3.5 Infrastructure Development and Northern Metropolis

The Northern Metropolis is the largest infrastructure and urban development project currently planned in Hong Kong. Covering about 30,000 hectares in the north of the territory, or roughly one-third of Hong Kong's total land area, it is intended to create a new center of growth while easing long-standing constraints on housing and land supply.

The program includes several large projects at different stages of implementation. The Kwu Tung North and Fanling North New Development Areas involve extensive site formation and infrastructure works for new residential and commercial districts. The San Tin Technopole, including the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Loop, is expected to begin operations and release additional land for development.

Government projections indicate that about 60,000 housing units, including roughly 10 new public housing estates, will be completed and ready for occupation over the next five years as part of the Northern Metropolis program. The first tranche of land in the San Tin Technopole is to be released to the market for commercial, research, and residential use. If delivered, these projects will materially expand land supply and create new locations for economic activity.

The development is also intended to change the economic geography of northern Hong Kong. The official long-term vision is for the Northern Metropolis to provide 650,000 jobs, compared with about 110,000 jobs currently available in the new town areas. That implies a large increase in employment capacity and a shift in the spatial distribution of growth.

The government has stated that implementation should proceed faster than in earlier land development cycles, which often took more than a decade from raw land to developable site. Steering and advisory committees have been established to accelerate decision-making while maintaining fiscal prudence. Whether the project achieves its stated goals will depend on financing, coordination, and administrative execution over several years.

### 3.6 Regional Trade Opportunities

Trade diversification is likely to remain a major policy objective during 2025-2029. While North America and Europe will remain important markets, faster-growing economies in the Global South offer alternative sources of demand and investment.

Government trade promotion has increasingly focused on Southeast Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Latin America. These markets are expanding demand for finance, logistics, legal services, and other professional services in which Hong Kong has experience. The overseas expansion of mainland Chinese firms also creates demand for Hong Kong as a platform for financing, advisory work, and international market entry.

Additional bilateral and regional economic arrangements may further strengthen Hong Kong's trade links. Although its constitutional position places limits on its participation in some multilateral trade agreements, targeted agreements with individual economies or regional groupings can still improve market access and reduce barriers.

The Belt and Road Initiative continues to provide opportunities, especially in services. Hong Kong has capabilities in project finance, insurance, legal advice, risk management, and dispute resolution that are relevant to infrastructure and development projects across participating economies. Its role as an arbitration and dispute resolution center may be especially important in this context.

Digital trade and e-commerce offer another avenue for growth. Better physical and digital connectivity can lower barriers for small and medium-sized enterprises seeking international customers. Hong Kong's logistics infrastructure and regulatory framework for digital commerce may allow it to capture part of this expanding market.

### 3.7 Challenges and Risks

The medium-term outlook is accompanied by substantial risks. These will affect not only the pace of growth but also the durability of any recovery.

The most important external risk is geopolitical tension. Continued friction between the United States and Mainland China creates uncertainty for Hong Kong's trade, financial, and legal relationships. Further changes in the special treatment Hong Kong receives under U.S. law, additional sanctions, or tighter restrictions could affect access to markets and financial infrastructure. Any escalation in cross-strait tensions involving Taiwan would also carry implications for regional confidence and economic stability.

Global monetary conditions present another source of uncertainty. Lower interest rates in major economies could support asset prices and activity, but any reversal, or a return of inflationary pressure, could produce financial volatility. Because Hong Kong operates under a linked exchange rate system, U.S. Federal Reserve policy strongly influences local monetary conditions, even when domestic economic circumstances differ.

Domestic structural challenges are also important. Population ageing will place increasing pressure on labor supply and on public spending. The government's fiscal position, weakened by large pandemic-era deficits, requires continued attention. Although the operating account returned to surplus in fiscal year 2025-26 because tax revenue rose with economic recovery and stronger equity markets, land premium income and some other revenue sources remain below earlier norms.

Competition for talent is another concern. Hong Kong still offers high-quality universities, international connectivity, and close links to mainland markets, but regional competition for skilled workers is increasing. Housing costs and other living expenses will continue to affect the city's ability to attract and retain professionals.

Environmental sustainability introduces both constraints and opportunities. High-density urban development creates environmental pressure, but it also provides scope for advances in green building, low-carbon transport, renewable energy, and sustainable urban design. Moving toward a greener economy will require investment and adjustment across sectors.

## 4. Conclusion

Hong Kong's economic development from 2020 to 2024 was shaped by a deep pandemic shock and by substantial political change following the implementation of the National Security Law. The economy entered its sharpest recession in decades in 2020, recovered partially in 2021, weakened again in 2022, and returned to moderate growth in 2023 and 2024 after the border with Mainland China reopened. This sequence showed both the scale of the disruption and the economy's capacity to recover once mobility and demand were restored.

Several findings stand out from the 2020-2024 record. The pandemic caused severe but ultimately temporary damage, while the main supports of Hong Kong's economy remained in place: its role as a financial center, its links to Mainland China, and its open trading system. The labor market adjusted with difficulty but improved substantially as restrictions were lifted, with unemployment falling from above 7 percent to around 3.2 percent. The financial sector remained active throughout the period, sustaining Hong Kong's position in IPOs and cross-border finance despite geopolitical uncertainty.

The outlook for 2025-2029 is for moderate growth rather than rapid expansion. Integration with the Greater Bay Area offers scope for Hong Kong to combine its strengths in finance and professional services with the industrial and technological capacity of neighboring mainland cities. The Northern Metropolis could ease land constraints and create new centers of production, research, and housing. Continued development in artificial intelligence, Web3, and related fields may widen the economic base if regulation, infrastructure, and talent policy remain aligned.

Several policy priorities follow from this assessment. Hong Kong is likely to benefit from deeper integration with the Greater Bay Area, but that integration will be most useful if the city also preserves the institutional and commercial features that distinguish it internationally. Investment in innovation and technology should continue, together with support for research infrastructure, skills, and regulatory clarity in emerging sectors. Trade and investment links should broaden further toward emerging markets, while domestic structural issues such as housing affordability, population ageing, and fiscal sustainability require continued attention.

Hong Kong enters the 2025-2029 period with significant assets: established financial infrastructure, close ties with Mainland China, a skilled services economy, and an ability to adapt under pressure. Whether those assets translate into sustained growth will depend on implementation, policy coherence, and the city's ability to operate effectively within a more complex geopolitical and regional environment.
