# Hong Kong's Economic Development: Retrospect and Prospect (2021-2030)

## Abstract

This paper reviews the economic development of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR) from 2021 to 2025 and analyzes its growth drivers, structural constraints, and major policy responses. The evidence suggests that, after the combined shocks of social unrest and the global pandemic, Hong Kong entered a recovery phase in 2021 and moved onto a more stable growth path after 2023. Its established position as an international financial, trade, and shipping center was reinforced through national support and domestic adjustment, while emerging sectors such as innovation and technology registered initial progress. On that basis, the paper considers development prospects, possible growth paths, major risks, and strategic directions for 2026 to 2030 in light of existing plans and broader macroeconomic trends. Over the next five years, Hong Kong is likely to continue adjusting under the overlapping pressures of integration into national development, industrial upgrading, and the preservation of its distinct advantages. Its long-term prosperity and stability will depend on whether it can manage structural transformation while maintaining a workable balance between domestic and external conditions.

**Keywords**: Hong Kong Economy; Economic Development; 14th Five-Year Plan; Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area; International Financial Center; Innovation and Technology

## Part I: Retrospective on Economic Development (2021-2025)

### 1.1 Overall Economic Performance and Cyclical Fluctuations

From 2021 to 2025, Hong Kong’s economy followed a V-shaped pattern marked by recession, uneven recovery, and subsequent stabilization. This trajectory reflected the interaction of external shocks, domestic disruptions, and policy responses.

In 2021, after the sharp contraction of 2020 caused by the lingering effects of social unrest and the global pandemic, Hong Kong’s economy rebounded strongly. Real GDP grew by 6.4%, driven mainly by strong global goods demand in the first half of the year, which supported a marked increase in exports. The recovery, however, was uneven. Sectors tied closely to external trade recovered more quickly, while tourism, retail, and catering, all highly dependent on cross-border mobility, remained under severe pressure. Total inbound arrivals for the year were about 90,000, compared with nearly 56 million in 2019 before the pandemic, leaving sustained pressure across related service industries.

In 2022, the economic environment became more difficult. The fifth wave of the pandemic early in the year disrupted local consumption and business activity. At the same time, major global central banks began an aggressive cycle of interest rate increases to contain inflation, weakening external demand and increasing financial market volatility. Geopolitical tensions, especially uncertainty in China-U.S. relations, added further pressure on Hong Kong’s position as an international business center. Under these conditions, annual economic growth slowed to 3.2%. Activity improved in the second half of the year as anti-pandemic measures were eased, but inbound tourism remained weak and limited the pace of recovery.

The years 2023 to 2025 marked a move toward fuller recovery and structural adjustment. In 2023, Hong Kong returned to normal mobility conditions. The full resumption of travel with the Mainland and overseas markets at the start of the year became the main source of growth. Inbound tourist arrivals rose sharply to about 34 million. Although still below pre-pandemic levels, this recovery lifted tourism, hospitality, retail, and catering. Financial markets remained under pressure from high global interest rates and weak financing activity, but the banking sector was broadly stable, and areas such as Renminbi (RMB) business and wealth management remained resilient. In 2024 and 2025, the economy is expected to maintain moderate growth, with a broader set of growth drivers. Government efforts to attract firms and talent have begun to produce results and have partly offset fluctuations in external demand. Growth during this period is projected to remain in the 2.5% to 3.5% range. The economic structure also shows clearer movement toward innovation and technology and green finance, while traditional strengths remain important.

### 1.2 Key Driving Factors and Evolution of Pillar Industries

Over the past five years, Hong Kong’s economic drivers have reflected pressure on traditional pillar industries to adjust and policy support for emerging sectors.

Financial services remained central to the economy. Over this period, the sector faced major changes in the global interest rate environment, rising geopolitical risk, and volatile capital markets, yet its core role was maintained.

Stock market activity, especially initial public offerings (IPOs), fluctuated with global market sentiment. At the same time, Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing Limited (HKEX) continued to expand and refine Stock Connect arrangements, including the extension of Mainland-Hong Kong market links, the launch of Swap Connect, and consideration of RMB counters. These changes reinforced Hong Kong’s role as an intermediary between Mainland China and international capital markets.

Asset and wealth management also remained important. Hong Kong continued to benefit from the growth of Mainland wealth and from international demand for diversified asset allocation. Family office business and Wealth Management Connect became policy priorities. Although competition from Singapore intensified, Hong Kong retained advantages in market size, legal institutions, and connectivity with the Mainland.

In fintech and green finance, the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) promoted further development. Virtual banks and digital payments became more widely used. Green and sustainable finance expanded quickly, and Hong Kong became one of Asia’s major centers for green bond issuance, accompanied by continued product innovation.

Hong Kong’s position as the world’s largest offshore RMB business hub was also strengthened. RMB deposits, loans, foreign exchange transactions, and clearing business all expanded steadily.

Trade and logistics continued to face both pressure and opportunity as global supply chains adjusted. Hong Kong’s role as a global trade center came under strain from supply-chain regionalization, rising protectionism, and geopolitical risk.

Goods trade moved with global cyclical conditions. Hong Kong retained strengths in the re-export of high-end consumer products and electronics, but it also faced stronger competition from Mainland ports and from broader supply-chain reconfiguration.

Trade-related professional services, including arbitration, testing and certification, and supply-chain management, remained competitive.

In shipping and air logistics, Hong Kong International Airport continued to rank among the world’s busiest cargo airports. The completion of the Three-Runway System in 2024 is expected to expand capacity substantially. Recovery in passenger aviation, however, has been constrained by the pace of tourism recovery and growing regional competition.

Tourism and retail experienced the most direct and severe disruption from the pandemic. After reopening, Mainland visitors remained the dominant source of arrivals, but spending behavior became more restrained and shifted toward in-depth and cultural experiences. The older image of Hong Kong as a shopping-focused destination weakened relative to the past. Growth in short-haul markets, especially Southeast Asia, emerged as a new source of support.

Retail sales recovered significantly in 2023, though unevenly. Luxury goods and cosmetics rebounded more quickly than mass-market consumer categories. The sector accelerated online-merge-offline (OMO) integration and expanded cross-border e-commerce in response to new consumption habits.

Innovation and technology emerged more clearly as a policy-led growth sector. The current government elevated innovation and technology (I&T) to a strategic priority, established the Office for Attracting Strategic Enterprises (OASES), launched the Research, Academic and Industry Sectors One-plus Scheme (RAISe+), and advanced the Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Science and Technology Innovation Co-operation Zone.

Life and health technology became a major focus and attracted several internationally leading biopharmaceutical and medical device firms to establish or expand research and development operations in Hong Kong.

Artificial intelligence and data science also advanced in research and application, although commercialization and industrial scale remain limited.

The start-up ecosystem continued to expand. The number of start-ups in Hong Kong increased, and although venture capital activity was affected by tighter global financing conditions, government-backed funds helped sustain interest in deep technology sectors. Constraints remain, including the small size of the local market, intense competition for high-level talent, and the need for stronger channels to commercialize research outcomes.

### 1.3 Major Challenges and Structural Contradictions

The experience of the past five years has made several structural challenges more visible.

External uncertainty remains high. Weak global growth, divergent monetary policies among major economies, ongoing geopolitical conflict such as the Russia-Ukraine war, and the long duration of China-U.S. strategic competition all affect Hong Kong through trade, finance, and confidence channels. As a highly open and relatively small economy, Hong Kong is especially exposed to these external conditions.

The economy also remains concentrated in a limited number of sectors. Despite efforts to diversify, finance, trade logistics, professional services, and tourism-related retail still account for most of GDP. Dependence on these sectors and on external conditions reduces resilience when cyclical downturns or structural changes occur.

There is also continuing tension between livelihood conditions and economic development. Problems such as housing affordability, wealth inequality, and limited upward mobility among younger people were not resolved by the post-pandemic recovery. High living and operating costs continue to affect Hong Kong’s appeal to both firms and skilled workers. A more even distribution of the gains from growth remains important for long-term social stability and economic sustainability.

Regional competition has intensified. Singapore competes directly with Hong Kong in attracting multinational headquarters, family offices, and high-level talent. Mainland cities such as Shanghai and Shenzhen have also advanced rapidly in finance and technology, placing greater pressure on Hong Kong’s traditional advantages. Hong Kong therefore faces the task of preserving its distinctive functions while identifying new sources of differentiation.

### 1.4 Major Policies and Strategic Responses

In response to these pressures, the HKSAR Government, supported by the Central Government, introduced a series of policy measures and longer-term strategies.

A central policy direction over the past five years has been deeper alignment with national development. Under the framework of the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA), Hong Kong has participated more actively in regional integration. Progress has been made in financial connectivity, the construction of an innovation and technology corridor, and the alignment of rules and institutional mechanisms. Platforms such as Qianhai, Nanshan, and Hetao have expanded access for Hong Kong firms and professionals to Mainland markets.

The National 14th Five-Year Plan gave Hong Kong a clearer strategic position. It explicitly supported the city in consolidating and strengthening its status as an international financial, shipping, and trade center; enhancing its role as a global offshore RMB business hub, an international asset management center, and a risk management center; and developing into an international innovation and technology center and a regional intellectual property trading center. This policy framework provided institutional backing and strategic direction.

The government also adopted more active measures to attract firms and talent. In response to outward migration of skilled workers and the relocation of some businesses, it introduced policies such as the Top Talent Pass Scheme, the new Capital Investment Entrant Scheme, and a range of sector-specific funding and subsidy programs. These measures have begun to produce visible effects.

Long-term planning and infrastructure investment have also remained central. The Northern Metropolis Development Strategy has been promoted as a future engine of growth, with the aim of closer integration with Shenzhen and other Greater Bay Area cities in innovation and technology and related industries. At the same time, major infrastructure projects such as the Three-Runway System and the Lantau Tomorrow Vision have continued as part of a longer-term effort to expand capacity and sustain competitiveness.

Financial stability has been preserved through maintenance of the Linked Exchange Rate System and prudent regulation. Despite volatility in global financial markets and episodes of capital outflow pressure, the Hong Kong Monetary Authority preserved exchange rate stability. Strong capital and liquidity positions in the banking system helped sustain investor confidence and demonstrated the resilience of Hong Kong’s financial institutions.

## Part II: Future Economic Development Outlook (2026-2030)

### 2.1 Growth Drivers and Potential Pathways

Between 2026 and 2030, Hong Kong is likely to face an environment shaped by both opportunity and constraint. Its growth drivers may become more diversified and less dependent on a narrow set of external conditions.

Finance is expected to remain the core engine of the economy, but with a shift from expansion in scale toward functional upgrading and product innovation.

As the RMB takes on a larger role in international trade and reserves, Hong Kong’s importance in offshore RMB pricing, clearing, and product development is likely to increase. A broader range of RMB-denominated financial products, including equities, commodities, and derivatives, may develop on that basis.

Green and sustainable finance is also likely to expand further. Global energy transition policies and the continued growth of ESG investment could support Hong Kong’s development as a regional and potentially global center for green capital allocation.

Asset and wealth management is expected to return to a stronger growth path by 2030. Family offices, cross-border pension investment, and regulated virtual asset management are likely to become important sources of expansion.

Demand for risk management services may also rise as geopolitical and economic volatility persists. This could create additional room for growth in insurance, reinsurance, and derivatives markets.

Innovation and technology may enter a more productive phase if earlier policy investment begins to yield industrial outcomes. In life and health technology, Hong Kong has the potential to build an internationally significant cluster in biotechnology research and development, clinical trials, and advanced medical devices by drawing on university research capacity and the presence of leading firms.

Artificial intelligence is also likely to see wider application in finance, smart city governance, and professional services such as legal technology and construction technology. Wider adoption could improve efficiency and service quality across the economy.

Re-industrialization is expected to focus on high-value, technology-intensive manufacturing rather than traditional labor-intensive production. Areas such as food technology, advanced materials, and precision engineering are likely to be more relevant than conventional manufacturing sectors.

The Northern Metropolis may become a new geographic center of economic activity as it moves from planning into large-scale construction and industrial development. Its role is likely to extend beyond residential expansion. It is expected to function as Hong Kong’s main point of integration with the national innovation system and with Shenzhen. The Hetao Co-operation Zone may play a central role if the “One Zone, Two Parks” framework can support institutional experimentation in the cross-boundary movement of people, goods, capital, and data. If implemented effectively, the area could attract leading technology enterprises and research institutions, generate a substantial number of high-quality jobs, and reshape Hong Kong’s economic geography.

High value-added professional services may also gain wider scope. Drawing on the common law system, internationally trained professionals, and close links with the Mainland, Hong Kong’s legal, arbitration, accounting, architecture, design, and brand management services may find larger markets as Chinese firms expand overseas and Belt and Road projects deepen. In that case, these services would increasingly serve regional and international demand rather than primarily local demand.

Consumption and tourism are also likely to change in composition. Tourism policy may place less emphasis on total visitor numbers and more on spending quality and broader economic contribution. Cultural tourism, sports tourism, and major events may account for a larger share of activity. Retail is likely to continue shifting toward experience-based, personalized, and high-end formats. In this context, Hong Kong may seek to strengthen its role as a center for cultural exchange between East and West and attract higher-spending visitors who stay longer and engage more deeply with the city.

### 2.2 Major Risks and Challenges

The period from 2026 to 2030 will also involve significant risks that could restrict growth.

Global economic and geopolitical risks remain central. A prolonged combination of high interest rates and weak global growth could continue to suppress external demand. If China-U.S. tensions in technology and finance intensify further, Hong Kong’s role as an intermediary between the Mainland and international markets could be directly affected. Greater instability in areas such as the Taiwan Strait or the South China Sea would also weigh heavily on investor confidence and regional stability.

Financial market volatility and inter-city competition will remain relevant constraints. Large swings in global capital flows could affect Hong Kong’s equity and property markets. Competition from Singapore, Shanghai, and Dubai is likely to remain strong, requiring Hong Kong to sustain advantages in institutional reliability, product innovation, and operating efficiency.

Industrial transformation will also create adjustment costs. Traditional sectors, including parts of retail and lower-end services, may contract as the economy restructures, creating pressure for retraining and labour-market adjustment. At the same time, Hong Kong faces strong international competition for scientists, engineers, and financial technology specialists. Whether domestic STEM graduates are sufficient in number and whether they are willing to remain in local industries will be an important test.

Social cohesion and cost pressures remain closely linked. If high housing prices and living costs persist, they are likely to continue weakening social cohesion and reducing urban vitality. The government’s ability to improve living conditions while sustaining economic development will remain a major governance issue.

Data governance presents another unresolved challenge. As Hong Kong expands its digital economy and innovation sectors, it will need institutional arrangements that allow orderly cross-boundary flows of data within the Greater Bay Area and beyond while also meeting security requirements and complying with national regulations.

### 2.3 Suggested Strategic Development Pathways

To support sustainable and higher-quality development, Hong Kong will need a strategy that combines national integration, domestic reform, and institutional adaptation.

A first requirement is to maintain a clear development position based on “national needs and Hong Kong’s strengths.” This means deeper participation in the national innovation system and supply chains, alongside a distinct role in areas such as financial opening and experimentation under controlled conditions. The Greater Bay Area can serve as both economic depth and operational hinterland in this process.

A second requirement is a more effective relationship between government action and market allocation. The government will need to act more decisively in areas such as land supply, housing, and talent policy. At the same time, public investment and targeted policy guidance can reduce the risks faced by private firms entering sectors such as innovation and technology and the green economy.

A third priority is the development of Hong Kong as a global center for high-end talent. Attraction alone will not be sufficient. Retention and effective use of talent will depend on internationally competitive pay, research conditions, migration and residence arrangements, and the broader quality of education, healthcare, and daily life.

A fourth priority is stronger regional coordination and rule alignment. Within the Greater Bay Area, Hong Kong will need to support more extensive “soft connectivity,” including mutual recognition of qualifications, greater compatibility of standards, and more efficient customs and clearance arrangements. Better coordination of this kind could allow production factors to move more efficiently and could turn institutional differences into economic advantages.

A fifth priority is to link green transition and digital transition more closely with economic restructuring. Progress toward carbon neutrality can support the development of green technology and green finance while also encouraging traditional sectors to upgrade. At the same time, broader digital transformation in government, business, and society can raise efficiency and strengthen Hong Kong’s overall competitiveness.

## Conclusion

From 2021 to 2025, Hong Kong’s economy moved through a period of disruption, recovery, and adjustment. Despite substantial setbacks, it returned to growth and greater stability on the basis of its institutional foundations, market adaptability, and support from the national development framework. These years also exposed the depth of Hong Kong’s structural adjustment challenge. Traditional pillar industries retained their central role but did so under growing pressure to adapt, while emerging sectors advanced mainly through policy support and early-stage investment.

For 2026 to 2030, Hong Kong’s economic trajectory will depend on how it manages several tensions at the same time. It will need to work within the framework of “One Country, Two Systems” while preserving the institutional features that support its international role. It will need to maintain established strengths in finance, trade, and services while building new sources of growth. It will need to deepen integration with national development while remaining outward-facing and internationally credible. It will also need to pursue growth in ways that respond more effectively to issues of housing, inequality, and social mobility.

Hong Kong’s economic history has repeatedly shown a capacity for adaptation under pressure. If it can continue to draw on the advantage of being both connected to the Mainland and open to the world, while addressing structural constraints with sustained reform, it is likely to preserve its position as an international center over the next five years and beyond. The outcome will matter not only for Hong Kong’s own long-term prosperity and stability, but also for the wider process of national opening-up and high-quality development.
