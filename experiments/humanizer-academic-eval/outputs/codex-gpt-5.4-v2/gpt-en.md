# Hong Kong’s Economic Development in the Past Five Years and Its Prospects for the Next Five Years (2021–2025; 2026–2030)

### Abstract

From the vantage point of April 2026, Hong Kong’s economic path over 2021–2025 is better understood as a sequence of shocks and structural adjustment than as a simple post-pandemic recovery. Real GDP grew by 6.4% in 2021, contracted by 3.5% in 2022, recovered by 3.2% in 2023, expanded by 2.5% in 2024, and accelerated to 3.5% in 2025. Unemployment fell sharply from its pandemic peak, but the recovery was uneven. The economy moved from border restrictions and a collapse in tourism toward renewed growth in finance, services exports, inbound travel, and technology-related activities. At the same time, commercial real estate remained weak, fiscal revenue from land stayed subdued, and demographic pressures became clearer. Hong Kong did not simply return to its earlier model; it began to adjust to a different one.

For 2026–2030, moderate growth is a more realistic expectation than rapid expansion. The HKSAR Government forecasts real GDP growth of 2.5% to 3.5% in 2026 and average annual growth of about 3% in 2027–2030, while the IMF projects medium-term growth closer to 2.5% because of rapid population ageing and slower capital accumulation. The central issue over the next five years is therefore whether Hong Kong can consolidate a growth model based on international finance, offshore RMB business, cross-border wealth management, high-end services, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, and deeper integration with the Greater Bay Area, rather than recreate the property-led and retail-led expansions of earlier decades.

### Introduction

Using 2021–2025 as the past five years and 2026–2030 as the next five years provides a clear analytical frame. Within it, Hong Kong’s recent economic record falls into three phases. The first was the rebound in 2021, driven mainly by exports and policy support rather than by a broad recovery in local services. The second was the setback in 2022, when a major local COVID outbreak and tighter global financial conditions pushed the economy back into contraction. The third was the reopening period from 2023 to 2025, when tourism, cross-border mobility, external trade, and capital-market sentiment improved step by step. This sequence matters because the stronger data in 2025 do not amount to a full restoration of the previous growth regime. They point instead to a transition toward a new economic structure that remains incomplete.

### I. A Stage-by-Stage Review of 2021–2025

In 2021, Hong Kong recorded a strong cyclical rebound, but the recovery was narrow. Real GDP expanded by 6.4%, reversing the declines of the previous two years, yet the economy was still about 2% smaller than in 2018. Labour-market conditions improved from the worst pandemic levels, but the annual unemployment rate still averaged 5.2%. Tourism was largely absent, with full-year visitor arrivals of only about 91,000. The year therefore reflected a rebound driven by external demand and base effects rather than a normal recovery in domestic demand, mobility, or the visitor economy.

That narrow recovery was exposed in 2022. Real GDP fell by 3.5%, private consumption declined by 1.1%, and gross domestic fixed capital formation dropped by 8.5%. Hong Kong faced the fifth wave of COVID, continuing mobility constraints, and the spillover effects of aggressive US monetary tightening under the Linked Exchange Rate System. Tourist arrivals recovered only marginally, to about 600,000 for the year. The contraction showed how exposed the economy remained to disruptions in border flows, service-sector activity, and global financial conditions.

The turning point came in 2023, the first full year after reopening. Real GDP grew by 3.2%, and private consumption expenditure surged by 7.4%, indicating a strong rebound in household activity and service demand. Full-year visitor arrivals recovered to 34 million, and tourism’s value added rose back to 2.6% of GDP. The recovery, however, was uneven. Goods exports remained soft in real terms, so the fastest improvement occurred in mobility, travel, and face-to-face services rather than across the economy as a whole. The year marked reconnection more than a return to previous conditions.

In 2024, growth continued, but its composition changed. Real GDP expanded by 2.5%. Goods exports resumed growth, services exports continued to rise, and overall investment expenditure increased further. However, private consumption expenditure edged down, suggesting that domestic demand remained weaker than the headline GDP figure implied. Residential property prices fell by 7.1% for the year. Visitor arrivals reached nearly 45 million, up 31% year on year, but the tourism rebound did not translate automatically into a broad recovery in consumption and property markets. Growth was supported externally, while local demand remained fragile.

By 2025, the recovery had become broader. Real GDP grew by 3.5%. Service-sector business receipts showed strong momentum: financing excluding banking, insurance, import/export trade, and banking all recorded double-digit growth, while computer and information technology services rose by 85.5% for the year. Visitor arrivals climbed to 49.9 million, retail sales value increased by 1.0% for the year, and the stock market rebounded strongly. Even so, the recovery was not simply consumption-led. It rested on stronger exports, more active capital markets, renewed tourism flows, and the increasing weight of technology-related and financial services.

### II. Structural Changes Over the Past Five Years

The first major structural change is the renewed centrality of finance and other high-value services. In 2025, the goods balance remained negative on a GDP basis, but the services surplus reached HK$168.9 billion and the combined goods-and-services balance remained positive. The current account surplus was HK$406.6 billion, equal to 12.2% of GDP. By the end of 2025, bank deposits had exceeded HK$19 trillion. The SFC’s latest survey showed that Hong Kong’s asset and wealth management business had reached HK$35.1 trillion in assets under management at end-2024, and Hong Kong regained first place globally in IPO fundraising in 2025 with roughly HK$286 billion raised. These figures indicate renewed strength in the financial sector after several uneven years.

The second structural change is the widening gap between tourism recovery in volume and tourism performance in spending quality. Visitor arrivals rose from about 91,000 in 2021 to 600,000 in 2022, 34 million in 2023, nearly 45 million in 2024, and 49.9 million in 2025. Tourism’s contribution to GDP had already returned to 2.8% in 2024, and the sector employed about 159,700 people that year; hotel occupancy in 2025 averaged around 87%. At the same time, the government stated in April 2026 that both the average length of stay and per-capita spending of Mainland visitors had declined in 2025. Hong Kong has therefore recovered tourism volume without fully restoring tourism value. The next stage depends on whether events, conventions, concerts, sports, and cultural attractions can generate higher-yield visitor spending rather than mainly short-duration, low-margin visits.

The third structural change concerns trade and logistics. In customs terms, merchandise exports and imports both grew strongly in 2025, but Hong Kong still recorded a trade deficit of HK$446.6 billion. Total port cargo throughput fell by 4.0% to 169.6 million tonnes, and container throughput declined by 5.1% to 12.99 million TEUs. By contrast, Hong Kong International Airport handled 4.9 million tonnes of cargo in 2024, accounting for about 45% of the value of Hong Kong’s external trade. Hong Kong’s trade role is shifting away from port volume toward high-value air cargo, re-export intermediation, financial settlement, supply-chain coordination, and cross-border services. This is both an upgrade and an adjustment to stronger regional competition in conventional logistics.

The fourth structural change is the reduced centrality of property in both growth and public finance. Residential property prices fell by 7.1% in 2024 and stabilised only in 2025, when they rose by 3.3%, ending a three-year decline. The commercial property market remained weak, and the government explicitly noted that land premium revenue stayed low, with the 2025–26 revised estimate at only HK$17.5 billion. Fiscal reserves had fallen to HK$647.3 billion by the end of March 2025, though they were then expected to recover to HK$657.2 billion by the end of March 2026 and to rise above HK$700 billion over the medium term. Hong Kong can no longer rely on land and property as the dominant fiscal and macroeconomic engine that they once were.

### III. Prospects for 2026–2030

Taken together, official and IMF forecasts point to a moderate-growth outlook. The government expects real growth of 2.5% to 3.5% in 2026 and about 3% annual growth in 2027–2030. The IMF expects growth to converge toward roughly 2.5% over the medium term because of ageing and slower capital accumulation. The most plausible baseline is therefore annual growth of around 2.5% to 3.0% over the next five years. This would be slower than Hong Kong’s strongest pre-2019 periods, but still solid for a mature, service-dominated, externally exposed economy undergoing structural change.

The first major growth pillar over the next five years is likely to remain international finance, though in a more diversified form. With bank deposits above HK$19 trillion, substantial inflows into Hong Kong-domiciled funds, the continuation of Wealth Management Connect, and the successful issuance of digital green bonds, Hong Kong is positioning itself as a cross-border wealth-management, offshore RMB, green-finance, and capital-intermediation hub as well as an equity market. Future growth is therefore likely to come less from traditional brokerage activity alone than from a broader ecosystem of asset management, family offices, cross-border investment channels, bonds, structured products, and financial infrastructure.

The second pillar is innovation and industrial upgrading linked to the Northern Metropolis. The 2025–26 Budget earmarked HK$1 billion for the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute and expanded support for AI applications, microelectronics, and new industrialisation. The 2026–27 Budget reinforced this agenda by identifying the Northern Metropolis as a long-term development engine, adding HK$30 billion in works expenditure over the coming two to three years and projecting capital works expenditure of about HK$128 billion in 2026–27. The government also stated that more than 100 strategic enterprises had been attracted to Hong Kong, bringing around HK$60 billion in investment and about 22,000 jobs. If these measures move beyond announcement into sustained R&D, production, hiring, and export activity, they could broaden Hong Kong’s growth base beyond finance and property.

The third pillar is tourism, events, and the wider experience economy. Hong Kong projects 53.8 million visitor arrivals in 2026, and the first quarter of 2026 already recorded more than 14.31 million arrivals, up 17% year on year. In 2025, the city also welcomed 2.51 million MICE visitors, who generated about HK$10.7 billion in spending. Alongside the opening of Kai Tak Sports Park and a larger calendar of cultural and sports events, this creates room for further expansion in hotels, restaurants, retail, and transportation. The critical question, however, is whether Hong Kong can improve visitor quality through longer stays, higher per-capita spending, stronger convention demand, and better links between events and local consumption, rather than simply increase headcount.

These growth drivers face clear structural constraints. The Census and Statistics Department projects that the elderly population aged 65 and over will rise from 1.45 million in 2021 to 2.74 million in 2046, while the labour-force participation rate excluding foreign domestic helpers will decline from 55.2% in 2023 to 51.6% in 2046. The Labour and Welfare Bureau’s manpower projection further estimates a labour shortage of 180,000 over the next five years. The IMF has also warned that Hong Kong’s traditional role as a logistics and trading centre remains under pressure from regional competition. Even if cyclical conditions improve, medium-term growth is therefore likely to remain constrained unless Hong Kong raises productivity, attracts talent, and scales new industries successfully.

External risks also remain substantial. The 2026–27 Budget notes that global trade tensions have moderated, but it also stresses that protectionism, fragmentation, and policy uncertainty in major advanced economies will persist. The HKMA has likewise highlighted risks arising from tariffs and a weak property market. An upside scenario for 2026–2030 would combine steadier US easing, firmer Mainland growth, successful execution of the Northern Metropolis, and continued strength in capital markets. A downside scenario would involve renewed tariff escalation, weaker Mainland demand, and prolonged weakness in commercial property. The baseline suggested by the official and IMF evidence is that Hong Kong will remain a resilient low- to medium-growth economy with strong external buffers and substantial high-value service capacity, rather than revert to a boom economy.

### Conclusion

Hong Kong’s economy over 2021–2025 moved from rebound to relapse and then to broader recovery. The deeper change was structural. Finance revived, tourism returned in volume, technology policy gained weight, and property became a less reliable source of growth and fiscal revenue. Over 2026–2030, Hong Kong’s performance will depend less on restoring the previous formula than on combining finance, offshore RMB business, wealth management, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, Greater Bay Area linkages, and wider international connectivity within a more diversified model. If that transition succeeds, Hong Kong can remain steadily prosperous without very high growth. If it does not, the city may retain deep capital, strong institutions, and international relevance while operating with a lower trend growth rate.
