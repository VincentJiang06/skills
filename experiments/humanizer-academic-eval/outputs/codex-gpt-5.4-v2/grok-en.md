# Economic Development of Hong Kong: A Review of 2021–2025 and Prospects for 2026–2030

## Abstract
Hong Kong, an international financial centre in Asia and a Special Administrative Region of the People’s Republic of China, faced major domestic and external pressures between 2021 and 2025. During these five years, the economy moved through post-pandemic recovery, geopolitical tension, and closer integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA). Real GDP growth averaged about 2.3% a year. In 2025, real GDP grew by 3.5%, the third consecutive year of expansion. Financial services, import and export trade, and tourism supported the recovery, while construction and real estate remained under pressure. Policy changes, including the National Security Law, Article 23 legislation, CEPA enhancements, and GBA-related measures, contributed to a more stable operating environment and increased cross-border economic linkages.

For 2026–2030, the economy is expected to grow by about 3% a year in real terms. This outlook is consistent with the Mainland’s 15th Five-Year Plan and rests on investment in innovation and technology (I&T), development of the Northern Metropolis, and continued external demand. The discussion draws on official data from the Census and Statistics Department (C&SD), the 2026–27 Budget Speech, Hong Kong Economic Reports, IMF assessments, and HKTDC research. It reviews macroeconomic performance, sectoral trends, policy effects, structural constraints, future growth drivers, risks, and policy options for sustaining long-term growth. The central argument is that Hong Kong’s position under “one country, two systems” continues to shape its response to a more uncertain external environment while enabling deeper integration with the Mainland.

## 1. Introduction
Hong Kong’s economy has long been associated with low taxation, a common-law judicial system, a freely convertible currency pegged to the US dollar, and its role as a financial, trading, shipping, and aviation centre. With a population of about 7.5 million and per capita GDP above US$56,000 in 2025, Hong Kong remained highly placed in global measures of economic freedom, although those rankings were increasingly read against a more contested external setting.

The years 2021–2025 followed the disruption caused by the 2019 social unrest and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, when real GDP contracted by about 6.5%. Recovery was uneven. Global supply-chain disruption, US-China strategic competition, higher interest rates, changes in talent flows, and domestic structural issues such as high housing costs and population ageing all constrained growth. By 2025, however, the economy had regained momentum, with real GDP growth of 3.5%, the highest rate since 2021 and the third consecutive year of expansion. That outcome reflected both cyclical recovery and policy efforts to stabilize the domestic environment while strengthening links with the Mainland.

The discussion below reviews macroeconomic performance and sectoral outcomes from 2021 to 2025, then considers policy changes and structural constraints, followed by forecasts, growth drivers, risks, and policy recommendations for 2026–2030. The analysis uses C&SD national accounts, the 2026–27 Budget Speech released in February 2026, quarterly economic reports, and related international assessments.

## 2. Economic Performance in the Past Five Years (2021–2025)

In 2021, output rebounded strongly as global vaccination programmes expanded and border restrictions began to ease. In 2022, the Omicron outbreak and continued controls pushed the economy back into contraction. Growth resumed in 2023 after full reopening and strengthened further in 2024 and 2025, supported by exports and a gradual recovery in domestic demand. Nominal GDP increased from about US$369 billion in 2021 to about US$427 billion in 2025. Unemployment fell from pandemic levels to 3.8% by early 2026, while underemployment also remained low. Inflation stayed moderate, suggesting limited pass-through from external price pressures and only moderate domestic demand pressure.

External trade was the main source of growth. Early data show merchandise exports rising by 15.4% year on year in 2025, led by electronics, AI-related components, and shipments brought forward in anticipation of possible tariff changes. Services exports, especially finance, tourism, and professional services, recovered after 2023 as cross-border movement normalized. Visitor arrivals moved closer to pre-pandemic levels, reaching around 33 million in the first eight months of 2025. Domestic demand also improved in 2025. Private consumption returned to positive growth at about 2.5%, and gross fixed capital formation strengthened as infrastructure projects advanced.

### 2.2 Sectoral Analysis
Services remained dominant, accounting for about 93.5% of GDP over 2023–2025. Sectoral performance, however, varied considerably.

Financing and insurance recorded strong growth, at about 4.8% in 2025 and 4.9% in the fourth quarter. Hong Kong maintained its position in wealth management and green finance, supported by more than 2,700 single-family offices, continued Stock Connect activity, and growth in IPO and green bond business. Cross-border financial links within the GBA added to this momentum.

Import and export, wholesale, and retail trades grew by about 6.0% in 2025. Re-exports of high-value electronics were especially important. Regional supply-chain adjustment, together with RCEP and CEPA-related market access, also supported activity.

Accommodation and food services recovered after border reopening. Higher visitor arrivals supported retail sales, which rose by 11.8% in early 2026. Even so, value added in tourism-related services did not rise smoothly and fell by 0.5% in some quarters because of base effects.

Transportation, storage, postal, and courier services expanded by about 3.7% in 2025, helped by the recovery in aviation and higher port throughput. Information and communications recorded weaker growth, around 0.9%, although fintech, data centres, and digital infrastructure gained policy support under the I&T agenda.

Real estate, professional, and business services contracted mildly, by roughly 0.2% to 0.7% in 2025, reflecting high interest rates and a property-market correction. Residential and commercial prices fell by about 25% to 30% from the 2021 peak before showing some recovery. Construction performed more poorly, declining by about 7.8% to 10.4% in mid-2025 because of project delays, although preparatory work for the Northern Metropolis and other major infrastructure projects continued. Manufacturing remained small, at around 1% of GDP, but still recorded growth of about 0.7% to 3.2% through higher-value activities and closer production links with the GBA.

The overall pattern was clear. External-facing sectors performed better than domestically oriented ones, reflecting Hong Kong’s dependence on trade and services. Weakness in construction and parts of the property-related economy reduced overall growth, but the resilience of major service sectors offset much of that drag.

### 2.3 Key Events, Policy Impacts, and International Comparisons
The National Security Law in 2020 and the Article 23 legislation enacted in 2024 changed the political and legal setting for business. The paper attributes these measures to the restoration of social stability and a firmer business environment, although they were also associated with initial capital outflows. At the same time, GBA integration moved forward under the 2019 Outline Development Plan. Infrastructure such as the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge and the Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Innovation and Technology Cooperation Zone increased cross-border movement of talent, capital, and technology. CEPA upgrades widened mutual recognition and market access in finance and professional services. The 14th Five-Year Plan for 2021–2025 identified Hong Kong as an international financial centre and an I&T centre, and policy results were most visible in RMB internationalization and sustainable finance.

Compared with other economies, Hong Kong’s average growth over this period exceeded that of some advanced economies, including Japan at about 1%, but remained below faster-growing Asian economies such as Vietnam, where annual growth was around 7% to 8%. Talent outflows between 2021 and 2023 reduced labour-market depth in some professions, though inflows from Mainland China through talent schemes, with more than 300,000 visas issued, partly offset those losses. Property-market deleveraging weighed on investment throughout much of the period, with only tentative signs of recovery appearing in late 2025.

Longer-run comparisons with the 2010s suggest slower productivity growth than in Hong Kong’s earlier expansion phase. Sectoral evidence from fintech and green bond issuance points to areas of continuing strength, but the economy remains highly sensitive to shifts in external demand. The reported elasticity of external demand, at roughly 1.5 to 2.0, is consistent with that exposure.

## 3. Challenges and Structural Issues
The recovery did not remove deeper structural constraints. Geopolitical tension remained a persistent source of uncertainty, particularly through US export controls, tariff risks, and broader de-risking strategies. Population ageing and labour shortages continued to affect the economy, even though labour-force participation stabilized and population inflows increased after 2024. High living costs and inequality remained prominent social concerns, including those reflected in the Gini coefficient. Property-market volatility, including a price correction of about 25% to 30% between 2021 and 2025 followed by a partial rebound in 2026, continued to affect household balance sheets, investment sentiment, and fiscal expectations. Climate-related and sustainability pressures also became more significant. More broadly, productivity growth lagged behind that of several regional peers, which points to the need for higher investment in technology, skills, and human capital.

## 4. Prospects for the Next Five Years (2026–2030)

### 4.1 Official Forecasts and Medium-Term Outlook
According to the 2026–27 Budget Speech, real GDP growth in 2026 is forecast at 2.5% to 3.5%, supported by external trade and resilient domestic demand. Underlying inflation is projected at 1.7% and headline inflation at 1.8%. For 2027–2030, the official medium-term projection is average real growth of about 3% a year, with underlying inflation averaging around 2%. Nominal GDP is projected to exceed HK$800 billion by 2030.

Under this outlook, exports remain the main driver of growth. Services are expected to benefit from Mainland tourism, cross-border finance, and continued demand for electronics linked to AI-related supply chains.

### 4.2 Key Drivers and Opportunities
Closer integration with the GBA and alignment with China’s 15th Five-Year Plan for 2026–2030 are expected to shape the next stage of growth. The Northern Metropolis, covering about 30,000 hectares, or roughly one-third of Hong Kong’s land area, is intended to support more than 500,000 jobs in innovation and technology. Within that plan, the Hetao zone is designed for research and development, while the San Tin Technopole is expected to support pilot production and related commercial activity. The “one zone, two parks” model in Hetao is intended to formalize collaboration across the Hong Kong-Shenzhen boundary.

Innovation and technology policy is likely to remain central. OASES has reportedly attracted more than 100 strategic enterprises, with planned investment of HK$60 billion and about 22,000 jobs. Tax measures for family offices, funds, and research and development are intended to support AI, biotechnology, fintech, and green technology. Planned expansion in computing capacity, including the Sandy Ridge data centre, is expected to raise capacity thirty-six fold by 2032.

Talent policy has also shifted. The Top Talent Pass Scheme and related immigration changes were intended to reverse earlier population losses, and inflows from the Mainland have added both labour supply and local demand. Sustainable finance remains another area of opportunity, especially in green financing, Belt and Road-related capital raising, and ESG-related financial services. Tourism and consumption may recover further if annual visitor numbers move above 50 million through major events and improved connectivity. Infrastructure projects, including the Northern Metropolis, the high-speed rail network, and the phased development associated with Lantau Tomorrow Vision, could also support medium-term demand and capacity.

Hong Kong’s role as an intermediary between Mainland China and international markets continues to depend on familiar institutional features: common law, free-port status, and the widespread use of English in business and finance. In policy terms, these features are expected to support stronger links with RCEP and CEPA markets as well as a wider range of economies in the Global South.

### 4.3 Quantitative Projections and Scenario Analysis
Under a base-case assumption of 3% average annual real growth, nominal GDP could reach about HK$4.5 trillion to HK$5 trillion by 2030. A stronger outcome, driven by faster I&T growth and more effective GBA integration, could add about 0.5 to 1 percentage point to annual growth. Under a weaker scenario, where geopolitical conflict intensifies and external conditions deteriorate, growth could remain closer to 2%.

## 5. Risks, Challenges, and Policy Recommendations
The medium-term outlook remains exposed to several risks. A more confrontational geopolitical environment, changes in US policy, and wider protectionism could weaken trade and investment. Slower Mainland growth, projected at an average of about 4.5%, would also reduce external support. Cybersecurity threats, climate-related disruption, and demographic pressures remain relevant. Without reform, potential growth may stay near 2.5%. The property market is another source of uncertainty, especially if interest rates remain high and recovery loses momentum.

Several policy responses follow from these constraints. Regulatory and professional rule convergence with the GBA could be accelerated, especially in mutual recognition of qualifications and cross-border data use within security frameworks. Public and private spending on education and research could move toward 3% of GDP, with stronger institutional support for I&T development in the Northern Metropolis. Economic diversification also remains necessary. The most plausible areas include creative industries, services linked to an ageing population, and high-end manufacturing under re-industrialization policies. In finance, stronger ESG disclosure and continued green bond development would help maintain access to sustainable capital. Talent policy still depends on housing and urban conditions, so immigration measures need to be matched by housing supply, including the target of 196,000 public units, and by broader improvements in living conditions. Digital policy will also matter. Data centres, Web3-related activity, and AI applications are likely to remain on the agenda, subject to national security requirements and fiscal capacity under the 2026–27 Budget, which allocated HK$843 billion in expenditure, with about 60% recurrent spending on health, education, and welfare.

The forecast fiscal surplus of HK$22.1 billion in 2026 gives the government some room for targeted investment, although medium-term spending commitments will still require prioritization.

## 6. Conclusion
Between 2021 and 2025, Hong Kong moved from pandemic-era instability to a more stable recovery led largely by exports and services. That recovery rested on both cyclical reopening and policy efforts to restore stability and deepen integration with the Mainland. The period from 2026 to 2030 is likely to depend less on simple reopening effects and more on whether Hong Kong can raise productivity through innovation and technology, use GBA integration more effectively, and deliver large infrastructure projects without major delay.

The outlook remains favourable but conditional. Continued openness, deeper national integration, and institutional credibility remain central advantages, but structural weaknesses have not disappeared. Labour-market pressures, property-market volatility, external political risk, and slower productivity growth will continue to shape outcomes. If the policies set out in the 2026–27 Budget and the 15th Five-Year Plan are implemented effectively, Hong Kong is likely to retain its position as a leading financial centre in Asia while expanding its role in innovation and technology.
