### Economic Development of Hong Kong: Retrospective and Outlook (2020-2029)

### Abstract

This paper reviews the economic development of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region over 2020-2024 and assesses prospects for 2025-2029. Examining macroeconomic indicators, industrial restructuring, trade and investment, labor markets, and financial markets, it finds that the economy entered a recovery phase in 2023-2025 after the combined shocks of the 2019 social unrest and the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020-2022. Although geopolitical tensions and rising trade protectionism continue to weigh on the external environment, Hong Kong retains support from the institutional framework of "One Country, Two Systems," its role as a "super connector" between Mainland China and global markets, and domestic growth drivers including Northern Metropolis development and the innovation and technology sector. On this basis, the paper expects average annual growth of 2.5%-3.5% in 2025-2029, with artificial intelligence, green finance, high-end professional services, and deeper integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area as the main areas of development.

**Keywords:** Hong Kong economy; Greater Bay Area; Northern Metropolis; IPO market; talent policy

### Chapter 1: Introduction

As an international financial center, trade hub, and shipping center, Hong Kong has long occupied an important place in academic and policy discussions on economic development. The period from 2020 to 2029 is historically significant for Hong Kong because it covers both a difficult opening to the decade and an important phase of economic repositioning under the support of China's 14th Five-Year Plan.

The five years from 2020 to 2024 exposed Hong Kong's economy to several major shocks: the after-effects of the 2019 social unrest, the global COVID-19 pandemic from 2020 to 2022, and the resulting supply-chain disruption and cross-border travel restrictions. World Bank data show GDP growth of -6.54% in 2020, 6.45% in 2021, and -3.68% in 2022. These movements illustrate both the economy's vulnerability to external shocks and its capacity for recovery.

During the same period, the HKSAR government introduced a series of measures aimed at economic stabilization and livelihood support. In February 2024, the government announced the full removal of property-market "cooling measures," including Buyer's Stamp Duty for non-permanent residents, New Residential Stamp Duty for second-time buyers, and Special Stamp Duty for short-term resale. This decision released part of the suppressed housing demand. In 2025, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange introduced a further set of IPO reforms, including changes to price discovery mechanisms and lower public float requirements for large listings, altering Hong Kong's position in the Asian listing market.

The next five years, from 2025 to 2029, will unfold in a domestic and external environment that remains unsettled. Externally, geopolitical tensions persist, trade protectionism has become more prominent, and changes in US-China relations affect Hong Kong directly because of its high degree of external orientation. The ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) projects Hong Kong's economic growth at 2.1% in 2025 and 1.9% in 2026. At the same time, domestic policy is oriented toward new sources of growth, including the Northern Metropolis, the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop, and several talent-admission schemes.

This paper aims to provide a comprehensive review of Hong Kong's economic performance over the past five years through systematic analysis of the available data, followed by an assessment of current conditions and a projection for the next five years based on existing policies and macroeconomic trends. The objective is to offer a reference for further research and policy discussion.

### Chapter 2: Retrospective of Economic Development (2020-2024)

#### 2.1 Macroeconomic Performance: From Recession to Recovery

Over the past five years, Hong Kong's economy followed a clear V-shaped path. According to World Bank data, GDP growth rates from 2019 to 2024 were -1.67% (2019), -6.54% (2020), 6.45% (2021), -3.68% (2022), 3.22% (2023), and 2.50% (2024). This trajectory shows both Hong Kong's sensitivity to external disturbances and the speed with which activity can recover once constraints ease.

**2020-2021: Pandemic impact and rebound**

In 2020, the global spread of COVID-19 caused a sharp contraction in an economy heavily dependent on cross-border travel and trade. GDP fell by 6.54%, making 2020 one of the deepest downturns since records began in 1962 and worse than the -5.9% contraction recorded during the 1998 Asian Financial Crisis. The unemployment rate rose to 5.81% in 2020, the highest level in 15 years. Private consumption expenditure declined sharply under social-distancing rules and border controls. Visitor arrivals fell from 55.9 million in 2019 to about 3.57 million in 2020, a drop of more than 93%.

In 2021, output rebounded by 6.45%, supported by the low base in the previous year and by the recovery of the global economy. The main contribution came from goods exports, which benefited from strong international demand for medical supplies, electronic products, and home-office equipment. Goods exports increased by 26.3%, while service exports began to recover as local epidemic conditions improved.

**2022-2023: Recurrent pandemic waves and gradual recovery**

Hong Kong faced a severe fifth wave of COVID-19 in early 2022. The government responded with strict social-distancing measures and dynamic zero-COVID policies, and GDP contracted by 4.0% in the first quarter. For the year as a whole, the economy shrank by 3.68%, with private consumption expenditure falling by 1.8% and fixed capital investment by 7.8%. The unemployment rate rose to 4.32% in 2022.

A clearer recovery began in 2023. Cross-boundary travel with the Mainland resumed fully on February 6, and restrictions on inbound overseas travelers were removed. GDP grew by 3.22% for the year, supported mainly by a recovery in service exports, which rose by 21.2%, and by a 7.4% increase in private consumption expenditure. Visitor arrivals rose to 34 million in 2023. This was still only about 60% of the pre-pandemic peak, but it represented a large improvement from the 600,000 arrivals recorded in 2022.

**2024: Steady growth and structural adjustment**

In 2024, the recovery continued, with full-year GDP growth of 2.6%. External demand was constrained by geopolitical tensions and tighter monetary conditions in major economies, but domestic demand improved further. Private consumption expenditure rose by 1.2%, and fixed capital investment returned to growth, increasing by 1.8%. The labor market remained tight, with the unemployment rate edging down from 2.95% in 2023 to 2.94% in 2024, close to full-employment conditions.

Measured in nominal terms, GDP rose from USD 363.1 billion in 2019 to USD 406.9 billion in 2024, while per capita GDP increased from USD 48,359 to USD 54,075. By this measure, output exceeded its pre-pandemic level.

#### 2.2 Industrial Structure Evolution: Service Sector Dominance and I&T Breakthrough

Hong Kong remains an economy in which services account for more than 93% of GDP. Over the past five years, several structural changes have become visible within that service-led framework.

**Consolidation and upgrading of financial services**

Financial services remained a core pillar of the economy, accounting for about 22% of GDP. Several segments recorded notable expansion:

- **Offshore RMB business:** Hong Kong retained its position as the world's largest offshore RMB business center. By the end of 2024, RMB deposits in Hong Kong exceeded RMB 1 trillion, and average daily turnover in the RMB Real Time Gross Settlement system exceeded RMB 1.8 trillion.
- **Asset and wealth management:** According to Securities and Futures Commission data, Hong Kong's asset and wealth management business reached HKD 35.5 trillion by the end of 2023, about 42% higher than in 2019.
- **IPO market changes:** In 2024, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange revised the Chapter 18C listing regime for specialist technology companies and launched the TECH (Technology Enterprises Channel) platform. In 2025, the IPO market expanded sharply. Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. (CATL) raised USD 5.3 billion in the largest global IPO since 2023, and total IPO fundraising reached USD 22 billion, returning Hong Kong to first place globally.

**Post-pandemic reconstruction of tourism and retail**

Tourism and retail were among the sectors most severely affected by the pandemic. In 2019, tourism contributed about 4.5% of Hong Kong's GDP and directly employed more than 250,000 people. During the pandemic, the total value of retail sales fell from HKD 431.1 billion in 2019 to HKD 326.5 billion in 2020, a decline of 24.3%.

After the full resumption of cross-boundary travel in 2023, tourism began to recover. Visitor arrivals reached 45 million in 2024, or about 75% of the 2019 level. In the first quarter of 2025, arrivals recovered further to 85% of the pre-pandemic level, and retail sales value rose by 12.8% year on year.

**Acceleration of innovation and technology industries**

The past five years marked a transition in Hong Kong's innovation and technology sector from policy ambition toward project implementation. The SAR government has treated I&T as a new engine of diversification and has allocated substantial public resources to it.

- **Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop:** On December 22, 2025, the Hong Kong Loop, formally the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park, officially opened. More than 60 companies had already signed agreements to locate there, mainly in artificial intelligence, biomedicine, and new materials.
- **Northern Metropolis development:** The year 2025 was presented as a breakthrough year for the Northern Metropolis. Large projects, including the San Tin Technopole and the Hung Shui Kiu/Ha Tsuen New Development Area, accelerated. Once fully operational, the San Tin Technopole is expected to contribute at least HKD 250 billion annually to GDP and to provide more than 300,000 jobs.
- **InnoHK research clusters:** Hong Kong established a number of international research centers in areas such as health technology, artificial intelligence, and robotics. The 2025-26 Budget allocated HKD 1 billion for the establishment of the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute.

#### 2.3 Foreign Trade and Investment: Resilience and Transformation

As a free port and separate customs territory, Hong Kong remains highly dependent on external trade. Over the past five years, the trade model has moved gradually away from traditional re-export intermediation toward higher value-added trade services.

**Fluctuations and adaptation in goods trade**

World Bank data show that goods exports increased from USD 543.1 billion in 2019 to USD 631.1 billion in 2024, while goods imports rose from USD 558.4 billion to USD 633.1 billion over the same period. The goods trade deficit narrowed from USD 15.3 billion in 2019 to USD 2 billion in 2024.

These aggregate figures also reflect structural change. As Mainland supply chains upgraded and direct export capacity expanded, part of Hong Kong's traditional re-export role came under pressure. In response, Hong Kong reinforced its position as an international trade hub through supply-chain management, trade finance, quality inspection, and other higher value-added services.

**Growth in service trade**

Service trade remains Hong Kong's most competitive external sector. Between 2019 and 2024, service exports continued to expand, especially in financial services, professional services, and logistics. As Mainland-Hong Kong financial-connectivity mechanisms such as Stock Connect, Bond Connect, and Cross-boundary Wealth Management Connect deepened, cross-border financial services became a more important source of growth.

**Foreign direct investment inflows**

Hong Kong remained one of the world's major destinations for foreign direct investment. According to World Bank data, net FDI inflows increased from USD 58.3 billion in 2019 to USD 125.8 billion in 2024, an increase of more than 116%. This rise indicates that investors continued to assign value to Hong Kong's position as an entry point to the Mainland market.

#### 2.4 Labor Market: From High Unemployment to Talent Competition

**A V-shaped reversal in the labor market**

Labor-market conditions changed sharply over the past five years. The unemployment rate rose from 2.92% in 2019 to 5.81% in 2020, the highest level in 15 years. The service sector absorbed much of the shock, with heavy job losses in catering, retail, and tourism.

As output recovered, the labor market improved quickly. The unemployment rate fell to 2.95% in 2023 and to 2.99% in 2024, close to the estimated natural rate. The underemployment rate fell from 3.4% in 2020 to 1.1% in 2024.

**Wages and income**

Even as the labor market tightened, wage growth remained moderate. Between 2020 and 2024, the nominal wage index grew by about 2.5% per year on average, slightly above average inflation of 2.1%. Median monthly household income increased from HKD 29,500 in 2019 to HKD 30,500 in 2024, implying only limited improvement in real purchasing power.

**A major shift in talent policy**

In response to population aging, outflow of local talent, and the demands of industrial upgrading, the HKSAR government introduced a series of talent-admission measures in 2022 and 2023:

- **Top Talent Pass Scheme (TTPS):** Launched at the end of 2022, the scheme targets high-income individuals with annual salaries above HKD 2.5 million and graduates of the world's top 100 universities. By the end of 2025, it had received more than 70,000 applications and approved about 55,000, for an approval rate of 88.2%.
- **Quality Migrant Admission Scheme (QMAS):** Quota limits were removed and procedures simplified. In 2025, the scheme received 18,707 applications and approved 7,101, with a renewal approval rate of 97.3%.
- **Admission Scheme for Mainland Talents and Professionals (ASMTP):** Sectoral restrictions were relaxed and approvals accelerated. In 2025, 26,714 applications were approved, and the renewal approval rate exceeded 93%.
- **Talent List:** Updated in March 2025, the list expanded to 60 professions, with 9 new additions including accountants, patent professionals, and aircraft maintenance engineers.

According to SAR government statistics, the various talent schemes attracted more than 230,000 people to Hong Kong between the end of 2022 and the end of 2025. This eased shortages in some professional occupations.

#### 2.5 Property Market: From Bubble Adjustment to Steady Recovery

Over the past five years, Hong Kong's property market moved from the strictest phase of cooling measures to their complete removal, alongside a prolonged and deep price correction.

**Price adjustment and market clearing**

According to Rating and Valuation Department data, the private domestic price index reached a historical peak in August 2019 and then entered a four-year downturn. By the end of 2024, prices had fallen by about 25% from the peak, the longest and deepest correction since 1998. In 2024 alone, prices declined by 7.1% year on year, or 8.4% after adjusting for inflation.

Several factors contributed to this correction:

- Aggressive Federal Reserve rate increases pushed up Hong Kong interest rates and mortgage costs.
- The local economy was weakened by the pandemic, reducing purchasing power.
- Population outflow weakened rental demand.
- Supply increased, with 24,261 private residential units completed in 2024, up 75.1% year on year.

**Policy shift and market recovery**

On February 28, 2024, Financial Secretary Paul Chan announced the full removal of the property-market cooling measures that had been in place for more than a decade, including:

- Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD), which targeted non-permanent residents.
- New Residential Stamp Duty (NRSD), which targeted multiple-property owners.
- Special Stamp Duty (SSD), which targeted short-term resale.

The market responded quickly. In the first ten months of 2025, residential transaction volume reached 51,361 deals, up 20.3% year on year and the highest level in four years. Prices appeared to bottom out by mid-2025 and rose by about 3% over the year. The share of Mainland buyers increased from around 18% in 2024 to 22%-24% in 2025, and exceeded 60% in the luxury segment.

In 2025, the government made further adjustments to housing-related policy. Under the New Capital Investment Entrant Scheme (CIES), the threshold for residential properties was lowered from HKD 50 million to HKD 30 million, and the maximum amount countable toward the investment requirement was raised from HKD 10 million to HKD 15 million.

Even after the correction, Hong Kong remained the least affordable housing market in the world. The Demographia International Housing Affordability Survey reported that in 2024 the median house price was 14.4 times median household income, making Hong Kong the world's least affordable city for the 14th consecutive year, although this was an improvement from 18.8 times in 2022.

#### 2.6 Financial Markets and IPO Ecosystem Reshaping

Over the past five years, Hong Kong's financial markets moved through a period of weakness followed by institutional adaptation. The Hang Seng Index was highly volatile from 2020 to 2024 and fell below 15,000 points in 2022, its lowest level in 13 years. Even so, market institutions continued to evolve.

**Deepening of listing-system reform**

The 2018 listing reform permitted companies with weighted voting rights and pre-revenue biotech firms to list in Hong Kong, broadening the market's sectoral base. Chapter 18C, introduced in 2023, further relaxed entry requirements for specialist technology companies. The 2025 reforms focused on price discovery and liquidity:

- At least 40% of IPO shares were required to be allocated to institutional bookbuilding tranches, so that pricing would be led by institutional investors.
- Public subscription tranches could adopt either "Clawback Mechanism A," with an initial allocation of 5% and a clawback up to 35% based on demand, or "Clawback Mechanism B," with a fixed allocation of 10%-60%.
- Cornerstone investor subscriptions were capped at 50%-55% to limit concentration.
- Free-float requirements were introduced to ensure adequate liquidity at listing.

**Strong expansion of the 2025 IPO market**

Supported by these reforms and by a wave of "A+H" listings from Mainland firms, Hong Kong's IPO market expanded substantially in 2025. Total fundraising reached USD 22 billion, more than 200% higher than a year earlier, placing Hong Kong first globally.

Major IPOs included:

- Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. (CATL): USD 5.3 billion, the world's largest IPO since 2023.
- Hengrui Pharmaceuticals: about USD 2 billion.
- Haitian Flavouring and Food: about USD 1.5 billion.
- Sanhua Intelligent Controls: about USD 1.2 billion.

These four deals accounted for four of the ten largest IPOs globally in 2025.

**Expansion of mutual market-access mechanisms**

In 2024, the Shanghai-Hong Kong and Shenzhen-Hong Kong Stock Connect programs reached their tenth anniversary. In 2025, Southbound Trading was expanded to include eligible international companies listed in Hong Kong. This change increased Hong Kong's incentive to attract international issuers with sufficient market quality and scale.

### Chapter 3: Analysis of Current Economic Situation (2025)

#### 3.1 Macroeconomic Situation: Steady Expansion

In 2025, Hong Kong's economy continued to recover at a moderate pace. Census and Statistics Department data show real GDP growth of 3.1% year on year in both the first and second quarters, marking ten consecutive quarters of positive growth. The official full-year forecast remained at 2%-3%.

**Growth driver analysis:**

- **External demand:** Goods exports rose by 8.4% in real terms in the first quarter of 2025, supported by strong global demand for AI-related electronic products and by front-loading of trade activity. Pressure increased in the second half of the year as US protectionist measures weighed on trade.
- **Domestic demand:** Private consumption expenditure declined slightly by 0.5% in the first quarter. This reflected changing consumption patterns and the rise in northbound consumption by Hong Kong residents in Mainland cities. As employment income continued to improve, with nominal income of employed persons rising by 4.2% year on year in the first quarter of 2025, and as consumption-support measures such as "Happy Hong Kong" events were rolled out, consumer confidence improved gradually.
- **Investment expenditure:** Overall investment returned to growth, rising by 4.1% in real terms in the first quarter. The increase was driven mainly by spending on machinery, equipment, and intellectual property products. Large infrastructure projects, including the San Tin Technopole and the Northern Metropolis, continued to advance.

#### 3.2 Inflation and Monetary Policy Environment

Inflation remained low and stable in 2025. The underlying Composite Consumer Price Index, excluding one-off government relief measures, rose by 1.2% year on year in the first quarter and 1.1% in the second quarter. The full-year forecast for underlying inflation remained at 1.5%, with headline inflation projected at 1.8%.

Hong Kong's monetary conditions continued to be shaped by the linked exchange rate system, under which local interest rates broadly follow US policy. In 2025, as the United States entered a rate-cutting cycle and the Federal Funds Rate moved down from earlier highs, Hong Kong's Prime Rate also declined. Lower borrowing costs provided some support to the property and equity markets and reduced financing pressure on firms and households.

#### 3.3 Labor Market: Full Employment and Structural Shortages

Hong Kong's labor market remained tight in 2025, with the unemployment rate holding at around 3%, close to full employment. At the same time, structural shortages became more visible, especially in innovation and technology, healthcare, aviation, and construction, where new industries and major infrastructure projects were competing for labor.

The SAR government continued to revise talent policies:

- The Talent List was expanded to 60 professions in March 2025.
- The Technical Professional Stream was launched in June 2025, allowing non-degree technical professionals to work in Hong Kong in eight sectors, including new industrialization, nursing, and aircraft maintenance.
- Enhanced measures for the Technology Talent Admission Scheme (TechTAS) were introduced in December 2025, simplifying procedures and removing the requirement that applicants be engaged in research and development within 14 designated technology areas.

#### 3.4 Financial Market Prosperity and Challenges

The year 2025 was a strong one for Hong Kong's financial markets. The Hang Seng Index rose materially, supported by the IPO recovery and by policy developments on the Mainland. IPO fundraising reached USD 22 billion, returning Hong Kong to the top position globally.

At the same time, several constraints remained:

- **Geopolitical risks:** US-China tensions continued to weigh on the valuation of China-related equities.
- **Liquidity concerns:** Despite a more active IPO market, overall turnover remained below past peaks.
- **Talent competition:** Other financial centers, especially Singapore, continued to use preferential policies to attract skilled financial professionals.

### Chapter 4: Economic Outlook for the Next Five Years (2025-2029)

#### 4.1 Growth Forecasts and Scenario Analysis

Looking ahead to 2025-2029, Hong Kong's growth path will be shaped by both external constraints and domestic restructuring. Based on the projections of several institutions and on an assessment of internal growth drivers, average annual real GDP growth is expected to fall within a range of 2.5%-3.5%.

**Baseline scenario (probability 60%): Moderate recovery**

Under assumptions of moderate global expansion, US-China relations characterized by competition without full decoupling, and Mainland growth of 4%-5%, Hong Kong could achieve annual growth of 2.8%-3.2%. The main drivers would be:

- Northern Metropolis construction entering its peak investment phase, with infrastructure investment growing by 5%-8% per year.
- Innovation and technology industries moving toward commercialization, raising their contribution to GDP from roughly 1.5% at present to 3%.
- Deeper financial connectivity, with asset and wealth management expanding at about 6% annually.
- Tourism recovering fully to the 2019 level of 55.9 million arrivals and then continuing to expand.

**Optimistic scenario (probability 25%): Faster integration**

If US-China relations were to ease and integration between Hong Kong and the Mainland, especially within the Greater Bay Area, were to deepen more rapidly, annual growth could rise to 3.5%-4.5%. This scenario would depend on:

- Much freer cross-boundary flow of capital, people, and data within the Greater Bay Area.
- Hong Kong becoming the preferred headquarters base for Mainland firms expanding abroad.
- Larger capital inflows to Hong Kong driven by safe-haven demand.

**Pessimistic scenario (probability 15%): External shocks**

If the global economy entered recession and geopolitical conflict intensified, Hong Kong's growth could slow to 1%-2% per year, with individual years possibly returning to negative growth. The main risks would be:

- A large increase in US tariffs on China, with spillovers to Hong Kong's entrepôt trade.
- Tighter global financial conditions, placing pressure on Hong Kong's property and equity markets.
- Further decoupling by major economies, weakening Hong Kong's intermediary role.

#### 4.2 Structural Growth Drivers

**4.2.1 Northern Metropolis and innovation and technology industries**

The Northern Metropolis, covering about 300 square kilometers, or roughly one-third of Hong Kong's total area, is expected to be the most important territorial and industrial development project affecting growth over the next five years.

**Key milestones:**

- **2026:** Hung Shui Kiu Industrial Estate begins operation, and construction starts on Hung Shui Kiu Station on the Tuen Ma Line.
- **2027:** All major development projects begin land-resumption procedures.
- **2028:** The first batch of residents moves into public housing in the Northern Metropolis.
- **2032:** Land formation is completed for 40% of the new development land, and 40% of the additional housing units are completed.

**Economic contribution forecast:** The San Tin Technopole alone is expected to contribute at least HKD 250 billion annually to GDP once fully operational. Including the Lok Ma Chau Loop, the Hung Shui Kiu logistics hub, and related projects, the Northern Metropolis as a whole could account for 15%-20% of Hong Kong's GDP by around 2030 and create 500,000 jobs.

**Specific I&T development directions:**

- **Artificial intelligence:** The Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute, backed by HKD 1 billion, is intended to support collaboration among industry, universities, and research institutions.
- **Life and health technology:** Hong Kong can use the Chapter 18A listing regime and the research strengths of HKU, CUHK, and HKUST to expand biomedical industries.
- **New energy technology:** In line with national carbon-reduction objectives, Hong Kong is expected to develop both green finance and green technology.
- **Advanced manufacturing:** The "Manufacturing+" scheme is intended to support the intelligent upgrading of local production lines.

**4.2.2 Financial-services upgrading**

Hong Kong's financial sector is expected to shift from a traditional fundraising center toward a broader role in asset allocation and risk management:

- **Green finance:** Hong Kong has already become Asia's largest green bond issuance center. Green and sustainable bond issuance is expected to grow by 20% annually over the next five years and to exceed USD 500 billion by 2029.
- **Virtual assets:** A virtual-asset ecosystem is likely to develop within a regulated framework, including stablecoins and virtual-asset ETFs.
- **Offshore RMB business:** As RMB internationalization advances, offshore RMB deposits in Hong Kong are expected to rise from about RMB 1 trillion to RMB 2 trillion by 2029, while the RMB share of trade settlement could increase to 40%.
- **Insurance and risk management:** Hong Kong may use its institutional position under "One Country, Two Systems" to expand specialty-risk businesses such as catastrophe bonds and cyber insurance.

**4.2.3 High-end professional services and trade**

Hong Kong is also expected to reinforce its role as an Asia-Pacific center for legal and dispute-resolution services, an international shipping center, and an international aviation hub:

- **Legal and dispute-resolution services:** Supported by the 14th Five-Year Plan, Hong Kong can expand international commercial arbitration and mediation and attract firms from Belt and Road countries to use Hong Kong as a dispute-resolution venue.
- **High-value-added supply-chain management:** The economy is likely to continue shifting from traditional entrepôt trade toward supply-chain management, quality inspection, brand management, and higher value-added logistics.
- **Aviation industry:** With the full operation of the Three-Runway System, Hong Kong International Airport's annual capacity is expected to rise to 120 million passengers and 10 million tonnes of cargo, reinforcing its position as the world's largest international cargo airport.

**4.2.4 Talent and population strategy**

Population aging will remain a structural constraint. The share of the population aged 65 or above is expected to rise from about 20% in 2024 to about 25% by 2029. Talent policy over the next five years is therefore likely to emphasize:

- **Targeted recruitment:** Continued revision of the Talent List, with emphasis on innovation and technology, healthcare, education, and finance.
- **Local training:** Expanded STEM education and cooperation with firms in the Northern Metropolis to develop applied talent.
- **Regional collaboration:** Greater mutual recognition of professional qualifications in the Greater Bay Area, allowing Hong Kong and Macao professionals to practice more easily in Mainland GBA cities.
- **Retention measures:** Better renewal arrangements and stronger housing, education, and healthcare support, with the aim of keeping talent renewal rates above 85%.

#### 4.3 Risks and Challenges

**4.3.1 External risks**

- **Geopolitics and trade protectionism:** Uncertainty over US tariff policy toward China remains the largest external risk. A substantial rise in tariffs would affect Hong Kong first as a trade intermediary. Market diversification toward ASEAN, the Middle East, Central Asia, and other Global South markets is therefore increasingly important.
- **Global financial conditions:** Uncertainty in US monetary policy may continue to affect Hong Kong's interest-rate environment and asset prices. If US inflation were to reaccelerate and trigger renewed rate hikes, adjustment pressure on Hong Kong's property and equity markets would increase.
- **Mainland economic slowdown:** Slower growth on the Mainland would affect Hong Kong's service exports and capital inflows. This increases the importance of strengthening Hong Kong's own competitive advantages and reducing excessive dependence on Mainland cyclical conditions.

**4.3.2 Internal challenges**

- **Costs of industrial adjustment:** Traditional sectors such as retail and catering remain under pressure from changing consumption habits, including northbound spending and the spread of online shopping. Adjustment will take time.
- **Living costs and the attractiveness of the city:** Even after the property-market correction, Hong Kong remains one of the world's most expensive cities. Sustaining attractiveness for younger workers while preserving market stability will remain difficult.
- **Social integration:** Larger inflows of new talent require time to integrate into local society. Policymakers will need to balance talent admission with concerns about local employment opportunities.

**4.3.3 Structural risks**

- **Pressure on the linked exchange rate system:** If interest-rate differentials between the United States and China remain wide for a prolonged period, pressure on the linked exchange rate system could intensify. Hong Kong holds more than USD 400 billion in foreign-exchange reserves, but extreme market conditions would still require close monitoring.
- **Industrial concentration risk:** Despite diversification efforts, the financial sector still accounts for more than 20% of GDP. This leaves the economy exposed to fluctuations in global financial markets.

#### 4.4 Policy Recommendations

Based on the analysis above, the following policy directions merit consideration.

**4.4.1 Deepen regional integration and expand the economic hinterland**

- Accelerate the "Twin Cities, Three Circles" strategy linking the Northern Metropolis with Shenzhen's Qianhai, with emphasis on infrastructure connectivity, industrial coordination, and freer factor mobility.
- Seek more early and pilot implementation measures for the Lok Ma Chau Loop, including cross-border data-flow arrangements and simplified immigration procedures for research personnel.
- Promote the Greater Bay Area "one-hour living circle" to improve the convenience of cross-boundary work and residence.

**4.4.2 Optimize the industrial structure and cultivate new growth points**

- Treat artificial intelligence as a strategic emerging industry by increasing research investment and widening application scenarios.
- Develop the low-altitude economy and improve the regulatory framework for drone delivery, air taxis, and related transport modes.
- Support digital transformation in traditional professional services, including legal, accounting, and construction, to improve productivity.

**4.4.3 Improve talent policy and strengthen urban attractiveness**

- Revise TTPS renewal conditions so that recognized entrepreneurial and self-employment arrangements can satisfy the scheme's requirements.
- Increase the supply of subsidized housing and consider rent-to-buy arrangements for incoming talent.
- Expand the supply of international school places to reduce education-related barriers for migrant households.
- Strengthen vocational retraining so that local workers can adjust more effectively to industrial restructuring.

**4.4.4 Strengthen financial infrastructure and market resilience**

- Refine the listing regime so that it balances institutional and retail participation and reduces unnecessary volatility.
- Develop RMB derivatives markets to improve firms' capacity to manage exchange-rate risk.
- Deepen cross-border regulatory cooperation to reduce systemic financial risk.

**4.4.5 Improve livelihoods and support inclusive growth**

- Accelerate public-housing construction and shorten waiting times.
- Improve the MPF system to strengthen retirement protection.
- Make measures such as the Working Family Allowance more targeted so that they better support lower-income households.

### Chapter 5: Conclusion

From 2020 to 2024, Hong Kong's economy moved from sharp contraction to recovery. GDP growth shifted from -6.54% in 2020 to 2.6% in 2024, and both total GDP and per capita GDP rose above pre-pandemic levels. This recovery reflected a combination of policy intervention, including the removal of cooling measures and the introduction of talent schemes, and the continued role of Hong Kong as an international financial and trade center.

The outlook for 2025-2029 is positive but conditional. Northern Metropolis development is likely to reshape both the urban economy and the geography of production. Innovation and technology are moving from the project stage toward industrial activity. Financial services are broadening into green finance and virtual assets, while talent policies are intended to address labor shortages and support sectoral upgrading.

On current assumptions, Hong Kong can maintain annual growth of 2.5%-3.5% over 2025-2029, together with further industrial adjustment and deeper integration with the Greater Bay Area. That outlook remains subject to geopolitical risk, the costs of industrial transition, and demographic aging. Policy will need to address these issues alongside growth objectives.

Hong Kong's position is often summarized as "backed by the motherland, connected to the world." Under "One Country, Two Systems," it continues to serve both as a window for China's high-level opening and as a gateway for international capital entering the Mainland market. Whether that position yields sustained growth over the next five years will depend on execution in infrastructure, sectoral upgrading, talent retention, and the preservation of institutions that remain compatible with international standards.
