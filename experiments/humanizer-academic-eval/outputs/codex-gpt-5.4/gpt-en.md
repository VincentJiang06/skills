# Hong Kong’s Economic Development in the Past Five Years and Its Prospects for the Next Five Years (2021–2025; 2026–2030)

### Abstract

From the standpoint of April 2026, Hong Kong’s economic path over 2021–2025 was not a simple post-pandemic recovery. It was a period of repeated shocks followed by structural reconfiguration. Real GDP grew by 6.4% in 2021, contracted by 3.5% in 2022, recovered by 3.2% in 2023, expanded by 2.5% in 2024, and accelerated to 3.5% in 2025. Over the same period, unemployment fell sharply from its pandemic peak, but the recovery remained uneven. The economy moved from border restrictions and a collapse in tourism to renewed growth in finance, services exports, inbound travel, and technology-related activity. At the same time, commercial real estate remained weak, fiscal revenue from land stayed subdued, and demographic pressures became more visible. Hong Kong therefore did not simply return to its earlier model; it began to search for a different one.

For 2026–2030, moderate growth is a more plausible expectation than rapid expansion. The HKSAR Government forecasts real GDP growth of 2.5% to 3.5% in 2026 and average growth of about 3% a year in 2027–2030, while the IMF projects medium-term growth closer to 2.5% because of rapid population ageing and slower capital accumulation. The central question for the next five years is not whether Hong Kong can reproduce the property-led and retail-led boom of earlier decades. It is whether the city can consolidate a new model based on international finance, offshore RMB business, cross-border wealth management, high-end services, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, and deeper integration with the Greater Bay Area.

### Introduction

Using 2021–2025 as the past five years and 2026–2030 as the next five years provides a clear analytical frame. Within that frame, Hong Kong’s recent economic record has three phases. The first was the rebound in 2021, driven mainly by exports and policy support rather than a full revival in local services. The second was the setback in 2022, when a major local COVID outbreak and tighter global financial conditions pushed the economy back into contraction. The third was the reopening period from 2023 to 2025, when tourism, cross-border mobility, external trade, and capital-market sentiment improved step by step. This sequence matters because the stronger data in 2025 should not be read as a full restoration of the earlier growth regime. They indicate a transition toward a new economic structure that remains incomplete.

### I. A Stage-by-Stage Review of 2021–2025

In 2021, Hong Kong recorded a strong cyclical rebound, but the recovery was narrow. Real GDP expanded by 6.4%, reversing the declines of the previous two years, yet the economy was still about 2% smaller than in 2018. Labour-market conditions improved from the worst pandemic levels, but the annual unemployment rate still averaged 5.2%. Tourism was almost absent: full-year visitor arrivals were only about 91,000. The point is straightforward. The rebound in 2021 was driven by external demand and base effects, not by a normalized recovery in domestic demand, mobility, or the visitor economy.

That narrow recovery was exposed in 2022. Real GDP fell by 3.5%, private consumption declined by 1.1%, and gross domestic fixed capital formation dropped by 8.5%. Hong Kong was hit at the same time by the fifth wave of COVID, continued mobility constraints, and the spillover effects of aggressive US monetary tightening under the Linked Exchange Rate System. Tourist arrivals recovered only marginally, to about 600,000 for the year. In practical terms, 2022 showed how exposed Hong Kong still was to disruptions in border flows, service-sector activity, and global financial conditions.

The turning point came in 2023, the first full year after reopening. Real GDP grew by 3.2%, and private consumption expenditure rose by 7.4%, pointing to a strong rebound in household activity and service demand. Full-year visitor arrivals recovered to 34 million, and tourism’s value added returned to 2.6% of GDP. The recovery, however, was uneven. Goods exports remained soft in real terms, so the fastest repair occurred in mobility, travel, and face-to-face services rather than across the economy as a whole. In this sense, 2023 was less a return to the old normal than a year of reconnection.

In 2024, Hong Kong continued to grow, but the composition of growth changed. Officially, real GDP expanded by 2.5%. Goods exports resumed growth, services exports continued to increase, and overall investment expenditure rose further. However, private consumption expenditure edged down, which suggests that domestic demand was weaker than the headline GDP figure implied. At the same time, residential property prices fell by 7.1% for the full year. Visitor arrivals reached nearly 45 million, up 31% year on year, but the rebound in tourism did not translate automatically into a broad recovery in consumption and property markets. In other words, 2024 was a year of externally supported growth with a still fragile local demand base.

By 2025, the recovery had become broader and more credible. Real GDP grew by 3.5%. Service-sector business receipts showed especially strong momentum: financing excluding banking, insurance, import/export trade, and banking all posted double-digit growth, while computer and information technology services surged by 85.5% for the year. Visitor arrivals climbed further to 49.9 million, retail sales value rose by 1.0% for the year, and the stock market staged a major rebound. Even in this stronger year, however, the recovery was not simply consumption-led. It rested on stronger exports, more active capital markets, renewed tourism flows, and the rising weight of technology-related and financial services.

### II. Structural Changes Over the Past Five Years

The first major structural change is the renewed centrality of finance and high-value services. In 2025, the goods balance remained negative on a GDP basis, but the services surplus reached HK$168.9 billion and the combined goods-and-services balance remained positive. The current account surplus was HK$406.6 billion, equal to 12.2% of GDP. By the end of 2025, bank deposits had exceeded HK$19 trillion. Meanwhile, the SFC’s latest survey showed that Hong Kong’s asset and wealth management business had reached HK$35.1 trillion in assets under management at end-2024, and Hong Kong returned to first place globally in IPO fundraising in 2025 with roughly HK$286 billion raised. These figures point to a financial centre that regained traction after several uneven years.

The second structural change is the gap between the recovery in tourism volume and the recovery in visitor spending quality. Visitor arrivals rose from about 91,000 in 2021 to 600,000 in 2022, 34 million in 2023, nearly 45 million in 2024, and 49.9 million in 2025. Tourism’s contribution to GDP had already returned to 2.8% in 2024, and the sector employed about 159,700 people that year; hotel occupancy in 2025 averaged around 87%. Yet the government also stated in April 2026 that the average length of stay and per-capita spending of Mainland visitors had both declined in 2025. Hong Kong has therefore restored tourism volume, but not necessarily tourism value. The next stage will depend on whether the city can convert events, conventions, concerts, sports, and cultural attractions into higher-yield visitor spending rather than short stays with thin margins.

The third structural change concerns trade and logistics. In customs terms, merchandise exports and imports both grew strongly in 2025, but Hong Kong still recorded a visible trade deficit of HK$446.6 billion. At the same time, total port cargo throughput fell by 4.0% to 169.6 million tonnes, and container throughput declined by 5.1% to 12.99 million TEUs. By contrast, Hong Kong International Airport handled 4.9 million tonnes of cargo in 2024, accounting for about 45% of the value of Hong Kong’s external trade. Hong Kong’s trade role is therefore shifting away from sheer port volume and toward high-value air cargo, re-export intermediation, financial settlement, supply-chain coordination, and cross-border services. This is both an upgrade and an adjustment to stronger regional competition in traditional logistics.

The fourth structural change is the reduced centrality of property in both growth and public finance. Residential property prices fell by 7.1% in 2024 and stabilized only in 2025, when they rose by 3.3%, ending a three-year decline. The commercial property market, however, remained sluggish, and the government stated explicitly that land premium revenue stayed weak, with the 2025–26 revised estimate at only HK$17.5 billion. Fiscal reserves had fallen to HK$647.3 billion by the end of March 2025, though they were then expected to recover to HK$657.2 billion by the end of March 2026 and to rise above HK$700 billion over the medium term. The broader implication is that Hong Kong can no longer rely on land and property as the single fiscal and macroeconomic engine that they once were.

### III. Prospects for 2026–2030

Taken together, official and IMF forecasts point to a moderate-growth outlook. The government expects real growth of 2.5% to 3.5% in 2026 and about 3% annual growth in 2027–2030. The IMF, however, expects growth to converge toward roughly 2.5% over the medium term because of ageing and slower capital accumulation. The most plausible baseline, therefore, is growth of about 2.5% to 3.0% a year over the next five years. That pace would be slower than in Hong Kong’s strongest pre-2019 periods, but it remains reasonable for a mature, service-dominated, externally exposed economy in structural transition.

The first major growth pillar over the next five years will remain international finance, though in a more diversified form. With bank deposits above HK$19 trillion, substantial inflows into Hong Kong-domiciled funds, the continuation of Wealth Management Connect, and the successful issuance of digital green bonds, Hong Kong is positioning itself not only as an equity market, but also as a centre for cross-border wealth management, offshore RMB business, green finance, and capital intermediation. Future growth is therefore likely to come less from traditional brokerage activity alone and more from the wider ecosystem of asset management, family offices, cross-border investment channels, bonds, structured products, and financial infrastructure.

The second pillar is innovation and industrial upgrading linked to the Northern Metropolis. The 2025–26 Budget earmarked HK$1 billion for the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute and expanded support for AI applications, microelectronics, and new industrialisation. The 2026–27 Budget reinforced this agenda by identifying the Northern Metropolis as a long-term engine of development, adding HK$30 billion in works expenditure over the coming two to three years and projecting capital works expenditure of about HK$128 billion in 2026–27. The government also stated that more than 100 strategic enterprises had been attracted to Hong Kong, bringing around HK$60 billion in investment and about 22,000 jobs. If these initiatives move beyond announcements into sustained R&D, production, hiring, and export activity, they could broaden Hong Kong’s growth base beyond finance and property.

The third pillar is tourism, events, and the wider experience economy. Hong Kong projects 53.8 million visitor arrivals in 2026, and the first quarter of 2026 had already recorded more than 14.31 million arrivals, up 17% year on year. In 2025, the city also welcomed 2.51 million MICE visitors, who generated about HK$10.7 billion in spending. Together with the opening of Kai Tak Sports Park and a larger calendar of cultural and sports events, this creates scope for further expansion in hotels, restaurants, retail, and transportation. The main test, however, is whether Hong Kong can improve visitor quality by lengthening stays, raising per-capita spending, strengthening convention demand, and linking events more effectively to local consumption, rather than simply increasing headcount.

These growth drivers face clear structural constraints. The Census and Statistics Department projects that the elderly population aged 65 and over will rise from 1.45 million in 2021 to 2.74 million in 2046, while the labour-force participation rate excluding foreign domestic helpers will decline from 55.2% in 2023 to 51.6% in 2046. The Labour and Welfare Bureau’s manpower projection further estimates a manpower shortage of 180,000 over the next five years. Meanwhile, the IMF has warned that Hong Kong’s traditional role as a logistics and trading centre remains under pressure from regional competition. Even if cyclical conditions improve, medium-term growth will remain capped unless Hong Kong raises productivity, attracts talent, and scales new industries successfully.

External risks also remain substantial. The 2026–27 Budget notes that global trade tensions have moderated, but it also states that protectionism, fragmentation, and policy uncertainty in major advanced economies will persist. The HKMA has likewise pointed to risks from tariffs and a weak property market. An upside scenario for 2026–2030 would combine steadier US easing, firmer Mainland growth, successful execution of the Northern Metropolis, and continued strength in capital markets. A downside scenario would involve renewed tariff escalation, weaker Mainland demand, and prolonged weakness in commercial property. On the official and IMF evidence, the baseline expectation is that Hong Kong will remain a resilient low- to medium-growth economy with strong external buffers and substantial high-value service capacity, but it is unlikely to return to boom conditions.

### Conclusion

Hong Kong’s economy over 2021–2025 passed through rebound, relapse, and broader recovery. The underlying change, however, was structural. Finance revived, tourism returned in volume, technology policy became more prominent, and property became a less reliable source of both growth and fiscal revenue. Over 2026–2030, Hong Kong’s performance will depend less on restoring the earlier formula than on combining finance, offshore RMB business, wealth management, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, Greater Bay Area linkages, and wider international connectivity within a more diversified model. If that transition succeeds, Hong Kong can remain steadily prosperous without very high growth. If it does not, the city may still retain deep capital, strong institutions, and international relevance, while facing a lower long-term growth rate.
