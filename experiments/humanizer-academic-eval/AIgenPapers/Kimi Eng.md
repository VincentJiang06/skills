### Economic Development of Hong Kong: Retrospective and Outlook (2020-2029)
### Abstract

This paper systematically analyzes the economic development trajectory of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region over the past five years (2020-2024) and provides an outlook for the next five years (2025-2029). Through in-depth multi-dimensional analysis of macroeconomic indicators, industrial structure transformation, foreign trade and investment, labor markets, and financial markets, the study finds that Hong Kong's economy experienced robust recovery during 2023-2025 after enduring dual shocks from the 2019 social unrest and the 2020-2022 COVID-19 pandemic. Despite external uncertainties including geopolitical tensions and rising global trade protectionism, Hong Kong demonstrates strong economic resilience through its institutional advantage of "One Country, Two Systems," its role as a "super connector" between Mainland China and global markets, and endogenous growth drivers such as the Northern Metropolis development and innovation and technology industries. Looking ahead, Hong Kong's economy is expected to maintain moderate annual growth of 2.5%-3.5% between 2025-2029, with key development directions including artificial intelligence, green finance, high-end professional services, and deep integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area.

**Keywords:** Hong Kong economy; Greater Bay Area; Northern Metropolis; IPO market; talent policy

### Chapter 1: Introduction

As a leading international financial center, trade hub, and shipping center, Hong Kong's economic development has long been a focus of academic and policy-making attention. The period from 2020 to 2029 holds special historical significance for Hong Kong—it represents both a challenging start to a new decade and a crucial window for repositioning and pursuing high-quality development under the support of China's 14th Five-Year Plan.

The past five years (2020-2024) saw Hong Kong's economy face unprecedented multiple challenges: the aftermath of the 2019 social unrest, the global COVID-19 pandemic from 2020-2022, and the subsequent supply chain disruptions and cross-border travel restrictions. According to World Bank data, Hong Kong's GDP growth recorded a negative growth of -6.54% in 2020

, rebounded strongly to 6.45% in 2021, but declined again to -3.68% in 2022 due to the fifth wave of the pandemic

. These dramatic fluctuations tested the resilience and recovery capacity of Hong Kong's economy.

Meanwhile, the HKSAR government actively introduced a series of measures to stabilize the economy and improve people's livelihood. In February 2024, the government announced the complete removal of property market "cooling measures" (including Buyer's Stamp Duty targeting non-permanent residents, New Residential Stamp Duty for second-time buyers, and Special Stamp Duty for short-term resale), unleashing pent-up housing demand

. In 2025, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange introduced a series of IPO market reforms, including optimizing price discovery mechanisms and lowering public float requirements for large listings, reshaping Hong Kong's competitiveness as Asia's preferred listing destination

.

Looking ahead to the next five years (2025-2029), Hong Kong faces a complex and evolving domestic and external environment. Externally, global geopolitical tensions persist, trade protectionism is on the rise, and particularly the evolution of US-China relations profoundly impacts Hong Kong as a highly externally-oriented economy. The ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) predicts that Hong Kong's economic growth will moderate to 2.1% in 2025 and 1.9% in 2026

. However, domestically, the HKSAR government is vigorously promoting Northern Metropolis development, the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop, and various talent attraction schemes, which are expected to inject new growth momentum into the economy.

This paper aims to provide a comprehensive review of Hong Kong's economic development over the past five years through systematic data analysis, an in-depth analysis of the current economic situation, and scientifically-based projections for the next five years based on existing policy frameworks and macro trends, providing reference for related research and policy formulation.

### Chapter 2: Retrospective of Economic Development (2020-2024)

#### 2.1 Macroeconomic Performance: From Recession to Recovery

Hong Kong's economy experienced a distinct V-shaped reversal over the past five years. According to World Bank data, Hong Kong's GDP growth rates from 2019 to 2024 were: -1.67% (2019), -6.54% (2020), 6.45% (2021), -3.68% (2022), 3.22% (2023), and 2.50% (2024)

. This trajectory reflects Hong Kong's economic sensitivity to external shocks and its strong self-healing capabilities.

**2020-2021: Pandemic Impact and Strong Rebound**

In 2020, the global outbreak of COVID-19 dealt a heavy blow to Hong Kong's economy, which is highly dependent on cross-border travel and trade. GDP contracted by 6.54%, marking the second-lowest level since records began in 1962 (only surpassed by the -5.9% during the 1998 Asian Financial Crisis). The unemployment rate reached 5.81% in 2020

, a 15-year high. Private consumption expenditure fell sharply due to social distancing measures and border controls, with visitor arrivals plummeting from 55.9 million in 2019 to approximately 3.57 million in 2020, a drop of over 93%.

However, in 2021, driven by a low base effect and global economic recovery, Hong Kong's GDP achieved a strong rebound of 6.45%. This growth was primarily led by goods exports, benefiting from robust global demand for medical supplies, electronic products, and home office equipment. Goods exports grew by 26.3% that year, while service exports gradually recovered as local COVID-19 conditions came under control.

**2022-2023: Repeated Pandemic Waves and Gradual Recovery**

In early 2022, Hong Kong was hit by the fifth wave of COVID-19 infections. The government implemented strict social distancing measures and dynamic zero-COVID policies, leading to a 4.0% GDP contraction in Q1. The full-year economy contracted by 3.68%, mainly dragged down by declining private consumption expenditure (-1.8%) and reduced fixed capital investment (-7.8%). The unemployment rate rose to 4.32% in 2022

.

2023 marked a turning point in Hong Kong's economic recovery. With the full resumption of cross-border travel with the Mainland (February 6) and the removal of overseas entry restrictions, economic activity quickly recovered. Full-year GDP grew by 3.22%, mainly benefiting from strong service export recovery (21.2% growth) and rebound in private consumption expenditure (7.4% growth). Visitor arrivals rebounded to 34 million in 2023, although only 60% of the pre-pandemic peak, representing a qualitative leap from the 600,000 in 2022.

**2024: Steady Growth and Structural Transformation**

In 2024, Hong Kong's economy continued its recovery momentum, with full-year GDP growth of 2.6%

. Despite slowing external demand due to geopolitical tensions and tighter monetary policies in major economies, domestic demand continued to improve. Private consumption expenditure grew by 1.2%, and fixed capital investment reversed its decline to grow by 1.8%. The labor market performed strongly, with the unemployment rate falling from 2.95% in 2023 to 2.94% in 2024

, approaching full employment levels.

In terms of total GDP, Hong Kong's nominal GDP recovered from USD 363.1 billion in 2019 to USD 406.9 billion in 2024, with per capita GDP rising from USD 48,359 to USD 54,075

, indicating that the economy has surpassed pre-pandemic levels.

#### 2.2 Industrial Structure Evolution: Service Sector Dominance and I&T Breakthrough

Hong Kong's economy is dominated by the service sector, accounting for over 93% of GDP. Over the past five years, industrial structure has shown the following evolution trends:

**Consolidation and Upgrading of Financial Services**

Financial services have always been a pillar industry of Hong Kong's economy, accounting for approximately 22% of GDP. Over the past five years, Hong Kong has achieved breakthroughs in multiple financial sectors:

- **Offshore RMB Business**: Hong Kong maintains its position as the world's largest offshore RMB business center. As of end-2024, RMB deposits in Hong Kong exceeded RMB 1 trillion, with average daily turnover in the RMB Real Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) system exceeding RMB 1.8 trillion.
    
- **Asset and Wealth Management**: According to SFC data, as of end-2023, Hong Kong's asset and wealth management business reached HKD 35.5 trillion, representing approximately 42% growth from 2019.
    
- **IPO Market Reshaping**: In 2024, the Hong Kong Stock Exchange implemented a series of major reforms, including optimizing the Chapter 18C (Specialist Technology Companies) listing regime and launching the TECH (Technology Enterprises Channel) service platform. In 2025, Hong Kong's IPO market experienced explosive growth, with Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. (CATL) raising USD 5.3 billion in the largest global IPO since 2023
    
    , and total IPO fundraising reaching USD 22 billion, reclaiming global leadership
    
    .
    

**Post-Pandemic Reconstruction of Tourism and Retail**

Tourism and retail were the hardest-hit industries during the pandemic. In 2019, tourism accounted for approximately 4.5% of Hong Kong's GDP, directly employing over 250,000 people. During the three years of the pandemic, total retail sales value fell from HKD 431.1 billion in 2019 to HKD 326.5 billion in 2020, a drop of 24.3%.

Following the full resumption of cross-border travel in 2023, tourism entered a rapid reconstruction phase. In 2024, visitor arrivals rebounded to 45 million, recovering to approximately 75% of 2019 levels. In Q1 2025, visitor arrivals further recovered to 85% of pre-pandemic levels, driving retail sales value up by 12.8% year-on-year.

**Accelerated Development of Innovation and Technology Industries**

The past five years represent a critical period for Hong Kong's I&T industry moving from "concept" to "implementation." The SAR government has positioned I&T as a new engine for economic diversification, investing substantial resources:

- **Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop**: On December 22, 2025, the Hong Kong Loop (Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park) officially opened, hailed as a "milestone moment for Hong Kong's I&T development"
    
    . Over 60 companies have already signed contracts to move in, focusing on artificial intelligence, biomedicine, and new materials.
    
- **Northern Metropolis Development**: 2025 marks the "breakthrough year" for Northern Metropolis development. Major projects such as the San Tin Technopole and the Hung Shui Kiu/Ha Tsuen New Development Area have accelerated. The San Tin Technopole is expected to contribute at least HKD 250 billion annually to Hong Kong's GDP upon full operation, providing over 300,000 jobs
    
    .
    
- **InnoHK Research Clusters**: Hong Kong has established multiple international innovation and technology research centers, focusing on health technology, artificial intelligence, and robotics. The 2025-26 Budget announced HKD 1 billion for establishing the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute
    
    .
    

#### 2.3 Foreign Trade and Investment: Resilience and Transformation

As a free port and separate customs territory, foreign trade is the lifeblood of Hong Kong's economy. Over the past five years, Hong Kong's trade model has undergone transformation from traditional re-exports to high-value-added trade services.

**Fluctuations and Adaptation in Goods Trade**

According to World Bank data, Hong Kong's goods exports fluctuated from USD 543.1 billion in 2019 to USD 631.1 billion in 2024, while goods imports rose from USD 558.4 billion to USD 633.1 billion

. The trade deficit narrowed from USD 15.3 billion in 2019 to USD 2 billion in 2024.

Notably, Hong Kong's trade model is undergoing structural transformation. As Mainland supply chains upgrade and direct export capabilities strengthen, Hong Kong's traditional re-export space has been squeezed. However, Hong Kong has consolidated its position as an international trade hub by developing high-value-added services such as supply chain management, trade finance, and quality inspection.

**Counter-Trend Growth in Service Trade**

Service trade is Hong Kong's most competitive sector. Between 2019 and 2024, Hong Kong's service exports maintained steady growth, particularly in financial services, professional services, and logistics. With the deepening of financial connectivity mechanisms between the Mainland and Hong Kong (Stock Connect, Bond Connect, Cross-boundary Wealth Management Connect), cross-border financial services have become an important growth point.

**Inflow of Foreign Direct Investment**

Hong Kong has long been one of the world's most attractive FDI destinations. According to World Bank data, Hong Kong's net FDI inflows grew from USD 58.3 billion in 2019 to USD 125.8 billion in 2024, an increase of over 116%

. This growth reflects international investors' confidence in Hong Kong's long-term development prospects and Hong Kong's unique value as a gateway to the Mainland market.

#### 2.4 Labor Market: From High Unemployment to Talent Competition

**V-Shaped Reversal in the Job Market**

Hong Kong's labor market experienced dramatic fluctuations over the past five years. The unemployment rate soared from 2.92% in 2019 to 5.81% in 2020

, a 15-year high. The service sector was hardest hit by the pandemic, with waves of layoffs in catering, retail, and tourism.

As the economy recovered, the labor market improved rapidly. The unemployment rate fell to 2.95% in 2023 and further to 2.99% in 2024

, approaching the natural rate of unemployment. The underemployment rate also fell from 3.4% in 2020 to 1.1% in 2024.

**Changes in Wages and Income**

Despite the labor market returning to full employment, wage growth remained relatively moderate. Between 2020-2024, Hong Kong's nominal wage index grew at an average annual rate of approximately 2.5%, slightly above the average inflation rate of 2.1%. Median monthly household income increased from HKD 29,500 in 2019 to HKD 30,500 in 2024, with limited real purchasing power improvement.

**Major Shift in Talent Policy**

Facing multiple challenges of population aging, local talent outflow, and industrial structure upgrading, the HKSAR government introduced a series of "talent recruitment" policies between 2022-2023:

- **Top Talent Pass Scheme (TTPS)**: Launched at the end of 2022, targeting high-income individuals (annual salary above HKD 2.5 million) and graduates of top 100 global universities. As of end-2025, the scheme received over 70,000 applications, with approximately 55,000 approvals, representing an approval rate of 88.2%
    
    .
    
- **Quality Migrant Admission Scheme (QMAS)**: Quota restrictions were removed and approval processes simplified. In 2025, 18,707 applications were received with 7,101 approvals, and the renewal approval rate reached an impressive 97.3%
    
    .
    
- **Admission Scheme for Mainland Talents and Professionals (ASMTP)**: Industry restrictions were relaxed and approvals accelerated. In 2025, 26,714 applications were approved, with a renewal approval rate exceeding 93%
    
    .
    
- **Talent List**: Updated in March 2025 to cover 60 professions, adding 9 new professions including accountants, patent professionals, and aircraft maintenance engineers
    
    .
    

These policies have achieved remarkable results. According to SAR government statistics, from end-2022 to end-2025, various talent schemes attracted over 230,000 talents to Hong Kong

, effectively alleviating professional talent shortages in specific industries.

#### 2.5 Property Market: From Bubble Adjustment to Steady Recovery

Hong Kong's property market experienced a major policy shift from "strictest-ever cooling measures" to "complete removal of cooling measures," along with deep price adjustments over the past five years.

**Price Adjustment and Market Clearing**

According to Rating and Valuation Department data, Hong Kong's private domestic price index reached its historical peak in August 2019, then entered a four-year downward cycle. By end-2024, prices had cumulatively fallen approximately 25% from the peak, representing the longest and deepest adjustment since 1998. In 2024, prices fell 7.1% year-on-year (8.4% inflation-adjusted)

.

This adjustment was mainly driven by:

- Aggressive Fed rate hikes, with Hong Kong interest rates following, increasing mortgage burdens;
    
- Local economy devastated by the pandemic, reducing purchasing power;
    
- Population outflow weakening rental demand;
    
- Increased supply, with 24,261 private residential units completed in 2024, up 75.1% year-on-year
    
    .
    

**Policy Shift and Market Recovery**

On February 28, 2024, Financial Secretary Paul Chan announced the complete removal of property market "cooling measures" implemented for over a decade, including:

- Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD, targeting non-permanent residents);
    
- New Residential Stamp Duty (NRSD, targeting multiple property owners);
    
- Special Stamp Duty (SSD, targeting short-term resale).
    

The market warmed rapidly after "cooling measures removal." In the first ten months of 2025, residential transaction volume reached 51,361 deals, up 20.3% year-on-year, a four-year high

. Prices bottomed out by mid-2025, rising approximately 3% for the full year. Mainland buyers' share rose from approximately 18% in 2024 to 22-24% in 2025, exceeding 60% in the luxury market

.

In 2025, the government further optimized property policies, including lowering the threshold for residential properties under the New Capital Investment Entrant Scheme (CIES) from HKD 50 million to HKD 30 million, and raising the maximum investment amount that can be counted toward the scheme from HKD 10 million to HKD 15 million

.

Despite market recovery, Hong Kong remains the world's most expensive city for housing. According to the Demographia International Housing Affordability Survey, in 2024, Hong Kong's median house price was 14.4 times median household income, ranking as the world's least affordable city for the 14th consecutive year, though significantly improved from 18.8 times in 2022

.

#### 2.6 Financial Markets and IPO Ecosystem Reshaping

Hong Kong's financial markets experienced a process from downturn to rebirth over the past five years. In the stock market, the Hang Seng Index fluctuated violently between 2020-2024, falling below 15,000 points in 2022, a 13-year low. However, the underlying market system continued to improve through reforms.

**Deepening of Listing System Reform**

The 2018 listing system reform allowed companies with weighted voting right (WVR) structures and pre-revenue biotech companies to list, injecting vitality into the Hong Kong market. Chapter 18C introduced in 2023 further relaxed listing thresholds for specialist technology companies. The 2025 reforms focused on optimizing price discovery mechanisms:

- Requiring at least 40% of IPO shares to be allocated to institutional bookbuilding tranches, ensuring price discovery is led by professional institutions
    
    ;
    
- Public subscription tranches could adopt "Clawback Mechanism A" (initial allocation 5%, clawback up to 35% based on demand) or "Clawback Mechanism B" (fixed allocation 10%-60%)
    
    ;
    
- Cornerstone investor subscriptions capped at 50%-55% to avoid excessive concentration
    
    ;
    
- Introduction of "free float" requirements to ensure sufficient liquidity at listing
    
    .
    

**Explosive Growth of 2025 IPO Market**

Benefiting from system reforms and the wave of Mainland enterprises pursuing "A+H" dual listings, Hong Kong's IPO market performed brilliantly in 2025. Full-year fundraising reached USD 22 billion, up over 200% year-on-year, reclaiming the global IPO market championship

.

Major IPO projects included:

- Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. (CATL): USD 5.3 billion, the world's largest IPO since 2023
    
    ;
    
- Hengrui Pharmaceuticals: approximately USD 2 billion;
    
- Haitian Flavouring and Food: approximately USD 1.5 billion;
    
- Sanhua Intelligent Controls: approximately USD 1.2 billion.
    

These four large IPOs occupied four of the top ten global IPOs in 2025

.

**Expansion of Mutual Market Access Mechanisms**

In 2024, the Shanghai-Hong Kong and Shenzhen-Hong Kong Stock Connect mechanisms celebrated their tenth anniversary. In 2025, Southbound Trading was further expanded to allow eligible international enterprises listed in Hong Kong to be included as Southbound Trading targets. This provides a strong incentive for Hong Kong to attract quality international enterprises to list

.

### Chapter 3: Analysis of Current Economic Situation (2025)

#### 3.1 Macroeconomic Situation: Steady Expansion

In 2025, Hong Kong's economy continued its steady recovery. According to Census and Statistics Department data, real GDP grew by 3.1% year-on-year in Q1 and 3.1% in Q2, marking ten consecutive quarters of positive growth

. The full-year economic growth forecast remains at 2%-3%

.

**Growth Driver Analysis:**

- **External Demand**: Benefiting from strong global demand for AI-related electronic products and front-loading of trade activities, goods exports grew by 8.4% in real terms in Q1 2025
    
    . However, the second half faced downward pressure from US protectionist policies.
    
- **Domestic Demand**: Private consumption expenditure recorded a slight decline (-0.5%) in Q1, mainly affected by changing consumption patterns and the trend of Hong Kong residents traveling north to the Mainland for consumption. However, as employment income continued to improve (nominal income of employed persons grew 4.2% year-on-year in Q1 2025) and government consumption stimulus measures (such as "Happy Hong Kong" events) advanced, consumer confidence gradually recovered.
    
- **Investment Expenditure**: Overall investment expenditure returned to growth, with real growth of 4.1% in Q1, mainly driven by increased purchases of machinery, equipment, and intellectual property products. Large-scale infrastructure projects such as the San Tin Technopole and Northern Metropolis continued to advance.
    

#### 3.2 Inflation and Monetary Policy Environment

Hong Kong inflation remained moderate. The underlying Composite Consumer Price Index (excluding one-off government relief measures) rose 1.2% year-on-year in Q1 2025 and 1.1% in Q2

. The full-year underlying inflation forecast remains at 1.5%, with headline inflation at 1.8%

.

Regarding monetary policy, Hong Kong maintains a linked exchange rate system with interest rates following the US Federal Reserve. In 2025, as the US began its rate-cutting cycle, the Fed Funds Rate retreated from highs, and Hong Kong's Prime Rate correspondingly declined, reducing interest burdens for businesses and residents and providing support for the property and stock markets.

#### 3.3 Labor Market: Full Employment and Structural Shortages

Hong Kong's labor market remained tight in 2025. The unemployment rate maintained at around 3%, approaching full employment. However, structural talent shortages became prominent, particularly in I&T, healthcare, aviation, and construction sectors for emerging industries and infrastructure projects.

The SAR government continued optimizing talent policies:

- Updated Talent List to 60 professions in March 2025
    
    ;
    
- Launched the Technical Professional Stream in June 2025, allowing non-degree technical professionals to work in Hong Kong across eight sectors including new industrialization technicians, nurses, and aircraft maintenance technicians
    
    ;
    
- Introduced enhanced measures for the Technology Talent Admission Scheme (TechTAS) in December 2025, streamlining application procedures and removing restrictions requiring engagement in R&D in 14 designated technology areas
    
    .
    

#### 3.4 Financial Market Prosperity and Challenges

2025 was a highlight year for Hong Kong's financial markets. In the stock market, the Hang Seng Index rose significantly driven by the IPO boom and Mainland policy tailwinds. The IPO market raised USD 22 billion for the full year, reclaiming global leadership

.

However, the market also faces challenges:

- **Geopolitical Risks**: US-China tensions put pressure on valuations of China concept stocks;
    
- **Liquidity Issues**: Despite active IPOs, overall market turnover remains below historical averages;
    
- **Talent Competition**: Competitors such as Singapore continue introducing preferential policies to attract financial talent.

### Chapter 4: Economic Outlook for the Next Five Years (2025-2029)

#### 4.1 Growth Forecasts and Scenario Analysis

Looking ahead to the next five years (2025-2029), Hong Kong's economy will advance under the interplay of multiple forces. Based on projections from various institutions and analysis of internal growth drivers, average annual real GDP growth is expected to be between 2.5%-3.5%.

**Baseline Scenario (Probability 60%): Moderate Recovery**

Assuming moderate global economic expansion, "competition without decoupling" in US-China relations, and Mainland economic growth of 4%-5%, Hong Kong is expected to achieve growth of 2.8%-3.2% annually. Main drivers include:

- Northern Metropolis construction entering its peak period, with infrastructure investment growing 5%-8% annually;
    
- I&T industries gradually becoming industrialized, contributing to GDP rising from the current approximately 1.5% to 3%;
    
- Deepening financial market connectivity, with asset and wealth management growing 6% annually;
    
- Tourism fully recovering to pre-pandemic levels (55.9 million visitor arrivals in 2019) and continuing to grow.
    

**Optimistic Scenario (Probability 25%): Accelerated Integration**

If US-China relations ease and integration between Hong Kong and the Mainland (particularly the Greater Bay Area) accelerates, Hong Kong could achieve rapid growth of 3.5%-4.5% annually. Triggers include:

- Full liberalization of factor flows in the Greater Bay Area (capital, personnel, data);
    
- Hong Kong becoming the preferred headquarters base for Mainland enterprises "going global";
    
- Large-scale capital inflows to Hong Kong due to safe-haven demand.
    

**Pessimistic Scenario (Probability 15%): External Shocks**

If the global economy falls into recession and geopolitical conflicts escalate, Hong Kong's growth could slow to 1%-2% annually, with individual years even seeing negative growth. Risk factors include:

- Significant escalation of US tariffs on China, affecting Hong Kong's entrepôt trade;
    
- Tightening of global financial conditions, impacting Hong Kong's property and stock markets;
    
- "Decoupling" by major economies weakening Hong Kong's intermediary role.
    

#### 4.2 Structural Growth Drivers

**4.2.1 Northern Metropolis and I&T Industries**

The Northern Metropolis (covering approximately 300 square kilometers, one-third of Hong Kong's total area) is the most important engine for Hong Kong's economic growth over the next five years.

**Key Milestones:**

- 2026: Hung Shui Kiu Industrial Estate commences operation; Tuen Ma Line Hung Shui Kiu Station construction begins
    
    ;
    
- 2027: All major development projects initiate land resumption procedures
    
    ;
    
- 2028: First batch of Northern Metropolis public housing residents move in;
    
- 2032: Completion of land formation for 40% of new development land and completion of 40% of additional housing units
    
    .
    

**Economic Contribution Forecast:** The San Tin Technopole alone is expected to contribute at least HKD 250 billion annually to GDP upon full operation

. Including the Lok Ma Chau Loop, Hung Shui Kiu logistics hub, and other projects, the Northern Metropolis as a whole could contribute 15%-20% of Hong Kong's GDP by around 2030, creating 500,000 jobs.

**Specific I&T Development Directions:**

- **Artificial Intelligence**: The Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute (HKD 1 billion funding) drives industry-academia-research collaboration;
    
- **Life and Health Technology**: Leveraging Chapter 18A listing regime and research strengths of HKU, CUHK, and HKUST to develop the biomedical industry;
    
- **New Energy Technology**: Aligning with national "dual carbon" goals to develop green finance and green technology;
    
- **Advanced Manufacturing**: Supporting intelligent upgrading of local production lines through the "Manufacturing+" scheme
    
    .
    

**4.2.2 Financial Services Upgrading**

Hong Kong's financial industry will transform from a traditional "fundraising center" to an "asset allocation center" and "risk management center":

- **Green Finance**: Hong Kong has become Asia's largest green bond issuance center. Green and sustainable bond issuance is expected to grow 20% annually over the next five years, exceeding USD 500 billion by 2029.
    
- **Virtual Assets**: Developing a virtual asset ecosystem under a prudent regulatory framework, including stablecoin issuance, virtual asset ETFs, etc.
    
- **Offshore RMB Business**: As RMB internationalization advances, Hong Kong's offshore RMB deposits are expected to grow from the current RMB 1 trillion to RMB 2 trillion by 2029, with RMB trade settlement ratio rising to 40%.
    
- **Insurance and Risk Management**: Utilizing "One Country, Two Systems" advantages to develop catastrophe bonds, cyber insurance, and other specialty risk businesses.
    

**4.2.3 High-End Professional Services and Trade**

Hong Kong will consolidate its position as an Asia-Pacific center for international legal and dispute resolution services, international shipping center, and international aviation hub:

- **Legal and Dispute Resolution Services**: With support from the 14th Five-Year Plan, developing international commercial arbitration and mediation services, attracting enterprises from Belt and Road countries to choose Hong Kong as their dispute resolution venue.
    
- **High-Value-Added Supply Chain Management**: Transforming from traditional entrepôt trade to supply chain management, quality inspection, brand management, and other services, developing high-value-added logistics.
    
- **Aviation Industry**: With the full operation of the Three-Runway System, Hong Kong International Airport's annual handling capacity will increase to 120 million passengers and 10 million tonnes of cargo, consolidating its position as the world's largest international cargo airport.
    

**4.2.4 Talent and Population Strategy**

Population aging is Hong Kong's long-term structural challenge. The proportion of population aged 65 and above will rise from approximately 20% in 2024 to approximately 25% by 2029. Talent policies over the next five years will feature:

- **Precision Recruitment**: Continuously updating the Talent List, focusing on introducing professionals in I&T, healthcare, education, and finance;
    
- **Local Training**: Strengthening STEM education, cooperating with Northern Metropolis enterprises to cultivate applied talent
    
    ;
    
- **Regional Collaboration**: Promoting mutual recognition of professional qualifications in the Greater Bay Area, allowing Hong Kong and Macao professionals to practice in GBA Mainland cities;
    
- **Retention Measures**: Optimizing renewal conditions, providing better housing, education, and healthcare support, aiming to maintain talent renewal rates above 85%.
    

#### 4.3 Risks and Challenges

**4.3.1 External Risks**

- **Geopolitics and Trade Protectionism**: Uncertainty in US tariff policy on China is the largest external risk. If the US significantly increases tariffs on China, Hong Kong as a trade intermediary will be first affected. Hong Kong needs to accelerate market diversification, expanding into ASEAN, Middle East, Central Asia, and other "Global South" markets.
    
- **Global Financial Conditions**: Uncertainty over the US monetary policy path may affect Hong Kong's interest rate environment and asset prices. If the US raises rates again due to inflation rebound, Hong Kong's property and stock markets could face adjustment pressure.
    
- **Mainland Economic Slowdown**: Slower Mainland economic growth will affect Hong Kong's service exports and capital inflows. Hong Kong needs to enhance its own competitiveness and reduce excessive dependence on Mainland economic cycles.
    

**4.3.2 Internal Challenges**

- **Industrial Structure Adjustment Pains**: Traditional retail, catering, and other industries face impact from changing consumption patterns (Hong Kong residents traveling north, online shopping proliferation), requiring time to adapt and transform.
    
- **Living Costs and Talent Attractiveness**: Despite price adjustments, Hong Kong remains one of the world's most expensive cities to live in. How to maintain market stability while enhancing attractiveness to young people is a long-term challenge.
    
- **Social Integration**: Integration between large numbers of newly arrived talents and local society takes time. Balancing talent introduction with protection of local employment is a policy challenge.
    

**4.3.3 Structural Risks**

- **Linked Exchange Rate System Pressure**: If US-China interest rate differentials remain high for extended periods, the linked exchange rate system may face speculative attacks. Although Hong Kong holds over USD 400 billion in foreign exchange reserves, extreme market conditions still require vigilance.
    
- **Industrial Concentration Risk**: Despite efforts to diversify the economy, the financial sector still accounts for over 20% of GDP. Excessive reliance on the financial sector makes the economy vulnerable to global financial market volatility.
    

#### 4.4 Policy Recommendations

Based on the above analysis, the following policy recommendations are proposed:

**4.4.1 Deepen Regional Integration and Expand Economic Hinterland**

- Accelerate the "Twin Cities, Three Circles" construction between the Northern Metropolis and Shenzhen's Qianhai, achieving infrastructure connectivity, industrial collaborative development, and free flow of factors;
    
- Secure more "early and pilot implementation" policies for the Lok Ma Chau Loop, such as facilitation of cross-border data flow and streamlined immigration for R&D personnel;
    
- Promote Greater Bay Area "one-hour living circle" construction to enhance convenience for residents' cross-border living.
    

**4.4.2 Optimize Industrial Structure and Cultivate New Growth Points**

- Position artificial intelligence as a strategic emerging industry, increasing R&D investment and opening application scenarios;
    
- Develop the low-altitude economy, improving regulatory frameworks for drone delivery, air taxis, and other new transportation modes
    
    ;
    
- Promote digital transformation of traditional professional services (legal, accounting, construction) to improve service efficiency.
    

**4.4.3 Improve Talent Policy and Enhance City Attractiveness**

- Optimize TTPS renewal conditions, including flexible employment forms such as entrepreneurship and self-employment in recognized categories;
    
- Increase supply of subsidized housing, providing "rent-to-buy" options for talents;
    
- Enhance international school education supply to address concerns about children's education for incoming talents;

### Chapter 5: Conclusion

Over the past five years (2020-2024), Hong Kong's economy has undergone severe trials but demonstrated strong resilience and self-healing capabilities. From the -6.54% negative growth caused by the pandemic in 2020 to the steady 2.6% growth in 2024, Hong Kong's economy has completed a V-shaped recovery, with both total and per capita GDP surpassing pre-pandemic levels. This recovery is attributed to the SAR government's decisive policy responses (such as comprehensive "cooling measures removal" and talent policies) and Hong Kong's deep foundation as an international financial center and trade hub.

Looking ahead to the next five years (2025-2029), Hong Kong's economic development prospects are cautiously optimistic. Despite an uncertain external environment, internal growth drivers are strong: Northern Metropolis construction will reshape Hong Kong's urban space and economic landscape; I&T industries will move from "concept" to "industry"; financial services will expand into new areas such as green finance and virtual assets; and talent policies will inject new vitality into the economy.

Between 2025-2029, Hong Kong's economy is expected to maintain moderate annual growth of 2.5%-3.5%, with further optimization of industrial structure and significantly enhanced GBA integration. However, Hong Kong must remain vigilant against geopolitical risks, industrial transformation pains, and population aging challenges. Through deepening reform, expanding openness, and improving livelihoods, Hong Kong can achieve high-quality development.

Hong Kong's unique value lies in "backed by the motherland, connected to the world." Under the "One Country, Two Systems" framework, Hong Kong is both an important window for the country's high-level opening to the outside world and the preferred gateway for international capital entering the Mainland market. Over the next five years, as long as Hong Kong can fully leverage this unique advantage, actively integrate into the national development blueprint, and maintain institutional advantages that connect with international standards, it will surely overcome challenges and create new brilliance.
