## Discussion

The results bear on the relationship between monetary policy and how inflation expectations form. As standard New Keynesian models predict, credible central-bank commitment to a stable price level goes with better-anchored expectations — among households and professional forecasters alike. The mechanism here is credibility, not the policy action itself: consistent communication is what does the anchoring.

The clearest pattern in the data is an asymmetry, and it is worth stating as a finding rather than a possibility. Expectations respond fast to signals of tightening: when the central bank moves against inflationary pressure, agents adjust quickly, which says they are watching for disinflationary intent. Signals of accommodation barely move long-run expectations. The likeliest reason is that agents doubt such measures will last and discount them accordingly. The practical implication is that easing and tightening are not symmetric problems — the bank faces a different task depending on which way it needs expectations to move.

The findings also speak to whether expectations are rational. The rational-expectations hypothesis remains the benchmark, but the evidence has long shown real expectations departing from it, and ours do too. What we see fits bounded rationality and adaptive learning more readily than full rationality: agents revise their beliefs gradually as new information arrives rather than absorbing everything at once. That reading sits with the broader literature on how heterogeneous expectations are across different groups of agents.

Two limitations qualify this. Causal identification is hard when policy is itself endogenous to economic conditions; we use several identification strategies to address it, but residual endogeneity cannot be ruled out entirely. And our expectation measures, widely used as they are, remain imperfect proxies — survey responses in particular can carry reporting bias and need not capture the beliefs that actually drive decisions.

Those caveats noted, the results point to one conclusion: managing expectations is not a passive byproduct of policy but an active, central object of it. The open question is the microfoundation — how expectations form at the level of the individual agent, and how that formation shifts across institutional and macroeconomic settings.
