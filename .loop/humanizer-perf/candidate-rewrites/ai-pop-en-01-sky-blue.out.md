## Why Is the Sky Blue?

It's one of the first questions many of us ask as children. The short answer: the sky is blue because of how sunlight scatters as it passes through Earth's atmosphere. What makes that worth a second look is how much physics is packed into it.

Sunlight looks white, but it's really all the colors of the rainbow mixed together, and each color is a different wavelength. Red and orange sit at the long-wavelength end; blue and violet are much shorter. When that light enters the atmosphere, it runs into the tiny gas molecules — mostly nitrogen and oxygen — that make up the air, and those collisions scatter it in every direction.

Here's the key part: shorter wavelengths scatter far more strongly than longer ones. The effect is called Rayleigh scattering, after the British physicist who first described it. Blue light, being short, gets thrown around the sky far more than red or yellow, so when you look up you see blue arriving from every direction at once, scattered across the whole dome overhead.

You might be wondering: if violet has an even shorter wavelength than blue, why isn't the sky violet? Two reasons. The Sun puts out less violet than blue to begin with, and our eyes are more sensitive to blue than to violet. So violet does scatter — we just don't register it the same way.

The same rule paints sunsets red. When the Sun sits low on the horizon, its light has to travel through much more atmosphere to reach you, and along that longer path most of the blue scatters away before it arrives, leaving the warm reds and oranges behind. Blue sky and red sunset are the same physics seen from two angles — short wavelengths winning the scattering race overhead at noon, and losing the long road to your eye at dusk. The color overhead is never really a fixed fact about the sky; it's a running tally of which wavelengths made it through the air between you and the Sun.
