# Carbon Pricing and Industrial Competitiveness: A Review

## Abstract

Carbon pricing is now a central instrument of climate policy, and what it does to the competitiveness of energy-intensive, trade-exposed industries remains contested among policymakers, firms, and researchers. This review synthesizes the literature on that relationship: the theoretical channels through which a carbon price reaches firm-level costs, investment, and trade; the empirical record across jurisdictions and sectors; and the responses built to blunt competitiveness effects — free allowance allocation, border carbon adjustments, and output-based rebating. The picture that holds up is narrower than the early alarm suggested. Aggregate competitiveness effects observed to date have been modest, the burden is concentrated rather than broad, and complementary policy design has done much of the work in keeping it so. That last point is also the warning: the modest record was produced under low prices and heavy protection, so it cannot be read as evidence that carbon pricing is competitiveness-neutral. Where the evidence thins — investment leakage, the long-run innovation response — is precisely where the stakes rise as prices climb, and the review closes on those gaps.

## 1. Introduction

Pricing carbon emissions has become one of the most widely adopted climate instruments. Putting an explicit cost on greenhouse-gas emissions internalizes the externality of fossil-fuel combustion, rewards abatement, and tilts investment toward lower-carbon technology. Over two decades, carbon taxes, emissions trading systems, and hybrids have spread across a growing list of jurisdictions and now cover a substantial, expanding share of global emissions.

That spread has carried one persistent worry with it: industrial competitiveness. The logic is simple. If a carbon price raises a firm's costs while its competitors abroad face none, the regulated firm can lose market share, cut output, or move production to where the price does not reach. This is the carbon-leakage concern, and it bears on both the welfare of the regulating jurisdiction and the environmental point of the policy — if production simply relocates, emissions are displaced, not avoided. The worry has weighed heavily in policy debate and has shaped how schemes are designed.

The effect is not one mechanism with one outcome. Its size turns on the carbon price, the energy intensity of production, the sector's trade exposure, the abatement options on hand, and the complementary policies wrapped around the price signal — and it reads differently at the plant, the sector, and the national economy. Understanding it therefore means holding mechanism, evidence, and policy design together rather than in turn; that is how this review proceeds, before returning to what the record does and does not yet license us to conclude.

## 2. Mechanisms Linking Carbon Pricing and Competitiveness

A carbon price is, at bottom, a higher price on one input: the right to emit. How much that raises a firm's costs depends on its carbon intensity and on how readily it can cut emissions in response. A firm with few short-run abatement options feels the price almost directly; one with cheap abatement absorbs it with little change to its cost base.

Whether those costs actually bite depends on a second channel — pass-through. Can the firm move the cost onto customers? That turns on demand elasticity and on how much competition comes from unregulated producers. Where products are homogeneous and international competition is fierce, pass-through is limited and the firm eats the cost as thinner margins. Where products are differentiated, or where rivals carry comparable carbon costs, more of it passes through. This is the crux of why competitiveness worries cluster in a narrow band of energy-intensive, trade-exposed industries rather than spreading across the economy.

A third channel works on a longer clock: investment. Beyond operating costs, a sustained carbon price can steer where new capital goes, and firms expecting it to persist may place plants in jurisdictions with weaker climate rules — investment leakage. This is harder to observe than a short-run output dip, because investment plays out over years and answers to far more than carbon cost: labor, market access, infrastructure, regulatory stability. Its weight in the theory is what makes policy credibility matter, since a price firms do not believe will last cannot redirect a decision that long.

Three factors, then, govern the effect: carbon intensity, trade exposure, and the room to abate or pass through costs. They interact rather than add, which is why effects are so uneven across sectors and firms — and why they are not separable from policy design, since free allocation and border adjustments work precisely by damping one channel or another. Whether these mechanisms actually produced large effects in practice is the empirical question.

## 3. Empirical Evidence

Two decades of carbon taxes and trading systems have generated a substantial empirical record — firm-level, sector-level, and macroeconomic. It is consistent on several points, and pointedly silent on others.

Start with the headline: aggregate competitiveness effects have been modest. The large losses of output, employment, and market share that early concern anticipated did not arrive. Two reasons account for most of this, and both matter for reading the result. Prices through much of the study period were low, so the cost increase was small to begin with; and the wide use of free allowance allocation shielded energy-intensive industries from much of what remained. A modest aggregate effect is not no effect — it is a smaller, more diffuse effect than was feared, under conditions chosen to keep it small.

The aggregate also conceals its own distribution. The burden is concentrated, not spread: cement, steel, aluminum, and basic chemicals carry far more exposure than the average, and within them the most carbon-intensive installations carry the most. Economy-wide or even sector-wide averages can wash this out entirely, which is why disaggregated analysis is not a refinement here but a requirement — the average firm is fine while specific facilities are not.

On leakage itself — emissions displaced to unregulated jurisdictions — the evidence is thin, and partly thin by construction, since isolating a carbon-price effect from every other driver of trade and investment is genuinely hard. What there is offers limited support for substantial leakage under existing schemes: several studies find little robust evidence, a few find modest sector-specific effects. The cleaner reading is that limited leakage is what low prices and heavy protection should produce — not proof that leakage stays negligible once prices rise or protection is withdrawn.

Innovation is the one forward-looking strand. A carbon price can pull investment into cleaner technologies and processes, and it has been linked to more patenting and adoption of low-carbon methods in some settings, though how large the effect is, and how much it depends on the price level, is still open. It may be the channel that matters most: the long-run competitiveness of these industries likely rests less on the short-run cost than on whether they can build lower-carbon production at all. That this is the least-developed part of the evidence is the problem — the question that matters most over time is the one we can answer least.

So the record reads: modest in aggregate, concentrated by sector, light on observed leakage, tentatively positive on innovation. Every one of those findings is conditioned on a low-price, high-protection period, and none of them transfers automatically to the higher, more widespread prices ambitious policy will require.

## 4. Discussion

The throughline of the evidence is that policy design, not the carbon price alone, produced the modest record — which makes the design choices worth examining directly, along with where they fall short. Three instruments carry the protection. Free allocation hands allowances to exposed firms at no cost, offsetting the burden while leaving the marginal incentive to abate in place; it has been the main shield for energy-intensive, trade-exposed industry. Output-based allocation ties that support to current production, which weakens the reason to move production abroad. Border carbon adjustments take the other route — a charge on the carbon embodied in imports — addressing competitiveness and leakage at the point where the gap originates.

None is free of cost. Free allocation protects competitiveness but can blunt the incentive to cut production-related emissions and can hand windfalls under some conditions. Output-based rebating keeps production incentives intact but adds administrative complexity and can slow exactly the structural change deep decarbonization demands. Border adjustments are conceptually clean and practically fraught: measuring embodied carbon, squaring the charge with trade law, and the risk of retaliation. Designing competitiveness provisions is therefore a balance — protecting exposed industry against preserving the environmental and efficiency point of the price itself.

Credibility runs underneath all three. Because investment looks forward, the expected path of the carbon price can matter as much as today's level: a credible rising price paired with predictable protection can move firms to invest in abatement rather than relocate. International coordination would help more directly — linked trading systems, or carbon prices converging across jurisdictions, shrink the very differentials that create leakage risk. Today's fragmented patchwork leaves most of that benefit on the table.

And the evidence is incomplete in ways that bear directly on the central worry. Most of it comes from a low-price era, so whether the modest effects survive prices consistent with ambitious targets is genuinely unknown. The evidence on investment leakage and the long-run innovation response is thinner than on short-run output, because long-horizon outcomes are hard to observe. And carbon pricing's interaction with the wider climate and industrial policy mix is not well understood, so its effect cannot always be cleanly separated from the instruments around it. Each gap counsels against extrapolating the comfortable past onto a more demanding future.

## 5. Conclusion

The competitiveness effects of carbon pricing run through cost, pass-through, and investment, gated by carbon intensity, trade exposure, and the room to abate or pass costs along. On the record so far, the aggregate effect is modest, the burden falls hard on a few sectors, observed leakage is limited, and design choices — free allocation, output-based rebating, border adjustments — shaped each of those outcomes.

The single thread tying the review together is that this reassurance is conditional. The modest record was bought with low prices and extensive protection; it is evidence about the conditions that produced it, not about carbon pricing as such, and it cannot be assumed to hold once prices rise toward what ambitious climate policy requires and protection is gradually withdrawn. The thinnest evidence sits exactly where the rising stakes concentrate — investment leakage, the long-run innovation response, and the entanglement of carbon pricing with the policies around it. Closing those gaps, on richer data and on the experience of maturing schemes, is what would let the past inform the higher-price future rather than merely flatter it.
