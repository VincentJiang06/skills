Some volcanic eruptions don't just throw ash into the sky — they throw lightning. When a volcano erupts, it blasts a towering plume of ash upward, and inside that plume something electrical happens. The ash particles are constantly rubbing against one another, and that friction strips charge from one and deposits it on another, the way any two surfaces grinding together can build up a static charge. Bit by bit the plume separates into regions of opposite charge. When the difference grows large enough, it lets go all at once, and the plume discharges as lightning. There is still plenty researchers are working out about exactly when and where it strikes — which is the real reason it's worth studying, not just watching.
