Cities face a worsening problem: their own built fabric traps heat, raising temperatures above the surrounding countryside. The cost is not abstract — it lands on public health, on energy bills as air-conditioning demand climbs, and on the broader goal of livable cities. Three levers are usually proposed to push back. Expanding green space is the most discussed; reflective "cool" roofs attack the problem at the surface that absorbs the most sun; and designing ventilation corridors into the urban form lets hot air escape rather than pool. None is free, and none works everywhere equally — what cools a low-rise district may do little in a dense core, and each carries its own installation and maintenance cost. The honest conclusion is that no single measure is sufficient: meaningful cooling will come from combining them, matched to the specific city. The source does not quantify how much each contributes, and that gap is exactly what the next round of evidence needs to close.
