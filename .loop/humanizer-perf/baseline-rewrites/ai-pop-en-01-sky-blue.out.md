## Why Is the Sky Blue?

The sky is blue because of how sunlight scatters as it passes through the atmosphere. That one sentence is the answer, but it hides a chain of small steps worth walking through.

Sunlight looks white, yet it is really a mix of every color, each with its own wavelength. Red and orange light have longer wavelengths; blue and violet have much shorter ones. When sunlight enters the air, it runs into the tiny gas molecules—mostly nitrogen and oxygen—that the atmosphere is made of, and those collisions send the light scattering in all directions.

The crucial detail is that shorter wavelengths scatter much more strongly than longer ones. Physicists call this Rayleigh scattering, after the British scientist who first described it. Blue light, with its short wavelength, gets thrown around far more than red or yellow. So when you look up, blue is what reaches your eye from every part of the sky at once.

You might ask: if violet has an even shorter wavelength than blue, why isn't the sky violet? Two reasons. The Sun puts out less violet than blue to begin with, and our eyes are simply more sensitive to blue. Violet does scatter—we just don't see the sky that way.

The same physics explains why sunsets turn red and orange. With the Sun low on the horizon, its light has to cross much more atmosphere to reach you. Over that longer path, the blue scatters away before it arrives, and the warm reds and oranges are what's left.

So a clear blue sky is really scattering at work overhead: sunlight bouncing off invisible molecules of air, with the short blue wavelengths winning the race to your eye.
