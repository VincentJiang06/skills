## Seven Things Worth Knowing About Octopuses

Octopuses are strange in ways that go well beyond their eight arms. A handful of their features look almost alien, and most of them turn out to be solutions to the particular problem of being a soft-bodied animal in the sea.

Start with the heart—or rather, the three of them. Two pump blood to the gills, and a third pushes it to the rest of the body. Oddly, that third heart stops beating when the octopus swims.

Their blood is blue, not red. Instead of iron-based hemoglobin, octopuses carry a copper-based molecule called hemocyanin, and copper gives the blood its bluish color.

They can also, in a sense, taste what they touch. Their suckers are lined with receptors, so an octopus exploring a crevice is sampling its chemistry directly, not just feeling its shape.

That sensitivity pairs with a knack for escape. Octopuses are known to squeeze through remarkably small gaps, unscrew jar lids, and slip out of aquarium tanks—usually at night. A soft, boneless body can fit through openings that look far too small for it.

They are quick-change artists, too. An octopus can shift its skin color and even its texture in a fraction of a second, blending into its surroundings—one of the most effective forms of camouflage in the animal kingdom.

By most measures they are genuinely intelligent. Octopuses solve puzzles, use tools, and can recognize individual people, which makes them among the smartest invertebrates studied.

Part of that may come from how their nervous system is arranged. Beyond the central brain, most of an octopus's neurons sit in its arms, so each arm can act with a striking degree of independence—less a single command center than a brain working alongside eight semi-autonomous limbs.
