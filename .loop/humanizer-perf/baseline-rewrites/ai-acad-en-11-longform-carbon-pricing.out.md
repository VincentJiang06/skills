# Carbon Pricing and Industrial Competitiveness: A Review

## Abstract

Carbon pricing is now a central instrument of climate policy, and its bearing on industrial competitiveness remains a live concern for policymakers, firms, and researchers. This review synthesizes the literature on how carbon pricing mechanisms affect the competitive position of energy-intensive, trade-exposed industries. It sets out the theoretical channels through which a price on carbon may alter firm-level costs, investment decisions, and trade flows, and it surveys the empirical evidence accumulated across jurisdictions and sectors. It then considers the policy responses developed to address competitiveness concerns, including free allowance allocation, border carbon adjustments, and output-based rebating. Three conclusions recur. The aggregate competitiveness effects observed to date have been modest; the effects are highly heterogeneous across sectors and firms; and the design of complementary policies does much to shape outcomes. The evidence base remains incomplete in several respects, and the review closes by identifying where further research is warranted.

## 1. Introduction

Pricing carbon emissions has become one of the most widely adopted tools in the response to climate change. By attaching an explicit cost to greenhouse gas emissions, carbon pricing aims to internalize the externality of fossil fuel combustion, to reward abatement, and to steer investment toward lower-carbon technologies. Over the past two decades a growing number of jurisdictions have adopted carbon taxes, emissions trading systems, or hybrids of the two, and these instruments now cover a substantial and expanding share of global emissions.

A persistent worry has shadowed this expansion: the effect on industrial competitiveness. The logic is straightforward. When a carbon price raises production costs for firms in one jurisdiction while their competitors elsewhere face no comparable cost, the regulated firms may lose market share, cut output, or relocate production to unregulated regions. This dynamic, usually labeled carbon leakage, matters both for the economic welfare of the regulating jurisdiction and for the environmental effectiveness of the policy itself, and it has weighed heavily in policy debates and in the design of pricing schemes.

The relationship between carbon pricing and competitiveness does not reduce to a single mechanism or a single outcome. The size of any effect depends on the price level, the energy intensity of production, the sector's trade exposure, the availability of abatement options, and the complementary policies that accompany the price signal. The effect also operates differently at the level of the individual plant, the sector, and the national economy. Understanding it therefore requires attention to mechanisms, empirical evidence, and policy design together.

The review proceeds in that order. Section 2 sets out the theoretical mechanisms; Section 3 surveys the empirical evidence; Section 4 discusses the policy instruments developed to address competitiveness concerns and the limits of current knowledge; and Section 5 concludes.

## 2. Mechanisms Linking Carbon Pricing and Competitiveness

At its most basic, a carbon price raises the cost of an input — the right to emit. How large the resulting cost increase is depends on the carbon intensity of production and on how readily firms can cut emissions in response to the price. Where short-run abatement options are scarce, the price feeds more directly into higher costs; where low-cost abatement is available, firms may absorb the price with little change to their cost structure.

A second channel runs through the pass-through of costs to product prices. How much of the carbon cost a firm can pass to customers depends on the structure of the market, including the elasticity of demand and the intensity of competition from unregulated producers. In sectors with homogeneous products and intense international competition, the room for pass-through is narrow, and regulated firms may absorb more of the cost as reduced margins. Where products are differentiated, or where competitors face comparable carbon costs, pass-through is easier. The distinction matters because it explains why competitiveness concerns concentrate in a relatively narrow set of energy-intensive, trade-exposed industries.

A third channel concerns investment and relocation. Beyond its effect on short-run operating costs, a carbon price can shape the long-run allocation of capital. Firms that expect sustained carbon costs may direct new investment toward jurisdictions with weaker climate policies — a pattern sometimes called investment leakage. This channel is harder to observe than short-run output effects, because investment decisions play out over long horizons and respond to many factors besides carbon costs: labor costs, market access, infrastructure, and regulatory stability. It nonetheless looms large in the theory, and it points to the importance of policy credibility and predictability.

Together, these mechanisms imply that the competitiveness effects of carbon pricing turn on three conditions: the carbon intensity of production, the trade exposure of the sector, and the scope for abatement and cost pass-through. These conditions interact in ways that vary widely across sectors and firms. They are also not independent of policy design, since instruments such as free allocation and border adjustments work precisely by blunting one or more of these channels. The next section turns to what the evidence says about how large these effects have actually been.

## 3. Empirical Evidence

A substantial body of research has examined the major emissions trading systems and carbon taxes of the past two decades, drawing on firm-level, sector-level, and macroeconomic data. The findings are reasonably consistent on several points, even as they leave important questions open.

The first recurring finding is that aggregate competitiveness effects have been modest. Across the jurisdictions and periods studied, carbon pricing has not produced the large losses of output, employment, or market share that early concerns anticipated. Two explanations stand out. The carbon prices prevailing through much of this period were relatively low, which limited the cost increases on regulated firms; and the extensive use of free allowance allocation shielded many energy-intensive industries from the full force of the price. A modest aggregate effect does not mean no effect — only that the effects have been smaller and more diffuse than initially feared.

A second finding concerns heterogeneity across sectors and firms. The reassuring aggregate picture conceals sharper exposure in certain energy-intensive, trade-exposed sectors — cement, steel, aluminum, and basic chemicals among them. Within these sectors, effects vary from firm to firm with carbon intensity, abatement opportunities, and position in international markets, and the most carbon-intensive installations appear to bear a disproportionate share of the cost burden. This is why disaggregated analysis matters: sector-level or economy-wide averages can hide concentrated effects on particular firms or facilities.

A third finding bears on carbon leakage — the displacement of emissions to unregulated jurisdictions. Detecting leakage has proven difficult, since it requires separating the effect of carbon pricing from the many other drivers of trade and investment. The evidence to date offers limited support for the view that substantial leakage has occurred under existing schemes; some studies find little robust evidence of it, while others identify modest effects confined to specific sectors. That limited leakage is consistent with the low prices and protective measures of the period, and it should not be read as assurance that leakage would stay negligible under substantially higher prices or without mitigating policies.

A fourth strand examines innovation. Beyond its effects on costs, a carbon price may spur investment in low-carbon technologies and processes. In some settings, carbon pricing has been associated with increased patenting and adoption of cleaner technologies, though the size of the effect and its sensitivity to the price level remain under investigation. This channel matters because the long-run competitiveness of regulated industries may rest less on the short-run cost burden than on their capacity to develop and deploy lower-carbon methods. The evidence here is thinner than that on short-run cost and output effects.

In sum, the literature points to modest aggregate effects, marked sectoral heterogeneity, limited observed leakage, and a tentative but promising innovation response. These findings are conditioned by the circumstances of the period studied, and their bearing on a future of higher and more widespread carbon prices cannot be assumed.

## 4. Discussion

The evidence above carries implications for policy design and also exposes important gaps in what is known. This section takes each in turn.

A central message is that policy design does much to shape the competitiveness effects of carbon pricing. The modest effects observed so far owe a good deal to the protective measures bundled with pricing schemes, of which three are prominent. Free allocation of emission allowances has been the main tool for shielding energy-intensive, trade-exposed industries, handing allowances to firms at no cost to offset the burden while leaving the marginal incentive to abate intact. Output-based allocation and rebating tie support to current production, which reduces the incentive to shift output abroad. Border carbon adjustments attempt to level the field by charging the carbon content of imports, addressing competitiveness and leakage at the source. Each involves trade-offs, and the choice among them has been much debated.

None of these instruments is free of difficulty. Free allocation protects competitiveness but can dull the incentive to cut production-related emissions and may generate windfall gains in some cases. Output-based rebating preserves production incentives but adds administrative complexity and may slow the structural change that deep decarbonization eventually requires. Border carbon adjustments are conceptually attractive but raise hard practical and legal problems — measuring embodied carbon, squaring the charge with international trade rules, and the risk of retaliation. Designing competitiveness provisions thus means balancing protection for exposed industries against the environmental integrity and economic efficiency of the carbon price.

Beyond the choice of instrument, the discussion points to the importance of credibility and coordination. Because investment is forward-looking, the expected path of carbon prices may matter as much as the price today. A credible commitment to a rising price, paired with predictable competitiveness provisions, can encourage firms to invest in abatement rather than relocate. International coordination — through linked trading systems or the gradual convergence of carbon prices — would narrow the differentials that give rise to leakage concerns in the first place. The fragmentation of current pricing efforts limits these gains.

The knowledge base, meanwhile, remains incomplete in several respects. Much of the evidence comes from a period of relatively low carbon prices, and whether the modest effects observed would persist as prices rise toward levels consistent with ambitious targets is uncertain. The evidence on investment leakage and on the long-run innovation response is thinner than that on short-run output effects, reflecting how hard long-horizon outcomes are to observe. And the interaction between carbon pricing and the wider set of climate and industrial policies is not well understood, so the effects of pricing cannot always be cleanly separated from those of complementary measures. These gaps counsel caution in extrapolating from past experience to future conditions.

## 5. Conclusion

This review has examined the relationship between carbon pricing and industrial competitiveness across three fronts: the theoretical mechanisms, the empirical evidence, and the policy responses developed to address competitiveness concerns. The competitiveness effects operate through cost, pass-through, and investment channels, and they are conditioned by the carbon intensity of production, the trade exposure of the sector, and the scope for abatement and pass-through. The evidence to date shows modest aggregate effects, substantial heterogeneity across sectors and firms, and limited observed leakage. And the design of complementary policies — free allocation, output-based rebating, and border carbon adjustments — does much to shape these outcomes.

These conclusions warrant caution. The modest effects observed so far reflect a context of low carbon prices and extensive protective measures, and they cannot be assumed to hold under the higher prices that ambitious climate policy will require. The evidence base, though substantial, leaves important questions open — particularly on investment leakage, the long-run innovation response, and the interaction of carbon pricing with complementary policies. Further research on these questions, drawing on richer data and on the experience of maturing schemes, is where the most useful progress lies. As carbon pricing continues to spread, an empirically grounded understanding of its competitiveness implications will remain essential.
