## Discussion

The results in the preceding section offer several insights into how monetary policy shapes the formation of inflation expectations. Consistent with standard New Keynesian models, we find that credible central-bank commitments to a stable price level are associated with better-anchored expectations among both households and professional forecasters. Credibility, in other words, is central to how policy transmits, which puts a premium on clear and consistent communication.

The most notable pattern in our data is an asymmetry in how expectations respond to policy announcements. When the central bank signals tightening in response to inflationary pressure, expectations adjust relatively quickly, which suggests that agents are attentive to signals of disinflationary intent. Signals of accommodation, by contrast, have a more muted effect on long-run expectations — perhaps because agents discount the durability of such measures. The practical implication is that the central bank may face different challenges depending on which direction it is trying to move expectations.

Our findings also speak to the debate over the rationality of expectations. The rational expectations hypothesis has long served as a benchmark in macroeconomic modeling, yet a substantial body of evidence indicates that real-world expectations frequently deviate from full rationality. What we observe fits models of bounded rationality and adaptive learning, in which agents update their beliefs gradually as new information arrives rather than incorporating all available data at once. This reading aligns with a growing literature that emphasizes how heterogeneous expectations are across different groups of agents.

The analysis has several limitations. Identifying causal effects in this setting is inherently difficult, because policy decisions are themselves endogenous to economic conditions; although we employ a range of identification strategies, residual endogeneity cannot be entirely ruled out. Our measures of inflation expectations, though widely used, are also imperfect proxies for the underlying beliefs of agents — survey-based measures in particular may carry reporting biases and may miss the expectations that actually drive decisions.

These caveats aside, our results contribute to a richer understanding of the expectations channel of monetary policy. The evidence suggests that managing expectations is not a passive byproduct of policy actions but an active and central objective of effective central banking. Future research should examine the microfoundations of expectation formation in more detail, and how these dynamics vary across institutional and macroeconomic environments.
