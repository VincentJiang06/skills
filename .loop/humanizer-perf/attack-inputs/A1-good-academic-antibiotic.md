We estimate the private and social returns to a delinked "subscription" payment for novel antibiotics, using the UK NHS pilot as our identifying case. The pilot guarantees two manufacturers a fixed annual sum (up to £10 million each) regardless of prescription volume, severing revenue from unit sales. Under our preferred specification, the implied social rate of return exceeds the private rate by a factor of roughly four, driven almost entirely by the option value of preserving last-line agents for future resistant infections. This wedge is sensitive to the assumed rate of resistance accrual: when we raise the annual resistance hazard from 2% to 5%, the social-private gap narrows, because faster resistance shortens the horizon over which stewardship pays off. We do not find support for the stronger claim, common in the policy literature, that delinkage alone restores private incentives to socially optimal levels; it closes perhaps half the gap. The remainder would require either larger transfers or complementary stewardship mandates, and our data cannot adjudicate between those two channels.
