So where does the energy in your breakfast actually go? You eat toast, your body burns it, and somehow that becomes the energy to walk up the stairs — but the handoff is not as direct as "fuel goes in, motion comes out." Think of the citric acid cycle as a roundabout in the middle of your cells. Carbon fragments left over from breaking down food enter the roundabout, get passed from one chemical exit to the next, and at each turn the cell strips off high-energy electrons like a pickpocket lifting wallets. Those electrons are the real prize. They get marched off to a separate machine — the electron transport chain — where their energy is finally cashed in to make ATP, the molecule your muscles actually spend. And here is the part that surprises people: the cycle itself makes almost no ATP directly. Its job is not to pay you. Its job is to load the electron carriers that do. Round and round it goes, never used up, handing off its passengers and coming back empty for more.
