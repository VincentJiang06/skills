How do you survive losing almost all the water in your body? For you, it would be fatal within minutes. For a tardigrade — the half-millimeter "water bear" living in the moss on your roof — it is just a bad week. When its pond dries up, the animal pulls in its legs, curls into a barrel shape called a tun, and lets itself dry out almost completely. The trick, as far as we can tell, is a set of proteins that turn the cell's interior into a kind of glass: instead of forming sharp ice-like crystals that would shred delicate machinery, the proteins set into a smooth, stable solid that holds everything in place until water returns. Add a drop, and hours later the water bear unpacks itself and walks away as if nothing happened. We still do not fully understand every step — how the animal repairs the damage that does accumulate is an open question — but the headline is hard to argue with: there is a creature on your roof that can, for all practical purposes, pause being alive.
