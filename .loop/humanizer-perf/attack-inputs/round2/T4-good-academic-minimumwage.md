This paper examines whether the 2021 minimum-wage increase in Seattle reduced employment among low-wage workers. We exploit administrative payroll records covering 14,300 establishments and compare hours and headcount before and after the increase against a synthetic control built from neighbouring counties.

The estimated employment effect is small and statistically insignificant: a 0.4% decline in headcount (95% CI: −1.9% to +1.1%), with no detectable change in average weekly hours. The point estimate is, if anything, slightly negative, but we cannot reject zero. Three margins might in principle absorb the cost increase — headcount, hours, and non-wage compensation — and our data speak only to the first two; the third remains an open question we flag rather than resolve.

We read these results as consistent with a labour market in which modest wage floors are absorbed largely through channels other than separations, at least over the one-year horizon we observe. This does not license the stronger claim that minimum-wage increases never reduce employment; our estimates are local to a tight labour market and a moderate increase, and they may not travel to looser markets or larger hikes.
