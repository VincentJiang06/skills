Your brain has no lymph vessels of the kind that drain the rest of your body, so for a long time nobody could say where its metabolic garbage went. The answer, it turns out, is that the brain mostly takes itself out at night. During deep sleep, the channels of fluid that wrap around blood vessels widen, and cerebrospinal fluid washes through brain tissue in slow pulses, flushing out waste — including the amyloid-beta protein that clumps into plaques in Alzheimer's disease. Researchers watched this happen in mice by injecting a tracer dye and filming where it went; clearance roughly doubled once the animals fell asleep. Whether the same machinery runs at the same pace in humans is still being worked out, and the field argues about how much of the flow is real bulk movement versus ordinary diffusion. But the basic picture is suggestive: one of the things sleep may be for is housekeeping the waking brain cannot afford to do while it is busy thinking.
