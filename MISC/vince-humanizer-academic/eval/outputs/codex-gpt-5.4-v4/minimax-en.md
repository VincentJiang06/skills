# Hong Kong's Economic Development: An Analysis of the 2020-2024 Period and Outlook for 2025-2029

## Abstract
Hong Kong's economy moved through a period of disruption and adjustment from 2020 to 2024. Two developments defined these years: the COVID-19 pandemic and the political changes that followed the implementation of the National Security Law. Real GDP contracted by about 4.8 percent in 2020, the steepest decline in decades. Growth then rebounded to 6.6 percent in 2021, before the economy contracted again by 3.68 percent in 2022. Recovery resumed in 2023 and 2024, with GDP growth of 3.28 percent and 3.2 percent respectively. The unemployment rate rose to 7.2 percent in the December 2020 to February 2021 period, then fell back to about 3.2 percent by 2024.  

The outlook for 2025-2029 depends on several interrelated factors: integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area, major infrastructure projects such as the Northern Metropolis, growth in innovation and technology, and Hong Kong's changing role as an international financial center. The government projects growth of 2 to 3 percent for 2025, while medium-term estimates point to average annual growth of about 3 percent through 2030.

## 1. Introduction

Hong Kong has long occupied a distinctive place in the international economy. It has functioned as an intermediary between Mainland China and global markets while operating under the institutional framework of "one country, two systems." The years from 2020 to 2024 were among the most difficult in its recent economic history. They tested the resilience of established institutions and required rapid adjustment across major sectors.

Several forces converged during this period. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted travel, trade, and consumption on a global scale. Sectors dependent on cross-border mobility were hit particularly hard. At the same time, the National Security Law, implemented in June 2020, altered Hong Kong's political environment and shaped perceptions of the business climate and investor risk. External pressure also increased as U.S.-China trade tensions continued.

This paper analyses Hong Kong's economic performance and structural changes during 2020-2024, focusing on major indicators and sectoral developments. It then considers the outlook for 2025-2029, including the main policy initiatives, opportunities, and risks likely to influence the economy over that period.

## 2. Analysis of the Past Five Years (2020-2024)

### 2.1 Macroeconomic Performance and GDP Growth

Hong Kong's macroeconomic performance from 2020 to 2024 was shaped first by the pandemic shock and then by an uneven recovery. In 2020, GDP contracted by about 4.8 percent, the sharpest annual decline in decades. Economic activity fell sharply after the outbreak of COVID-19 in early 2020. Border restrictions and social distancing measures reduced activity across tourism, retail, hospitality, and related services. Initial government forecasts projected a contraction of 4 to 7 percent, but estimates were later revised as the scale of the downturn became clearer. Some projections suggested a decline of up to 8 percent before the final outcome was recorded at about 4.8 percent.

In 2021, global vaccination campaigns and the gradual reopening of economic activity supported a rebound. Hong Kong's economy grew by 6.6 percent, the fastest rate since 2010. Domestic consumption improved and external demand strengthened. Even so, the recovery remained incomplete. Recurrent outbreaks of COVID-19 variants interrupted normalisation, and the emergence of Omicron in late 2021 led to renewed restrictions that weakened activity at the end of the year and added uncertainty to the 2022 outlook.

Conditions deteriorated again in 2022. Hong Kong experienced its most severe COVID-19 wave in early 2022, placing heavy pressure on the healthcare system and disrupting economic activity through lockdowns and containment measures. GDP fell by 3.68 percent. Retail sales volumes declined sharply, unemployment increased, and consumer confidence weakened. Much of the improvement recorded in 2021 was reversed.

A sustained recovery began only after restrictions were eased in late 2022 and early 2023. The reopening of the border with Mainland China in February 2023 was especially important. After nearly three years of closure, cross-border movement resumed and visitor arrivals rose markedly. Tourism and retail recovered, domestic consumption improved, and GDP grew by 3.28 percent in 2023.

Growth continued in 2024 at about 3.2 percent. Government assessments attributed this outcome mainly to the external sector, alongside a gradual recovery in domestic demand. Financial services and trade performed relatively well and offset weaker conditions in some domestic activities. Nominal GDP in 2024 reached about USD 407 billion, with GDP per capita at USD 54,009.

### 2.2 Labor Market Dynamics and Employment

Hong Kong's labour market changed sharply during 2020-2024. Before the pandemic, unemployment had been low by historical standards, at about 2.8 percent in early 2019. Conditions worsened during the social unrest of 2019 and then deteriorated more severely under the impact of COVID-19.

Unemployment began rising in early 2020. In the December 2019 to February 2020 period, the rate reached 3.7 percent, then the highest level in more than nine years. As successive pandemic waves disrupted activity, unemployment climbed further. By April-June 2020 it had reached 6.2 percent, a 15-year high. The peak came in the December 2020 to February 2021 period, when the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate rose to 7.2 percent, a 17-year high and above the level recorded during the 2003 SARS outbreak. Long-term unemployment also increased substantially.

The sectors most exposed to tourism and consumer spending faced the largest employment losses. Retail, accommodation, and food services recorded double-digit unemployment rates. Arts, entertainment, and recreation also contracted sharply as public gatherings were restricted and tourist arrivals collapsed. Many small and medium-sized enterprises faced severe revenue shortfalls while fixed costs remained, leading to layoffs and business closures.

Labour market recovery was gradual. As vaccination rates increased and restrictions were eased, conditions improved. By July-September 2021, unemployment had fallen to 4.5 percent. The fifth COVID-19 wave in early 2022 interrupted this improvement and pushed the rate back above 5 percent, but the broader downward trend resumed later in the year.

The reopening of the border with Mainland China in February 2023 supported a more sustained labour market recovery. Tourism, retail, and hospitality began hiring again, and the unemployment rate continued to fall. By late 2024 and into 2025, the seasonally adjusted rate had stabilised at about 3.2 percent. Youth unemployment, which had risen above 12 percent at the height of the crisis, fell to about 9.5 percent by 2024, still high relative to earlier years but well below peak levels.

### 2.3 Inflation Trends and Price Stability

Hong Kong experienced low inflation, and at times mild deflation, during 2020-2024. Consumer price data show that inflation remained subdued throughout most of the period and was lower than in many advanced economies.

In 2020, consumer prices declined by about 0.25 percent. Demand weakened sharply during the pandemic, and lower oil prices also reduced price pressure. The underlying inflation rate, which excludes the effects of one-off government relief measures, remained slightly positive at around 0.5 percent in September 2020 but was close to zero for much of the year. These patterns reflected weak domestic consumption, the collapse in tourism spending, and broader economic uncertainty.

Inflation returned gradually in 2021 as activity recovered, but it remained moderate at about 1.57 percent for the year. Some upward pressure came from the reopening of economies and stronger demand for goods, yet Hong Kong's linked exchange rate and competitive domestic conditions helped limit broader increases. Inflation rose slightly to 1.88 percent in 2022 as supply chain disruption and higher commodity prices passed through to local consumers.

In 2023 inflation was about 2.1 percent, then eased to 1.73 percent in 2024. These remained comparatively low levels. Hong Kong's openness, the competitive structure of its retail and services sectors, and subdued domestic demand helped contain price growth. The government's inflation-linked retail bonds, which offered returns tied to local CPI, were introduced partly to provide residents with an investment alternative during a period when concern about weak prices had become more relevant.

### 2.4 The Impact of COVID-19 and Recovery Trajectory

COVID-19 was the largest external economic shock to affect Hong Kong since SARS in 2003, but its duration and effects were considerably greater. The pandemic affected the economy through several reinforcing channels.

Tourism was hit first and most directly. Visitor arrivals in 2020 fell to 3.57 million, down 93.6 percent from roughly 56 million in 2019. As the virus spread globally in early 2020, Hong Kong introduced entry restrictions and quarantine requirements. These measures remained in place, with varying intensity, for nearly three years and removed tourism as a meaningful source of growth during that time.

Retail also contracted sharply. The sector had already weakened during the 2019 unrest, and pandemic conditions produced record declines. In the first quarter of 2020, retail sales volume fell by 36.9 percent year on year. For the full year, retail sales volumes declined by 25.5 percent, the largest annual drop since records began. In March 2020 alone, retail sales fell by 42 percent from a year earlier as stores closed and consumption shifted downward.

Hotels, hospitality, and related business services faced similar pressure. With tourist arrivals almost eliminated and local spending subdued, occupancy rates fell sharply. Many hotels reduced operations, placed workers on unpaid leave, or closed temporarily. Convention and exhibition activity also contracted as major events were cancelled or moved online.

Recovery did not follow a straight path. Some easing of social distancing in late 2021 and early 2022 supported activity, but the fifth wave of infections in early 2022 reversed much of that progress. A more decisive improvement came only after the border with Mainland China reopened in February 2023. Cross-border travel resumed, visitor flows increased, and sectors that had been constrained for three years began to recover.

The "Hello Hong Kong" campaign formed part of that recovery effort. It included 500,000 free airline tickets and more than one million visitor consumption vouchers. Together with the lifting of travel restrictions, these measures supported renewed tourism demand. Mainland visitor entries exceeded 100,000 per day on several occasions after reopening. Retail sales recovered strongly in 2023, when total sales exceeded HKD 406 billion and increased by 16.2 percent year on year. Hong Kong was also recorded as the world's highest per capita spender in luxury retail that year.

### 2.5 Political Developments and the National Security Law

The Law of the People's Republic of China on Safeguarding National Security in the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region came into effect on June 30, 2020. Its implementation marked a major political change with economic implications. The law was enacted by the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress and signed into effect by President Xi Jinping. It established a legal framework to prevent, suppress, and punish acts deemed to endanger national security, following the unrest of 2019.

The economic effects were mixed. In the short term, some international businesses raised concerns about possible changes to the business environment and to the civil liberties that had formed part of Hong Kong's appeal as an international financial centre. Some foreign governments issued travel advisories and reviewed their policies toward Hong Kong, which added uncertainty for overseas investors.

Later assessments suggested a more differentiated response. A survey by the Hong Kong General Chamber of Commerce found that 61 percent of respondents expected the law to have either a positive effect or no effect on their long-term operations. The restoration of social order after 2019 was commonly cited as a factor that improved operating conditions by reducing disruption. Many firms reported that day-to-day business activity was unaffected so long as operations remained lawful.

Financial Secretary Paul Chan argued that the law helped provide a stable environment for business and that international firms continued to regard Hong Kong as a financial hub. Subsequent political and electoral changes were presented by the government as measures that strengthened stability and supported investment conditions. Official data also showed that substantial numbers of overseas and mainland firms continued to maintain regional operations in Hong Kong.

These political changes also aligned Hong Kong more closely with Mainland China's broader development strategy. Throughout the following years, policy placed greater emphasis on using the framework of "one country, two systems" to integrate Hong Kong into national development plans.

### 2.6 Trade Relationships and Geopolitical Dynamics

Hong Kong's trade performance in 2020-2024 was shaped not only by the pandemic but also by broader geopolitical tensions, especially those between the United States and Mainland China. As a small and highly open economy embedded in regional and global supply chains, Hong Kong was exposed to shifts in the bilateral relationship between the two largest economies in the world.

For many years, the United States treated Hong Kong separately from Mainland China under legislation such as the Hong Kong Policy Act of 1992. After the introduction of the National Security Law and wider concerns about autonomy, the U.S. Congress passed the Hong Kong Autonomy Act in July 2020, allowing sanctions on individuals and entities considered to be undermining Hong Kong's autonomy. These changes added uncertainty to Hong Kong's international commercial relations.

Trade tensions affected Hong Kong through several channels. Goods passing through the territory faced greater scrutiny. Some technology exports were restricted. Measures affecting financial infrastructure also created uncertainty for firms using Hong Kong as a re-export and coordination hub. Businesses reassessed supply chains and, in some cases, sought to diversify risk.

Even so, export performance remained comparatively resilient. Merchandise exports recorded positive growth in several years, indicating that Hong Kong continued to play an important role in regional trade. Government data showed that the value of total merchandise exports increased by 6.5 percent year on year in the first two months of 2025. At the same time, the government sought to widen commercial links with emerging economies in the Global South as a response to geopolitical uncertainty in established markets.

### 2.7 Real Estate Market Dynamics

Hong Kong's real estate market was volatile during 2020-2024. Prices, transaction volumes, and investment sentiment were affected by the pandemic, broader economic conditions, and changing expectations. Residential and commercial property did not move in the same way because they were exposed to different forms of demand.

The residential market had already begun to cool after government measures introduced in 2019. Pandemic-related uncertainty reduced transaction activity further as buyers and sellers delayed decisions. Some price segments softened. Even so, the market remained relatively resilient in parts, particularly where affluent buyers viewed property as an alternative store of value.

Survey evidence from mid-2020 showed divided expectations. Some respondents anticipated further price declines because of unemployment and economic uncertainty. Others expected buying opportunities to emerge. By late 2020, about 39 percent of respondents expected residential prices to rise over the next six months, a more positive reading than in the previous quarter.

The luxury segment was especially resilient. Demand from mainland buyers seeking asset diversification and more attractive valuations supported transactions in this part of the market. The growth of IPO activity in Hong Kong and Shenzhen also contributed to demand from newly wealthy buyers. Property consultants reported that luxury transactions recovered in the second half of 2020 and into 2021, with sales values in some prime districts returning to elevated levels.

Commercial property, especially offices and retail space, faced more persistent weakness. The loss of tourists reduced demand for retail premises, while remote and hybrid working arrangements softened office demand in central districts. Vacancy rates increased in some office markets, and landlords offered concessions to retain tenants. Recovery in this segment remained slower through 2024, even as the wider economy improved.

A major longer-term influence on the property market is the Northern Metropolis. This planned development covers about 30,000 hectares in the northern part of Hong Kong and is intended to create new residential communities, commercial districts, and development land. Over time, it is expected to alter the territory's spatial distribution of housing and economic activity.

### 2.8 Financial Sector Performance

Hong Kong's financial sector remained relatively strong during 2020-2024 and continued to support the territory's role as an international financial centre. Its performance rested on several structural features, including a well-established regulatory system, liquid capital markets, the presence of major international institutions, and continued access to Mainland China's capital markets.

Financial markets were volatile, and the Hang Seng Index reflected the uncertainty affecting the region. Yet the IPO market remained active. In 2020, Hong Kong raised about USD 51.28 billion through IPOs, ranking second globally after Nasdaq and recording an increase of 26.5 percent from the previous year. A total of 154 firms listed that year, bringing the total number of listed companies to 2,538.

One important trend was the return of mainland Chinese technology firms to Hong Kong. Alibaba's secondary listing in 2019 had already signalled this shift, and the pattern became clearer over the following years as companies such as JD.com, JD Health International, and NetEase completed major offerings in Hong Kong. Regulatory change in Mainland China and uncertainty in U.S.-China relations made Hong Kong a more attractive listing venue for many Chinese firms.

Fintech also became a more visible part of the financial system. The government and regulators moved to establish a framework for virtual assets and digital finance. In October 2022, the government set out its policy position on developing a virtual asset sector, and the Securities and Futures Commission introduced a regulatory framework covering relevant activities.

The licensing regime for virtual asset service providers, introduced in June 2023, marked an important regulatory development. It shifted the system from voluntary participation to mandatory licensing and placed Hong Kong among the first jurisdictions in the region to establish a formal framework for digital asset activity. Virtual asset exchange-traded funds, including futures and spot products, were later launched in Hong Kong. By 2024, assets under management in these products had approached USD 300 million.

Hong Kong's financial role also continued to depend on cross-border channels linking Mainland China with international markets. Programs such as Stock Connect, Bond Connect, and the Greater Bay Area Wealth Management Connect expanded during this period, increasing integration while preserving the institutional distinctions associated with "one country, two systems."

### 2.9 Tourism Industry Recovery

No major sector was affected more directly by the pandemic than tourism. Visitor arrivals fell by more than 93 percent in 2020 as border closures and quarantine rules sharply limited leisure travel. The 3.57 million arrivals recorded in 2020, down from 56 million in 2019, amounted to a near collapse of an industry that had long contributed significantly to output and employment.

The decline in tourism had wide spillover effects. Hotels, restaurants, transport operators, retailers, and entertainment venues all faced sharp reductions in demand. Businesses that had expanded on the expectation of continued visitor growth found themselves with excess capacity. Employment in tourism-related sectors fell sharply, and unemployment reached levels not seen for many years.

When recovery began, it was driven mainly by the return of Mainland visitors. The border reopening in February 2023 led to an immediate rise in arrivals. Within weeks, daily entries from Mainland China exceeded 100,000 on several occasions for the first time since restrictions had been imposed. Government measures, including the "Hello Hong Kong" campaign, supported this return through free airline tickets, MTR tourist day passes, and consumption vouchers.

The recovery remained uneven across source markets. Mainland arrivals rebounded more quickly than arrivals from Western economies, reflecting different patterns of pandemic recovery and travel behaviour. Tourism demand also changed in composition. Policymakers increasingly emphasised higher-value tourism and new visitor experiences, rather than simply restoring the previous volume model.

### 2.10 Innovation and Technology Development

Hong Kong's innovation and technology sector continued to develop during 2020-2024 despite wider economic pressures. Government policy and closer links with Mainland China's technology system supported that process. Hong Kong's designation in the National 14th Five-Year Plan as an international innovation and technology centre gave this direction additional policy weight.

Investment in research and development infrastructure continued through institutions such as the Hong Kong Science Park and Cyberport. These played central roles in supporting startups and attracting established firms. The Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park in the Lok Ma Chau Loop became a key cross-border initiative. Covering 87 hectares, it is intended to host technology firms, research institutions, and related facilities.

Fintech and Web3 received particular policy attention. The government allocated HKD 50 million to accelerate the development of the Web3 ecosystem and established a task force to advise on its longer-term direction. At the same time, the Securities and Futures Commission developed a regulatory framework for virtual assets that gave firms a clearer operating environment.

Talent and education remained central concerns. Hong Kong's universities retained strong international rankings and research capacity. The government introduced measures to attract overseas talent, including streamlined visa arrangements and targeted incentives for professionals in priority sectors. New academic and training programs in artificial intelligence, biotechnology, and other emerging fields reflected the sectoral priorities of this period.

## 3. Future Outlook (2025-2029)

### 3.1 Macroeconomic Prospects and Growth Trajectory

The outlook for 2025-2029 is one of moderate growth rather than rapid expansion. Official forecasts point to GDP growth of 2 to 3 percent in 2025, with average annual growth of about 3 percent through 2030. This outlook is supported by Hong Kong's structural role in finance, trade, and cross-border intermediation, but it is limited by domestic and external constraints.

Some support for growth is likely to continue from the post-pandemic reopening. Consumer spending, tourism, and business activity still have room for further normalisation after the long period of restrictions. The external sector may also benefit if global trade conditions remain stable and demand persists for Hong Kong's services linking Mainland China and international markets.

Financial Secretary Paul Chan has framed this strategy in terms of making fuller use of "one country, two systems" while deepening links with national development and maintaining global market connectivity. Official language has described Hong Kong as both a "super connector" and a "super value-adder." In practical terms, this means preserving the institutions that support international finance and trade while aligning more closely with Mainland China's economic strategy.

Inflation is expected to remain low. Projections place underlying inflation at around 1.7 to 1.8 percent annually. The linked exchange rate system continues to anchor monetary conditions and expectations, though it also means that Hong Kong's domestic monetary environment is closely tied to U.S. policy settings.

The medium-term outlook remains exposed to risk. Geopolitical tensions could affect both trade and finance. Changes in global interest rates will influence credit conditions, investment, and asset prices. Domestic structural limits, especially population ageing and restricted land supply, may also reduce potential growth. Moderate expansion therefore remains the most plausible baseline.

### 3.2 Greater Bay Area Integration

The Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area is likely to be one of the main determinants of Hong Kong's economic direction over 2025-2029. The region includes Hong Kong, Macao, and nine cities in Guangdong Province, including Guangzhou and Shenzhen. It has been positioned in national policy as a major centre of growth, manufacturing, services, and innovation.

The Greater Bay Area's combined output is projected to exceed 15 trillion yuan, or about USD 2.15 trillion, in 2025. Deeper regional integration may allow Hong Kong to make fuller use of its comparative advantages in finance, professional services, and international market access while drawing on the manufacturing scale and innovation capacity of neighbouring mainland cities.

Transport and connectivity have improved substantially. Intercity rail has expanded into a network with more than 4,000 kilometres of track. Water transport routes have also increased. Projects such as the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge and the Guangzhou-Shenzhen-Hong Kong Express Rail Link have reduced travel times and improved the movement of people and goods.

Financial services stand to benefit further from this integration. Wealth Management Connect already provides channels through which eligible residents in Hong Kong and mainland Greater Bay Area cities can invest across jurisdictions. Hong Kong's role as the main offshore Renminbi centre remains important in this context, particularly for firms seeking financing and international expansion.

Government policy has focused on closer cooperation with Guangdong and Macao through rule harmonisation, market access arrangements, and improved flows of people, goods, capital, and data. The Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Science and Technology Innovation Co-operation Zone is a central part of this strategy, and the operational phase of the Hetao Hong Kong Park is expected to support cross-border technology activity.

### 3.3 Technology and Innovation Hub Development

Innovation and technology are central to Hong Kong's medium-term development strategy. The policy objective is to build a stronger innovation ecosystem that uses Hong Kong's research base, professional services, and international links while contributing to national strategic priorities.

Support from the National 14th Five-Year Plan gives this objective a clearer institutional basis. Public resources have been directed toward artificial intelligence, quantum computing, biotechnology, new energy, and related fields. The expectation is that these sectors can contribute to productivity growth and economic diversification.

Artificial intelligence has been identified as a technology with economy-wide applications. Recent policy statements have used the term "AI+" to describe the deployment of AI across finance, logistics, healthcare, and other sectors. Hong Kong's research base and its access to Mainland China's technology ecosystem may allow it to participate in both development and application, though this will depend on talent, regulation, and the speed of commercial adoption.

Web3 and virtual assets remain another area of policy interest. The regulatory foundation established during 2020-2024 is expected to be extended through consultations on stablecoin issuers, over-the-counter trading, and other segments of the digital asset market. The stated aim is to support sectoral development while retaining investor safeguards.

Physical space for this expansion is expected to come largely from the Northern Metropolis. The San Tin Technopole, including the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park, is intended to provide dedicated land for firms, laboratories, and institutions engaged in cross-border collaboration. Over time, this may support clustering effects that are difficult to achieve within Hong Kong's more constrained urban core.

### 3.4 Financial Center Competitiveness

Hong Kong's position as an international financial centre is likely to remain central to its economic model during 2025-2029. The territory retains several core advantages: a well-established regulatory system, liquid capital markets, proximity to Mainland China, and a large pool of financial and legal expertise.

Further opening of Mainland China's capital markets should continue to create opportunities for Hong Kong. Programs such as Stock Connect and Bond Connect have already deepened market links, and further enhancements could strengthen Hong Kong's intermediary role. Its position as the principal offshore Renminbi centre also remains important for trade settlement, financing, and investment.

The IPO market may continue to benefit from mainland firms seeking access to capital outside domestic exchanges and, in some cases, outside the United States. Hong Kong's listing rules, including arrangements for weighted voting rights and biotech firms, remain relevant to companies in technology and other growth sectors.

Green and sustainable finance is another area of likely expansion. Hong Kong has sought to position itself as a centre for green bond issuance and sustainable finance in the Asia-Pacific region. This includes development of certification standards, market infrastructure, and investment products. If global demand for sustainable investment continues to grow, this could add another channel of business.

At the same time, competition is intensifying. Singapore and other regional centres continue to compete for institutions, listings, and high-value financial activity. Geopolitical developments and regulatory changes in major economies may also influence Hong Kong's role in cross-border finance. Its competitiveness will depend on how effectively it adapts rules, infrastructure, and talent policy to changing conditions.

### 3.5 Infrastructure Development and Northern Metropolis

The Northern Metropolis is the largest infrastructure and urban development initiative currently planned in Hong Kong. Covering around 30,000 hectares, or roughly one-third of the territory's land area, it is intended to address longstanding limits in land and housing supply while creating a new economic growth zone in the north.

The project includes several major developments at different stages of preparation and implementation. Kwu Tung North and Fanling North are among the most advanced new development areas, with site formation and infrastructure works intended to support future residential and commercial use. The San Tin Technopole, which includes the Loop and the Hong Kong-Shenzhen Innovation and Technology Park, is expected to release additional land for research, commercial, and residential purposes.

Government projections indicate that about 60,000 housing units across roughly 10 new public housing estates will be completed and ready for intake over the next five years as part of this broader development strategy. The first tranche of land at the San Tin Technopole is also expected to be brought to market. These additions would expand land supply significantly and alter the geography of both population growth and business activity.

The Northern Metropolis is intended not only as a housing project but also as a source of long-term economic value. Government estimates suggest that it could eventually support 650,000 jobs, compared with about 110,000 jobs currently located in the relevant new town areas. If realised, this would substantially reshape the spatial structure of Hong Kong's economy.

Officials have also stressed the need to accelerate implementation. Historically, converting undeveloped land into sites ready for use in Hong Kong has often taken more than a decade. The current approach places greater emphasis on compressing that timeline while maintaining fiscal caution.

### 3.6 Regional Trade Opportunities

Regional trade diversification is likely to remain a strategic priority during 2025-2029. North America and Europe will continue to matter for Hong Kong's external sector, but official policy increasingly emphasises opportunities in the Global South.

Southeast Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Latin America are all seen as growth markets where demand for logistics, finance, legal services, and other forms of commercial intermediation may expand. Hong Kong also aims to benefit from the overseas expansion of mainland Chinese firms by serving as a platform for capital raising, advisory services, and regional coordination.

Trade agreements and partnership arrangements could help widen access to these markets. Although Hong Kong's constitutional position shapes how it participates in multilateral agreements, bilateral and regional arrangements may still improve trade access and reduce some barriers.

The Belt and Road Initiative continues to create opportunities for Hong Kong's services sector. Project finance, legal services, risk management, arbitration, and dispute resolution are areas in which the territory has existing capacity. These may remain relevant as infrastructure and investment projects develop across participating economies.

Digital trade is another area of potential growth. Improved physical and digital connectivity may support cross-border e-commerce and give small and medium-sized firms wider access to international markets. Hong Kong's logistics infrastructure and regulatory environment may allow it to compete effectively in this segment if supporting systems continue to develop.

### 3.7 Challenges and Risks

The medium-term outlook is constrained by several identifiable risks. These do not preclude growth, but they shape its likely pace and stability.

Geopolitical risk remains the most significant external constraint. U.S.-China tensions continue to affect the environment in which Hong Kong operates. Further changes in how Hong Kong is treated under U.S. law, additional sanctions, or tighter restrictions on technology and finance could affect market access and investor sentiment. Wider regional instability, including any escalation around Taiwan, would add further uncertainty.

Global monetary conditions also matter. Lower interest rates in major economies could support asset prices and business activity, but unexpected inflation or policy reversal could produce volatility. Because Hong Kong's exchange rate is linked to the U.S. dollar, domestic monetary conditions are heavily influenced by Federal Reserve decisions even when local cyclical conditions differ.

Domestic structural pressures are also important. Population ageing will weigh on labour supply and increase fiscal pressure over time. Public finances, strained by pandemic-era deficits, have improved but still require careful management. The operating account returned to surplus in fiscal year 2025-26, largely because of stronger tax revenue linked to economic recovery and active equity markets, but income from land premiums and related sources remains below earlier norms.

Talent competition has become more intense. Hong Kong continues to attract professionals through educational quality, international connectivity, and proximity to mainland opportunities, but high housing costs and wider regional competition weaken its position. Housing affordability and broader living costs will remain important factors in retaining skilled labour.

Environmental sustainability is another medium-term issue. High-density urban form creates environmental pressure, but it also creates opportunities in green buildings, low-carbon infrastructure, and sustainable urban systems. Meeting climate and sustainability goals will require adjustment across public policy and private investment.

## 4. Conclusion

Hong Kong's economic development during 2020-2024 was shaped by an extended period of disruption followed by partial recovery. The economy absorbed the effects of the pandemic, which produced the deepest recession in decades, while also adjusting to political change after the implementation of the National Security Law. Recovery strengthened after the border with Mainland China reopened in early 2023, restoring some of the economic linkages that had been suspended for nearly three years.

The record of 2020-2024 points to several conclusions. The pandemic caused severe but reversible damage, especially in tourism, retail, hospitality, and labour markets. At the same time, the institutional foundations of Hong Kong's economy remained in place. Finance, trade intermediation, and cross-border connectivity continued to anchor economic activity. Unemployment, after rising above 7 percent, returned to around 3.2 percent by 2024. The financial sector remained active, especially in IPOs and cross-border market activity, despite political and geopolitical uncertainty.

The outlook for 2025-2029 is moderate rather than expansive. Greater Bay Area integration may widen Hong Kong's economic base by connecting its strengths in finance and services to mainland manufacturing and innovation. The Northern Metropolis may ease land constraints and shift part of the city's economic centre of gravity northward. Innovation and technology, including artificial intelligence and digital assets, are likely to remain policy priorities.

These opportunities are accompanied by clear constraints. Geopolitical tensions will continue to shape trade and finance. Competition for talent and capital will remain strong. Domestic issues, including housing affordability, demographic ageing, and fiscal sustainability, will continue to affect long-term performance.

Hong Kong enters this period with substantial institutional capacity, strong financial infrastructure, and deep economic links with Mainland China and global markets. Its economic performance over the next five years will depend on whether it can use those strengths while adapting to a more constrained and politically complex external environment.
