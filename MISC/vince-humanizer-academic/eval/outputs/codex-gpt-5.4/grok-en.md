# Economic Development of Hong Kong: A Comprehensive Review of the Past Five Years (2021–2025) and Prospects for the Next Five Years (2026–2030)

## Abstract
Hong Kong, one of Asia’s major international financial centers and a Special Administrative Region of the People’s Republic of China, faced substantial global and domestic pressures over the past five years. This paper examines the territory’s economic performance from 2021 to 2025, a period shaped by post-COVID-19 recovery, geopolitical tensions, and faster integration with the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA). Real GDP growth averaged about 2.3% a year, and a 3.5% expansion in 2025 marked the third consecutive year of positive growth. Financial services, import/export trade, and tourism supported the recovery, while construction and real estate remained weak. Policy measures, including the National Security Law, Article 23 legislation, CEPA enhancements, and GBA initiatives, contributed to business stabilization and cross-border integration.

For 2026–2030, the economy is projected to record average real GDP growth of 3% a year, consistent with the Mainland’s 15th Five-Year Plan and supported by investment in innovation and technology (I&T), development of the Northern Metropolis, and continued external demand. This study draws on official data from the Census and Statistics Department (C&SD), the 2026-27 Budget Speech, Hong Kong Economic Reports, IMF assessments, and HKTDC research. It reviews macroeconomic indicators, sectoral trends, policy effects, structural constraints, future growth drivers, risks, and policy measures relevant to sustainable growth. The analysis emphasizes Hong Kong’s distinct advantages under “one country, two systems” as it responds to external uncertainty while deepening integration with the Mainland.

## 1. Introduction
Hong Kong’s economy has long been characterized by low taxation, an independent judiciary based on common law, a freely convertible currency pegged to the US dollar, and its role as an international financial, trading, shipping, and aviation hub. With a population of about 7.5 million and per capita GDP above US$56,000 in 2025, Hong Kong remained highly ranked in measures of economic freedom despite mounting external pressures.

The period from 2021 to 2025 followed the disruptions of the 2019 social unrest and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, which caused a sharp contraction of about 6.5% in real GDP in 2020. The subsequent recovery was uneven. It was shaped by supply chain disruption, US-China strategic competition, higher interest rates, changes in talent mobility, and persistent domestic structural problems, including high property prices and population aging. By 2025, however, the economy had recovered more firmly, with real GDP growth reaching 3.5%, the highest rate since 2021 and the third consecutive year of expansion. This outcome points to Hong Kong’s capacity to adjust under pressure, supported by policy stabilization and closer economic ties with the Mainland.

This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews macroeconomic performance and sectoral contributions for 2021–2025. Section 3 examines major policy interventions and structural constraints. Section 4 sets out forecasts and growth drivers for 2026–2030. Section 5 considers risks and presents policy recommendations. Section 6 concludes with the broader strategic implications. The analysis relies on C&SD national accounts, the 2026-27 Budget Speech released in February 2026, quarterly economic reports, and selected international assessments. The English version is approximately 5,000 words, with tables, sectoral detail, historical comparison, and expanded analysis.

## 2. Economic Performance in the Past Five Years (2021–2025)

### 2.1 Macroeconomic Overview

In 2021, the economy rebounded with the help of global vaccine rollout and initial border easing. In 2022, however, Omicron outbreaks and strict controls pushed the economy back into contraction. Growth resumed in 2023 after full border reopening and strengthened further in 2024 and 2025 as exports improved and domestic demand recovered. Nominal GDP increased from about US$369 billion in 2021 to US$427 billion in 2025. Unemployment fell from pandemic-era highs to 3.8% by early 2026, while underemployment remained low. Inflation stayed moderate, reflecting limited external price pressure and only gradual recovery in domestic demand.

External trade was the main source of growth. Merchandise exports rose sharply in 2025, with early data showing a 15.4% year-on-year increase, driven by electronics, AI-related components, and front-loading ahead of possible tariffs. Services exports, including finance, tourism, and professional services, also expanded after 2023, with visitor arrivals moving closer to pre-pandemic levels at about 33 million in the first eight months of 2025. Domestic demand improved in 2025 as well. Private consumption returned to positive growth at 2.5% overall, and gross fixed capital formation accelerated alongside infrastructure spending.

### 2.2 Sectoral Analysis
Services remained dominant, accounting for 93.5% of GDP on a stable 2023–2025 baseline. C&SD data and economic reports show clear differences across sectors in real year-on-year net output growth:

- **Financing and Insurance**: Growth remained strong at about 4.8% in 2025, including 4.9% in the fourth quarter. Hong Kong reinforced its position in wealth management and green finance, with more than 2,700 single-family offices, continued Stock Connect inflows, and further IPO and green bond activity. Cross-border financial integration with the GBA supported this performance.
- **Import/Export, Wholesale and Retail Trades**: At +6.0% in 2025, this was the leading sector. Growth reflected regional supply chain adjustments, synergies from RCEP and CEPA, and stronger GBA connectivity. Re-exports of high-value electronics were a major contributor.
- **Accommodation and Food Services (Tourism-related)**: The sector recovered strongly after border reopening, and visitor arrivals supported retail sales, which rose 11.8% in early 2026. Even so, value added fluctuated in some quarters, including declines of 0.5%, largely because of base effects.
- **Transportation, Storage, Postal and Courier**: Output grew 3.7% in 2025, supported by recovery in aviation and higher port throughput.
- **Information and Communications**: Growth was modest at 0.9%. Fintech, data centers, and digital infrastructure showed some momentum under the I&T policy push.
- **Real Estate, Professional and Business Services**: The sector contracted mildly, by about 0.2% to 0.7% in 2025, under pressure from high interest rates and a property market correction. Property prices fell about 25–30% from the 2021 peak before a partial recovery.
- **Construction**: Output fell substantially, by about 7.8% to 10.4% in mid-2025, reflecting project delays. At the same time, preparatory work for the Northern Metropolis and other major infrastructure projects began.
- **Manufacturing**: Although manufacturing accounted for only about 1% of GDP, it recorded positive growth of about 0.7% to 3.2%, reflecting high-value activities and closer integration with the GBA.

Overall, sectors linked to external demand performed better than domestically oriented sectors. Weakness in the secondary sector, especially construction, was offset by the strength of services, again highlighting Hong Kong’s dependence on trade and external flows.

### 2.3 Key Events, Policy Impacts, and International Comparisons
The 2020 National Security Law and the 2024 Article 23 legislation restored social stability and, in this account, improved business confidence despite initial capital outflows. Integration with the GBA accelerated under the 2019 Outline Development Plan. Infrastructure such as the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge and the Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Innovation and Technology Cooperation Zone facilitated movement of talent, capital, and technology. Upgrades under CEPA expanded mutual recognition in finance and professional services. The 14th Five-Year Plan (2021–2025) identified Hong Kong as an international financial center and an I&T center, with progress subsequently recorded in RMB internationalization and sustainable finance.

In comparative terms, Hong Kong’s average growth exceeded that of some advanced economies, including Japan at about 1% on average, but remained below that of faster-growing Asian economies such as Vietnam, at about 7–8%. Talent emigration, including net outflows of professionals from 2021 to 2023, was partly offset by inflows from the Mainland through talent schemes that issued more than 300,000 visas. Deleveraging in the property market weighed on investment, although early signs of stabilization appeared by late 2025.

Expanded analysis of longer-run trends suggests that productivity growth remained below the levels recorded during the boom years of the 2010s. Case studies of fintech development and green bond issuance illustrate areas of sectoral strength. Detailed econometric estimates indicate that the elasticity of external demand remained high, at roughly 1.5–2.0.

## 3. Challenges and Structural Issues
Despite the recovery, several structural problems remained in place: (1) geopolitical risk and de-risking pressures, including US export controls and tariff uncertainty; (2) demographic aging and talent shortages, with labor force participation stabilizing but emigration pressures persisting until inflows strengthened in 2024; (3) high living costs and inequality, including continued concern over the Gini coefficient; (4) property market volatility, with prices correcting by about 25–30% from 2021 to 2025 before a partial rebound in 2026; and (5) climate and sustainability pressures. Productivity growth also lagged behind that of regional peers, pointing to the need for stronger I&T capacity and human capital investment.

## 4. Prospects for the Next Five Years (2026–2030)

### 4.1 Official Forecasts and Medium-Term Outlook
According to the 2026-27 Budget Speech:

- **2026**: Real GDP growth is forecast at 2.5%–3.5%, supported by external trade and resilient domestic demand. Underlying inflation is projected at 1.7%, and headline inflation at 1.8%.
- **2027–2030**: Average annual real GDP growth is projected at 3%, with underlying inflation averaging 2%. Nominal GDP is projected to exceed HK$800 billion by 2030.

Growth is expected to remain export-led, while services should continue to benefit from Mainland tourism, financial activity, and demand for AI-related electronics.

### 4.2 Key Drivers and Opportunities
- **GBA and National Plans**: Alignment with China’s 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) is expected to deepen integration. The Northern Metropolis, covering 30,000 hectares or about one-third of Hong Kong’s land area, is intended to generate more than 500,000 jobs in I&T. Its core zones include Hetao for R&D and the San Tin Technopole for pilot production. The Hetao Zone is intended to support “one zone, two parks” collaboration.
- **Innovation and Technology**: OASES has attracted more than 100 strategic enterprises, with HK$60 billion in investment and 22,000 jobs. Tax incentives for family offices, funds, and R&D are directed toward AI, biotechnology, fintech, and green technology. Computing-power projects, including the Sandy Ridge data center, aim to expand capacity by 36 times by 2032.
- **Talent and Human Capital**: The Top Talent Pass Scheme and other immigration reforms have reversed some of the earlier outflow of skilled workers. Inflows from the Mainland have also supported population growth and domestic demand.
- **Sustainable Finance and Green Economy**: Hong Kong is expected to build further on its position in green finance in Asia, including Belt and Road financing and strengthened ESG-related activity.
- **Tourism and Consumption**: The policy target is to attract more than 50 million visitors a year through events and improved connectivity, with corresponding support for retail activity and consumption.
- **Infrastructure**: Major projects include the phased Lantau Tomorrow Vision, high-speed rail, and the Northern Metropolis.

Hong Kong’s role as a “super-connector,” based on common law, free port status, and the use of English in international business, remains relevant to opportunities in the Global South and to synergies under RCEP and CEPA.

### 4.3 Quantitative Projections and Scenario Analysis
Under the baseline scenario, average annual growth of 3% would raise nominal GDP to about HK$4.5–5 trillion by 2030. Under an optimistic scenario, stronger I&T development and deeper GBA integration could add about 0.5–1 percentage point to growth. Under a pessimistic scenario, heightened geopolitical tensions could limit growth to about 2%.

## 5. Risks, Challenges, and Policy Recommendations

**Risks**: The main risks include worsening geopolitical tensions, shifts in US policy, rising global protectionism, slower Mainland growth projected at about 4.5% on average, cyber and climate threats, and demographic headwinds. Without further reform, potential growth may remain capped at about 2.5%. Recovery in the property market could also weaken if interest rates stay high.

**Policy Recommendations**:  
1. Accelerate institutional convergence with the GBA, including mutual recognition of qualifications and cross-border data flows within security frameworks.  
2. Raise spending on education and R&D to 3% of GDP and expand I&T ecosystems in the Northern Metropolis.  
3. Diversify the economy by developing creative industries, the silver economy associated with population aging, and high-end manufacturing through re-industrialization.  
4. Strengthen ESG reporting and green bond issuance to attract sustainable capital.  
5. Maintain active talent policies, expand housing supply in line with the target of 196,000 public units, and improve overall livability.  
6. Continue digital investment in data centers, Web3, and AI within national security safeguards, while making use of the 2026-27 Budget’s HK$843 billion in expenditure, 60% of which is recurrent spending on health, education, and welfare.

The forecast fiscal surplus of HK$22.1 billion in 2026 provides room for targeted public investment.

## 6. Conclusion
The period from 2021 to 2025 marked Hong Kong’s transition from pandemic-related instability to a more stable, export-led recovery. That recovery rested on policy adjustment and closer integration with the Mainland. The period from 2026 to 2030 offers a significant opportunity to shift toward higher-quality growth through I&T, GBA integration, and major infrastructure investment. Whether this is achieved will depend on Hong Kong’s ability to remain globally open while aligning more closely with national development priorities, addressing long-standing structural weaknesses, and implementing the reforms set out in the 2026-27 Budget and the 15th Five-Year Plan. With effective policy execution, Hong Kong can maintain its position as a leading international financial center and a developing innovation hub while continuing to contribute to national development and wider regional economic activity.
