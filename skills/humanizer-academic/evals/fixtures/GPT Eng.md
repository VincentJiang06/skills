# Hong Kong’s Economic Development in the Past Five Years and Its Prospects for the Next Five Years (2021–2025; 2026–2030)

### Abstract

Viewed from April 2026, Hong Kong’s economic trajectory over 2021–2025 is best understood not as a simple “post-pandemic recovery,” but as a period of repeated shocks followed by structural reconfiguration. Real GDP grew by 6.4% in 2021, contracted by 3.5% in 2022, recovered by 3.2% in 2023, expanded by 2.5% in 2024, and accelerated to 3.5% in 2025. Over the same period, unemployment fell sharply from the pandemic peak but the recovery remained uneven, and the economy moved from border restrictions and tourism collapse to renewed growth in finance, services exports, inbound travel, and technology-related activities. At the same time, commercial real estate stayed weak, fiscal revenue from land remained subdued, and demographic headwinds became more visible. Hong Kong, therefore, did not simply “return” to its old model; it began to search for a new one.

For 2026–2030, the most realistic expectation is moderate rather than high growth. The HKSAR Government forecasts real GDP growth of 2.5% to 3.5% in 2026 and an average of about 3% a year for 2027–2030, while the IMF projects medium-term growth closer to 2.5% because of rapid population ageing and slower capital accumulation. The key question for the next five years is therefore not whether Hong Kong can recreate the property-led and retail-led boom of earlier decades, but whether it can consolidate a new model built on international finance, offshore RMB business, cross-border wealth management, high-end services, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, and deeper integration with the Greater Bay Area.

### Introduction

Using 2021–2025 as the “past five years” and 2026–2030 as the “next five years” produces a clear analytical frame. Within that frame, Hong Kong’s recent economic story has three distinct phases. First came the 2021 rebound, driven largely by exports and policy support rather than by a full revival of local services. Second came the 2022 setback, when a major local COVID outbreak and tighter global financial conditions pushed the economy back into contraction. Third came the 2023–2025 reopening period, in which tourism, cross-border mobility, external trade, and capital-market sentiment progressively improved. This sequencing matters because it shows that the better data in 2025 should not be mistaken for a full restoration of the old growth regime; instead, they signal a transition toward a new and still incomplete economic structure.

### I. A Stage-by-Stage Review of 2021–2025

In 2021, Hong Kong recorded a strong cyclical rebound, but the recovery was narrow. Real GDP expanded by 6.4%, reversing the declines of the previous two years, yet the economy was still about 2% smaller than in 2018. Labour-market conditions improved from the worst pandemic levels, but the annual unemployment rate still averaged 5.2%. Tourism was essentially absent: full-year visitor arrivals were only about 91,000. The implication is straightforward: 2021 was a rebound led by external demand and statistical base effects, not a normalised recovery of domestic demand, mobility, or the visitor economy.

The weakness of that recovery was exposed in 2022. Real GDP fell by 3.5%, private consumption declined by 1.1%, and gross domestic fixed capital formation dropped by 8.5%. Hong Kong was hit simultaneously by the fifth wave of COVID, continued mobility constraints, and the spillover effects of aggressive US monetary tightening under the Linked Exchange Rate System. Tourist arrivals recovered only marginally to about 600,000 for the year. In effect, 2022 demonstrated how exposed Hong Kong remained to disruptions in border flows, service-sector activity, and global financial conditions.

The turning point came in 2023, the first full year after reopening. Real GDP grew by 3.2%, and private consumption expenditure surged by 7.4%, indicating a powerful rebound in household activity and service demand. Full-year visitor arrivals recovered to 34 million, and tourism’s value added rose back to 2.6% of GDP. Yet the recovery was still uneven: goods exports remained soft in real terms, which meant that the fastest repair occurred in mobility, travel, and face-to-face services rather than across all sectors at once. 2023 was therefore less a return to old normality than a reconnection year.

In 2024, Hong Kong kept growing, but the composition of growth shifted. Officially, real GDP expanded by 2.5%. Goods exports resumed growth, services exports continued to increase, and overall investment expenditure rose further. However, private consumption expenditure edged down, suggesting that domestic demand remained softer than the headline GDP number implied. At the same time, residential property prices fell by 7.1% for the full year. Visitor arrivals reached close to 45 million, up 31% year on year, but the rebound in tourism did not automatically translate into a broad-based revival in consumption and property markets. In other words, 2024 was a year of externally supported growth with a still-fragile local demand base.

By 2025, the recovery had become broader and more convincing. Real GDP grew by 3.5%. Service-sector business receipts showed particularly strong momentum: financing excluding banking, insurance, import/export trade, and banking all posted double-digit growth, while the computer and information technology services domain surged by 85.5% for the year. Visitor arrivals climbed further to 49.9 million, retail sales value rose 1.0% for the year, and the stock market staged a major rebound. Yet even in this stronger year, the recovery was not simply consumption-led; it was built on a combination of stronger exports, more active capital markets, renewed tourism flows, and the rising importance of technology-related and financial services.

### II. Structural Changes Over the Past Five Years

The first major structural change is that finance and high-value services re-emerged as the core of Hong Kong’s resilience. In 2025, the goods balance remained negative on a GDP basis, but the services surplus reached HK$168.9 billion and the combined goods-and-services balance stayed positive. The current account surplus was HK$406.6 billion, equal to 12.2% of GDP. By the end of 2025, bank deposits had exceeded HK$19 trillion. Meanwhile, the SFC’s latest survey showed that Hong Kong’s asset and wealth management business had reached HK$35.1 trillion in assets under management at end-2024, and Hong Kong reclaimed first place globally in IPO fundraising in 2025 with roughly HK$286 billion raised. These are not signs of a financial centre in decline; they are signs of a centre regaining traction after several uneven years.

The second structural change is the divergence between tourism recovery and consumer-spending quality. Visitor arrivals rose from about 91,000 in 2021 to 600,000 in 2022, 34 million in 2023, nearly 45 million in 2024, and 49.9 million in 2025. Tourism’s contribution to GDP had already returned to 2.8% in 2024, and the sector employed about 159,700 people that year; hotel occupancy in 2025 averaged around 87%. Yet the government also stated in April 2026 that the average length of stay and per-capita spending of Mainland visitors had both declined in 2025. This means Hong Kong has already restored tourism volume, but not necessarily tourism value. The next stage will depend on whether the city can convert events, conventions, concerts, sports, and cultural attractions into higher-yield visitor spending rather than short-duration, low-margin visits.

The third structural change concerns trade and logistics. In customs terms, merchandise exports and imports both grew strongly in 2025, but Hong Kong still recorded a visible trade deficit of HK$446.6 billion. At the same time, total port cargo throughput fell by 4.0% to 169.6 million tonnes, and container throughput declined by 5.1% to 12.99 million TEUs. By contrast, Hong Kong International Airport handled 4.9 million tonnes of cargo in 2024, accounting for about 45% of the value of Hong Kong’s external trade. The inference is that Hong Kong’s trade role is shifting away from sheer port volume and toward high-value air cargo, re-export intermediation, financial settlement, supply-chain coordination, and cross-border services. That is both an upgrade and an adjustment to stronger regional competition in traditional logistics.

The fourth structural change is the declining centrality of property to both growth and public finance. Residential property prices fell by 7.1% in 2024 and only stabilised in 2025, when they rose by 3.3%, ending a three-year decline. The commercial property market, however, remained sluggish, and the government explicitly noted that land premium revenue stayed weak, with the 2025-26 revised estimate at only HK$17.5 billion. Fiscal reserves had fallen to HK$647.3 billion by the end of March 2025, though they were then expected to recover to HK$657.2 billion by the end of March 2026 and to rise above HK$700 billion over the medium term. The broader point is that Hong Kong can no longer rely on land and property as the singular fiscal and macroeconomic engine it once was.

### III. Prospects for 2026–2030

Taken together, official and IMF forecasts point to a moderate-growth future. The government expects 2.5% to 3.5% real growth in 2026 and about 3% annual growth in 2027–2030. The IMF, however, expects growth to converge toward roughly 2.5% over the medium term because of ageing and slower capital accumulation. The most plausible baseline, therefore, is that Hong Kong will expand at around 2.5% to 3.0% a year over the next five years. This would be slower than the city’s strongest pre-2019 periods, but still respectable for a mature, service-dominated, externally exposed economy undergoing structural transition.

The first major growth pillar for the next five years will remain international finance—but in a more diversified form. With bank deposits above HK$19 trillion, substantial inflows into Hong Kong-domiciled funds, the continuation of Wealth Management Connect, and the successful issuance of digital green bonds, Hong Kong is increasingly positioning itself not only as an equity market, but also as a cross-border wealth-management, offshore RMB, green-finance, and capital-intermediation hub. In that sense, future growth is likely to come less from traditional brokerage activity alone and more from the full ecosystem of asset management, family offices, cross-border investment channels, bonds, structured products, and financial infrastructure.

The second pillar is innovation and industrial upgrading linked to the Northern Metropolis. The 2025-26 Budget earmarked HK$1 billion for the Hong Kong AI Research and Development Institute and expanded support for AI applications, microelectronics, and new industrialisation. The 2026-27 Budget then reinforced this agenda by identifying the Northern Metropolis as a long-term engine of development, adding HK$30 billion in works expenditure over the coming two to three years and projecting capital works expenditure of about HK$128 billion in 2026-27. The government also stated that more than 100 strategic enterprises had been attracted to Hong Kong, bringing around HK$60 billion in investment and about 22,000 jobs. If these initiatives move beyond announcements into sustained R&D, production, hiring, and export activity, they could materially broaden Hong Kong’s growth base beyond finance and property.

The third pillar is tourism, events, and the broader experience economy. Hong Kong projects 53.8 million visitor arrivals in 2026, and the first quarter of 2026 already recorded more than 14.31 million arrivals, up 17% year on year. In 2025, the city also welcomed 2.51 million MICE visitors, who generated about HK$10.7 billion in spending. Together with the opening of Kai Tak Sports Park and a larger calendar of cultural and sports events, this creates room for further expansion in hotels, restaurants, retail, and transportation. But the true test is whether Hong Kong can raise visitor quality—longer stays, higher per-capita spending, stronger convention demand, and better linkage between events and local consumption—rather than merely increasing headcount.

Against these growth drivers stand clear structural constraints. The Census and Statistics Department projects that the elderly population aged 65 and over will rise from 1.45 million in 2021 to 2.74 million in 2046, while the labour-force participation rate excluding foreign domestic helpers will decline from 55.2% in 2023 to 51.6% in 2046. The Labour and Welfare Bureau’s manpower projection further estimates a manpower shortage of 180,000 over the next five years. Meanwhile, the IMF has warned that Hong Kong’s traditional role as a logistics and trading centre remains under pressure from regional competition. This means that even if cyclical conditions improve, medium-term growth will still be capped unless Hong Kong lifts productivity, attracts talent, and successfully scales new industries.

Finally, external risks remain substantial. The 2026-27 Budget notes that global trade tensions have moderated, but it also stresses that protectionism, fragmentation, and policy uncertainty in major advanced economies will persist. HKMA has likewise highlighted the risks from tariffs and a weak property market. An upside scenario for 2026–2030 would combine steadier US easing, firmer Mainland growth, successful execution of the Northern Metropolis, and continued strength in capital markets. A downside scenario would involve renewed tariff escalation, weaker Mainland demand, and prolonged weakness in commercial property. My baseline judgment, based on the official and IMF evidence, is that Hong Kong is likely to remain a resilient low- to medium-growth economy with strong external buffers and high-value service capacity, but not a return-to-boom economy.

### Conclusion

Hong Kong’s economy over 2021–2025 was shaped first by rebound, then by relapse, and finally by broader recovery. But the deeper story is structural: finance has revived, tourism has returned in volume, technology policy has gained prominence, while property has become a less reliable engine for both growth and fiscal revenue. Over 2026–2030, Hong Kong’s success will depend less on restoring the exact formula of the past and more on integrating finance, offshore RMB business, wealth management, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, Greater Bay Area linkages, and wider international connectivity into a more diversified model. If that integration succeeds, Hong Kong can remain steadily prosperous even without very high growth. If it fails, the city may continue to possess deep capital, strong institutions, and global relevance, yet still struggle with a lower trend growth rate.
