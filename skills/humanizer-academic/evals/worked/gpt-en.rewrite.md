### Abstract

Hong Kong's economy over 2021–2025 was not a straightforward post-pandemic
recovery. It was a sequence of shocks and structural reconfiguration: real GDP
grew 6.4% in 2021, fell 3.5% in 2022, then ran 3.2%, 2.5%, and 3.5% through
2023–2025. Unemployment dropped from its pandemic peak, but unevenly, as activity
shifted from collapsed tourism and border restrictions back toward finance,
services exports, inbound travel, and technology. Commercial real estate stayed
weak, land revenue stayed subdued, and demographic headwinds grew. The city did
not return to its old model; it began, incompletely, to look for a new one.

For 2026–2030, moderate growth is the realistic expectation. The HKSAR Government
forecasts 2.5%–3.5% in 2026 and about 3% a year for 2027–2030; the IMF puts the
medium term nearer 2.5%, citing rapid ageing and slower capital accumulation. The
real question is not whether Hong Kong can rebuild the old property- and
retail-led boom — it cannot — but whether it can consolidate a model built on
international finance, offshore RMB business, cross-border wealth management,
high-end services, innovation and technology, the Northern Metropolis, and deeper
Greater Bay Area integration.

### Introduction

Treating 2021–2025 as the past five years and 2026–2030 as the next gives a clean
frame. Within it, the recent story runs in three phases. The 2021 rebound was
driven by exports and policy support, not a revival of local services. The 2022
setback came when a major local COVID outbreak and tighter global financial
conditions pushed the economy back into contraction. The 2023–2025 reopening then
restored tourism, cross-border mobility, trade, and capital-market sentiment. The
sequence matters because it warns against reading the stronger 2025 data as a full
restoration of the old growth regime. They mark a transition toward a new and
still-incomplete structure.

### Structural Changes Over the Past Five Years

The clearest change is that finance and high-value services returned as the core
of Hong Kong's resilience. In 2025 the goods balance stayed negative on a GDP
basis, yet the services surplus reached HK$168.9 billion and the current-account
surplus HK$406.6 billion — 12.2% of GDP. Bank deposits passed HK$19 trillion by
year-end. Asset and wealth management stood at HK$35.1 trillion under management at
end-2024, and Hong Kong reclaimed the top global IPO market in 2025, raising
roughly HK$286 billion. These are the markers of a financial centre regaining
traction, not one in decline.

Tourism tells a different story: volume returned, value did not. Arrivals climbed
from about 91,000 in 2021 to 600,000, 34 million, nearly 45 million, and 49.9
million across 2022–2025; the sector's GDP contribution was back to 2.8% in 2024,
and 2025 hotel occupancy averaged around 87%. But the government also noted in
April 2026 that Mainland visitors' average stay and per-capita spending both fell
in 2025. The next stage turns on conversion — events, conventions, concerts,
sport, and culture into higher-yield visits rather than short, low-margin ones.

Property's centrality, finally, has faded for both growth and public finance.
Residential prices fell 7.1% in 2024 and only stabilised in 2025, rising 3.3% to
end a three-year decline, while commercial property stayed sluggish and land
premium revenue stayed weak — the 2025-26 revised estimate was just HK$17.5
billion. Fiscal reserves had fallen to HK$647.3 billion by end-March 2025. Land
and property can no longer be the single fiscal and macroeconomic engine they once
were.
