# Source reliability & freshness (Admiralty-lite)

Grade sources quickly so corroboration is *meaningful*, not just *count*. Adapted
from skill-principle `procedure.source_reliability_grading` (Admiralty code) —
kept light for speed.

## Reliability tiers (prefer higher; reach corroboration faster)

| Tier | Examples | Use |
|------|------|------|
| **A — primary / official** | standards bodies (IETF/IUPAC/NIST), official docs & release notes, the org's own page, peer-reviewed papers, primary data | Strongest. One A-source can settle a simple fact. |
| **B — reputable secondary** | established news orgs, major encyclopedias, well-known domain references (MDN, etc.) | Good corroboration; two independent B's clear the complex bar. |
| **C — community / aggregator** | forums, Q&A sites, wikis without primary cites, blogs | Use only as a lead; corroborate against A/B before relying on it. |

**Independent** = different origin. Two outlets reprinting one wire story, or two
pages citing the same upstream, count as **one** source for the bar.

## Freshness (volatile facts)

A fact that changes over time — software versions, prices, "current" leadership,
records, live statistics — needs a **recent, dated** source. Rules:

- Prefer the most recent authoritative source; note the **as-of date** in the
  answer ("As of <year>, …").
- A single **stale** source on a volatile fact → cap **Confidence at Medium** and
  add a caveat.
- Conflicting "current" values across dates → the most recent dated source wins;
  say so.

## When sources conflict

Weigh by tier first (A > B > C), then by recency for volatile facts, then by
independence (more independent corroboration wins). If two A-sources genuinely
disagree, present **both** and mark the answer **complex / Medium or Low** — do not
manufacture false certainty for speed.
